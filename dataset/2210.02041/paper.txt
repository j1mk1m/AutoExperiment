::: {.ltx_abstract .ltx_centering}
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

Unrestricted color attacks, which manipulate semantically meaningful
color of an image, have shown their stealthiness and success in fooling
both human eyes and deep neural networks. However, current works usually
sacrifice the flexibility of the uncontrolled setting to ensure the
naturalness of adversarial examples. As a result, the black-box attack
performance of these methods is limited. To boost transferability of
adversarial examples without damaging image quality, we propose a novel
Natural Color Fool (NCF) which is guided by realistic color
distributions sampled from a publicly available dataset and optimized by
our neighborhood search and initialization reset. By conducting
extensive experiments and visualizations, we convincingly demonstrate
the effectiveness of our proposed method. Notably, on average, results
show that our NCF can outperform state-of-the-art approaches by
[15.0%$\sim$`<!-- -->`{=html}32.9%]{#id1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}
for fooling normally trained models and
[10.0%$\sim$`<!-- -->`{=html}25.3%]{#id2.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}
for evading defense methods. Our code is available at
[https://github.com/ylhz/Natural-Color-Fool](https://github.com/ylhz/Natural-Color-Fool){.ltx_ref
.ltx_url .ltx_font_typewriter target="_blank"}.
:::

## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para}
Deep neural networks (DNNs) have achieved excellent performance on a
large number of tasks, such as image
recognition [alex2012imagenet](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}
;
[he2016deep](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}
, medical
diagnostics [macnamee2002the](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}
;
[peng2021self](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}
, and autonomous
driving [tian2018deeptest](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}
. However, with the rise of the field of adversarial
attack [szegedy2014intriguing](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref}
;
[goodfellow2015explaining](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}
;
[naseer2021on](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref}
;
[zhang2022bia](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref}
;
[chen2022understanding](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref}
;
[zhang2020dual](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref}
;
[zhang2020principal](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref}
, the robustness of state-of-the-art
DNNs [he2016deep](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}
;
[huang2017densely](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}
;
[Dosovitskiy2021an](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref}
;
[touvron2021going](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref}
;
[zhang2022progressive](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}
is put in question. Adversarial examples thus reveal the vulnerability
of DNNs and inevitably raise concerns about widely deployed models. To
avoid potential risks, it is crucial to expose as many of the model's
"blind spots" as possible at this stage.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para}
Generally, scenarios of adversarial attacks can divide into white-box
and black-box. For the white-box
scenario [dezfooli2016deepfool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref}
;
[carlini2017towards](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}
;
[zhao2020towards](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref}
;
[liu2022practical](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib20){.ltx_ref}
, all information about the target model is publicly available, such as
the architecture and parameters. Although the attacker can easily fool
them with human-imperceptible perturbations in this case, it is not
practical in the real world since deployed models are usually opaque to
unauthorized users. To overcome this limitation, numerous works begin to
study the black-box
attack [goodfellow2015explaining](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}
;
[dong2018boosting](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref}
;
[xie2019improving](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref}
;
[gao2020patch](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}
;
[cheng2021fast](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}
;
[naseer2022on](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref}
, which relies on the cross-model transferability of adversarial
examples. Typically, most existing black-box attacks maintain image
quality by constraining the $L_{p}$-norm of
perturbation [dong2019evading](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref}
;
[gao2020patch](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}
;
[gao2020patch++](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref}
;
[naseer2021on](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref}
. However, the $L_{p}$-norm constraint in RGB color space is not an
ideal indicator for measuring the perceptual distance between two images
since adversarial examples with a small perturbation size may still look
unnatural to human
eyes [zhao2020towards](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref}
. Thus the unrestricted
attack [hosseini2018semantic](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}
;
[shamsabadi2020colorfool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}
;
[laidlaw2019functional](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}
;
[zhao2020adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}
;
[bhattad2020unrestricted](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
;
[shamsabadi2020edgefool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref}
;
[song2018constructing](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib34){.ltx_ref}
is proposed --- replacing the traditional small $L_{p}$-norm
perturbations with uncontrolled yet human-imperceptible ones, which is
more practically meaningful. Among existing unrestricted attacks, the
unrestricted color branch is a very promising direction. As demonstrated
in previous
works [laidlaw2019functional](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}
;
[bhattad2020unrestricted](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
;
[shamsabadi2020colorfool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}
, only manipulating the color of an image is not noticeable to human
eyes but can easily fool DNNs. This is mainly because global uniform
changes in images are usually imperceptible, and DNNs are vulnerable to
certain large-scale patterns.
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para}
However, current unrestricted color attacks usually sacrifice the
flexibility of the uncontrolled setting to ensure the naturalness of
adversarial examples: either rely on
intuition [hosseini2018semantic](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}
;
[shamsabadi2020colorfool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}
and objective
metric [bhattad2020unrestricted](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
or use relatively small
changes [laidlaw2019functional](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}
;
[zhao2020adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}
. Consequently, the transferability of adversarial examples generated by
these methods is limited.
:::

::: {#S1.p4 .ltx_para}
To remedy this, we propose a [Natural Color Fool (NCF)]{#S1.p4.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} based on a more flexible perturbation space.
Specifically, we build a color distribution library that contains
diverse yet realistic color distributions sampled from the ADE20K
dataset [zhou2017scene](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}
for each semantic class. By borrowing the color mapping technique
of [pitie2007the](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref}
, we utilize the library to generate a set of image variants with
realistic color distribution for each clean image. Then we use the
substitute model to select the most adversarial variant. To boost its
transferability, we further introduce [neighborhood search]{#S1.p4.1.2
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} and [initialization reset]{#S1.p4.1.3
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} to optimize, thus obtaining the resulting
adversarial example. Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Problem Formulation ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
illustrates the simplified pipeline for our method.
:::

::: {#S1.p5 .ltx_para}
In summary, our main contributions are:

-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i1}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    We observe that current unrestricted color attacks lack flexibility,
    which results in limited transferability of the adversarial
    examples.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i2}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    To alleviate this issue, we propose a Natural Color Fool (NCF),
    which fully exploits color distributions of semantic classes in an
    image to craft human-imperceptible, flexible, and highly
    transferable adversarial examples.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i3}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i3.p1 .ltx_para}
    Extensive experiments and visualizations demonstrate the
    effectiveness of our proposed method. Significantly, with high NIMA
    scores [talebi2018nima](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref}
    , NCF averagely outperforms state-of-the-art methods by
    [15.0%$\sim$`<!-- -->`{=html}32.9%]{#S1.I1.i3.p1.1.1 .ltx_text
    .ltx_font_bold} for fooling normally trained models, and
    [10.0%$\sim$`<!-- -->`{=html}25.3%]{#S1.I1.i3.p1.2.2 .ltx_text
    .ltx_font_bold} for evading defense models.
    :::
:::
:::

## [2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Related Works {#related-works .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [2.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Unrestricted Attacks {#unrestricted-attacks .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S2.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Unrestricted
attacks [laidlaw2019functional](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}
;
[xu2020adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref}
;
[duan2020adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}
;
[bhattad2020unrestricted](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
, which usually significantly modify the image but can yield
human-imperceptible adversarial examples, have been widely studied in
recent years. For example, some works modify domain-specific structural
attributes of the image.
SemanticAdv [xu2020adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref}
uses an attribute-conditioned image editing model to modify the
attributes of the face (e.g., modifying the degree of mouth opening).
advCam [duan2020adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}
hides adversarial perturbations by using the style transfer technique
(e.g., changing a traffic sign to a rust style). Of course, there are
also many works modify generic attributes of images, such as texture and
color. Specifically,
tAdv [bhattad2020unrestricted](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
infuses the texture of another image to generate adversarial examples.
EdgeFool [shamsabadi2020edgefool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref}
craft adversarial perturbations by enhancing edge details of an image.
:::

::: {#S2.SS1.p2 .ltx_para}
Compared with texture-based attacks, color-based ones usually yield more
natural results since manipulating groups of pixels along dimensions is
less perceptible to human eyes. To our knowledge,
Hosseini&Poovendran [hosseini2018semantic](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}
first proposed the Semantic Adversarial Examples (SAE) in this branch.
Specifically, they convert the image from the RGB color space to the HSV
one, then uniformly and randomly perturbed the H (Hue) and S
(Saturation) channels of the entire image.
Laidlaw&Feizi [laidlaw2019functional](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}
also notice that RGB color space is not a perceptually uniform space.
Thus, they operate in the CIELUV color space and propose a ReColorAdv
that uses a specific function to uniformly transform all pixels
belonging to the same color. Similar to ReColorAdv,
ACE [zhao2020adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}
proposes a simple piecewise-linear color adjustment filter to manipulate
the image. The main difference is that ACE treats three channels of an
image independent of each other when transformed.
AGV [baia2022one](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib40){.ltx_ref}
increases the attack effectiveness by filter composition. Unlike them,
cAdv [bhattad2020unrestricted](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
performs color transformation using a pretrained colorization network
and jointly varies input hints and masks to manipulate adversarial
examples.
:::

::: {#S2.SS1.p3 .ltx_para}
Among existing unrestricted color attacks,
ColorFool [shamsabadi2020colorfool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}
, which exploits image semantics to modify colors selectively, is the
most related to ours. Nevertheless, there are several significant
differences between our work and ColorFool. First, ColorFool requires
manually selecting several human-sensitive semantic classes (i.e., sky,
water, plants, and humans), while our approach does not need. Second,
ColorFool adds uncontrolled perturbations on human-insensitive semantic
classes, while our approach utilizes a color distribution library as a
guide. Third, ColorFool optimizes adversarial examples only via multiple
random trials, while our approach also utilizes the gradient of the
substitute model to fine-tune results.
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [2.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Adversarial Defenses {#adversarial-defenses .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S2.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
To mitigate the threat of adversarial examples, various defense methods
have been proposed for the past few years. Generally, existing defense
techniques can fall into two categories: adversarial
training [madry2018towards](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}
;
[tramer2018ensemble](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref}
;
[xie2019feature](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref}
;
[zhang2022adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref}
and input
pre-process [guo2018countering](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref}
;
[jia2019comdefend](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref}
;
[naseer2020a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib47){.ltx_ref}
. The former gains immunity to attacks by leveraging adversarial
examples during the training phase. For example, Tram$\grave{e}$r [et
al.]{#S2.SS2.p1.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} [tramer2018ensemble](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref}
improve the black-box robustness by considering adversarial examples
generated by other irrelevant models. Xie [et al.]{#S2.SS2.p1.1.2
.ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} [xie2019feature](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref}
introduce feature denoising modules and train them on adversarial
examples to build white-box robust models. Although adversarial training
is usually the most robust strategy, it suffers from expensive training
costs. To overcome this limitation, the input pre-process branch is
designed to cure the infection of adversarial examples before feeding to
DNNs. For example, Guo [et al.]{#S2.SS2.p1.1.3 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} [guo2018countering](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref}
introduce several input transformation techniques (e.g., JPEG
compression [guo2018countering](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref}
) to recover from the adversarial perturbations. Xie [et
al.]{#S2.SS2.p1.1.4 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} [xie2018mitigating](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib48){.ltx_ref}
propose R&P which mitigates the adversarial influence through random
resizing and padding. Liao [et al.]{#S2.SS2.p1.1.5 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} [liao2018defense](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib49){.ltx_ref}
come up with a high-level representation guided denoiser (HGD) to reduce
the effect of adversarial perturbations. Jia [et al.]{#S2.SS2.p1.1.6
.ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} [jia2019comdefend](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref}
propose an end-to-end image compression model called ComDefend against
adversarial examples. Naseer [et al.]{#S2.SS2.p1.1.7 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} [naseer2020a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib47){.ltx_ref}
train a neural representation purifier model (NRP) that cleans
adversarial perturbation based on the automatically derived supervision.
:::
:::
:::

## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Methodology {#methodology .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Problem Formulation {#problem-formulation .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Let $\mathbf{x}$ be a clean image with true label $y$ and
$\mathcal{F}_{\theta}\operatorname{}{( \cdot )}$ denote the deep
learning classifier with parameters $\theta$. Formally, unrestricted
attacks aim to craft human-imperceptible adversarial perturbations (may
be very large in RGB color space) for $\mathbf{x}$ so that the resulting
adversarial examples ${\mathbf{x}}^{\prime}$ can induce
$\mathcal{F}_{\theta}\operatorname{}{( \cdot )}$ to misclassify:

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathcal{F}_{\theta}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{x}}^{\prime})}} \neq {y,s}}.t.{{\mathbf{x}}^{\prime}\operatorname{}{{is}{{natural}.}}}$$      [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

In this paper, we focus on a more challenging black-box scenario
(compared to the white-box scenario), where the target model's
information (e.g., parameters and structure) is inaccessible. Therefore,
adversarial examples can only be crafted via the accessible substitute
model $\mathcal{F}_{\phi}\operatorname{}{( \cdot )}$ and rely upon their
transferability to fool target models.
:::

Finally, we fine-tune <span
class="math inline"><strong>T</strong></span> (i.e., neighborhood
search) according to the loss <span
class="math inline">ℒ<sub><em>a</em> <em>d</em> <em>v</em></sub></span>
so that more threatening adversarial examples can be
mapped.</figcaption>
</figure>
:::

::: {#S3.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Color Distribution Library {#color-distribution-library .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
Existing unrestricted color attacks tend to sacrifice their flexibility
so that they can generate human-imperceptible adversarial examples. For
example,
ReColorAdv [laidlaw2019functional](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}
requires constraining the perturbation to a relatively small range,
which cannot take full advantage of the "unrestricted" setting;
cAdv [bhattad2020unrestricted](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
enforces the color belonging to the low-entropy cluster to remain
unchanged, which inevitably reduces the attack space;
ColorFool [shamsabadi2020colorfool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}
manually splits an image into two parts and adds controlled noises on
the human-sensitive part, which largely depends on the authors'
intuition (but it varies from person to person).
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.p2 .ltx_para}
To remedy the above limitation, we borrow the idea
of [afifi2019image](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref}
to construct a "distribution of color distributions" (DoD) for different
semantic classes based on the
ADE20K [zhou2017scene](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}
dataset. Specifically, for each class, DoD provides 20 different
dominant distribution sets (represented by the color distributions),
which are obtained by a hierarchical clustering based on color
palettes[^1^[[^1^[1]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_note}Color palette is a
simplified color distribution composed of object's primary colors. It is
used to reduce the computation cost of
clustering.]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote1 .ltx_note
.ltx_role_footnote} of corresponding segmentation class in the dataset.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.p3 .ltx_para}
Since the color distributions in each distribution set are similar and
averaging all color distributions of a specific distribution set may not
yield a natural representation, we randomly select a color distribution
to represent the overall color characteristics for simplicity.
Therefore, for a semantic class $\overset{\sim}{y}$, its realistic color
distribution space can be expressed as:

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathbb{C}}_{\overset{\sim}{y}} = {\{{\mathbf{c}}_{\overset{\sim}{y}}^{m}\}}_{m = 1}^{20}},$$      [(2)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where ${\mathbf{c}}_{\overset{\sim}{\mathbf{y}}}^{\mathbf{m}}$ is a
randomly sampled distribution from the $m$-th set, and our color
distribution library $\mathbb{C}$ can be denoted as:

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathbb{C}} = {{\mathbb{C}}_{1} \cup {\mathbb{C}}_{2} \cup \ldots \cup {\mathbb{C}}_{M}}},$$      [(3)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $M$ denotes the total number of semantic classes contained in our
library (see Appendix [[A]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#A1 "Appendix A Color Distribution Library ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
for the pipeline of building color distribution library). In the
following, we adopt this color distribution library to ensure the colors
of adversarial examples are natural and coordinated, thus making our
attack more flexible.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Natural Color Fool {#natural-color-fool .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS3.p1 .ltx_para}
To generate natural adversarial examples, precisely controlling the
color perception is necessary. In this paper, we craft adversarial
perturbations in CIELab color space where the perception is more uniform
than RGB color space. The framework of our proposed [Natural Color Fool
(NCF)]{#S3.SS3.p1.11.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} is illustrated in
Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Problem Formulation ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
(refer to Appendix [[B]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#A2 "Appendix B Algorithm ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
for Algorithm of NCF). Formally, for a given image $\mathbf{x}$, we
first obtain a semantic mask through a semantic segmentation model.
Then, for each semantic class $\overset{\sim}{y}$ in $\mathbf{x}$, we
randomly select a color distribution
${\mathbf{c}}_{\overset{\sim}{\mathbf{y}}}^{\mathbf{i}}$ from
${\mathbb{C}}_{\overset{\sim}{y}}$ in the color distribution library.
After that, we fuse the color distributions of all semantic classes
based on the proportion of images occupied by each class (e.g.,
$w_{c\operatorname{}a\operatorname{}r}$,
$w_{m\operatorname{}o\operatorname{}u}$ in Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Problem Formulation ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}),
thus generating a target color distribution $\mathbf{H}$. With it, we
can use the following Eq. [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.E4 "4 ‣ 3.3 Natural Color Fool ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
to generate a target image variant ${\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}^{\prime}$
(refer to Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Problem Formulation ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
which approximately replaces the color distribution of each semantic
class of $\mathbf{x}$ with corresponding one in target
$\mathbf{H}$ [pitie2007the](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref}
:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}^{\prime} = {{{\mathbf{T}}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{x}} - {\mathbf{μ}}_{\mathbf{x}}})}} + {\mathbf{μ}}_{{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}}}},$$      [(4)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     $${{{\mathbf{T}}\operatorname{}\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\mathbf{x}}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{T}}^{\top}} = \mathbf{\Sigma}_{{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}}},$$                          [(5)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where ${\mathbf{T}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{3 \times 3}$ is the transfer
matrix, ${\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}$ is an image reconstructed by
$\mathbf{H}$ but without spatial information[^2^[[^2^[2]{.ltx_tag
.ltx_tag_note}${\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}$ is used to calculate the
covariance matrix. Please note that calculation result is the same no
matter how we reconstruct
it.]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote2 .ltx_note
.ltx_role_footnote} (see Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Problem Formulation ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}),
and
${\mathbf{μ}}_{\mathbf{x}},{{\mathbf{μ}}_{{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}}\mspace{7mu}{({\in {\mathbb{R}}^{3}})}},\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{\Sigma}_{{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}}\mspace{7mu}{({\in {\mathbb{R}}^{3 \times 3}})}}$
are channel means of $\mathbf{x}$, channel means of
${\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}$, the covariance matrix of $\mathbf{x}$, and
the covariance matrix of ${\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}$, respectively.
:::

::: {#S3.SS3.p2 .ltx_para}
Formally, there are numerous
solutions [reinhard2002photo](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib51){.ltx_ref}
;
[abadpour2004fast](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib52){.ltx_ref}
;
[kotera2005a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib53){.ltx_ref}
;
[pitie2007automated](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib54){.ltx_ref}
for $\mathbf{T}$. However, most of them may not obtain an intended color
mapping, i.e., only color proportions are expected. To tackle this
issue, we follow Piti$\acute{e}$ [et al.]{#S3.SS3.p2.3.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} [pitie2007the](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref}
and convert color mapping into a Monge--Kantorovich transportation
problem, thus getting the solution for $\mathbf{T}$:

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathbf{T}} = {\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\mathbf{x}}^{- {\mathbf{1}/\mathbf{2}}}\operatorname{}\left( {\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\mathbf{x}}^{\mathbf{1}/\mathbf{2}}\operatorname{}\mathbf{\Sigma}_{{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}}\operatorname{}\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\mathbf{x}}^{\mathbf{1}/\mathbf{2}}} \right)^{1/2}\operatorname{}\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\mathbf{x}}^{- {\mathbf{1}/\mathbf{2}}}}}.$$      [(6)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
:::

::: {#S3.SS3.p3 .ltx_para}
Intuitively, relying solely on randomly picking color distribution may
limit the attack success rate. Since our color distribution library
contains a wide variety of color combinations and we have accessible
substitute models, there is no reason not to take advantage of them.
Thus, we utilize the substitute model
$\mathcal{F}_{\phi}\operatorname{}{( \cdot )}$ to select a most
adversarial distribution from $\eta$ different color distributions
sampled from our color distribution library:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathbf{H}}^{*} = {\underset{{\mathbf{H}}\in{\mathbb{H}}}{\arg ⁡\max}\operatorname{}\mathcal{L}_{a\operatorname{}d\operatorname{}v}\operatorname{}{({\mathcal{F}_{\phi}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{H}}^{\prime})}},y)}}$$      [(7)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $\mathbb{H}$ is the set containing $\eta$ different color
distributions and $\mathcal{L}_{a\operatorname{}d\operatorname{}v}$ is
the C&W
loss [carlini2017towards](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}
:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathcal{L}_{a\operatorname{}d\operatorname{}v}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{z}},y)}} = {{\max{\{{{\mathbf{z}}_{j}:{j \neq y}}\}}} - {\mathbf{z}}_{y}}},$$      [(8)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

where ${\mathbf{z}}_{j}$ denotes the logit concerning the $j$-th class.
To further boost our attack, we propose two techniques: [neighborhood
search]{#S3.SS3.p3.8.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} and [initialization
reset]{#S3.SS3.p3.8.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. Specifically, the
former aims to slightly adjust $\mathbf{T}$ (derived from
Eq. [[6]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.E6 "6 ‣ 3.3 Natural Color Fool ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
to improve the attack success rate while ensuring a natural mapping
result (see the comparison of adversarial examples with or without
adopting neighborhood search in Appendix [[E]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#A5 "Appendix E Neighborhood Search Impact on Human Perception ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
Thus, the neighborhood search can be formulated as the following
optimization problem:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{\underset{{\mathbf{T}}^{\prime}}{\arg ⁡\max}\operatorname{}\mathcal{L}_{a\operatorname{}d\operatorname{}v}\operatorname{}{({\mathcal{F}_{\phi}\operatorname{}{({{{\mathbf{T}}^{\prime}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{x}} - {\mathbf{μ}}_{\mathbf{x}}})}} + {\mathbf{μ}}_{{\mathbf{x}}_{{\mathbf{H}}^{*}}}})}},y)}}\mspace{21mu} s}.t.{{\|{{\mathbf{T}} - {\mathbf{T}}^{\prime}}\|}_{\infty} \leq \epsilon}},$$      [(9)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $\epsilon$ denotes the maximal perturbation size for $\mathbf{T}$.
In our paper, we adopt
MI-FGSM [dong2018boosting](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref}
with $N$ iterations to optimize Eq. [[9]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.E9 "9 ‣ 3.3 Natural Color Fool ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
Nonetheless, an adversarial transfer matrix ${\mathbf{T}}^{\prime}$
found in the neighborhood of a specific $\mathbf{T}$ may not be
effective, since the search space is indeed limited. To alleviate this
issue, the initialization reset repeats the operation of selecting one
from $\eta$ randomly sampled color distributions (i.e.,
Eq. [[7]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.E7 "7 ‣ 3.3 Natural Color Fool ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
$K$ times, thus obtaining $K$ ideal initial color distributions for the
neighborhood search. Then we choose the best one among them:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${i^{\prime} = {\underset{i\in 1,2,..,K}{\arg ⁡\max}\operatorname{}\mathcal{L}_{a\operatorname{}d\operatorname{}v}\operatorname{}{({\mathcal{F}_{\phi}\operatorname{}{({{{\mathbf{T}}_{\mathbf{i}}^{\prime}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{x}} - {\mathbf{μ}}_{\mathbf{x}}})}} + {\mathbf{μ}}_{{\mathbf{x}}_{{\mathbf{H}}_{\mathbf{i}}^{*}}}})}},y)}}},$$      [(10)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------

where ${\mathbf{H}}_{\mathbf{i}}^{*}$ and
${\mathbf{T}}_{\mathbf{i}}^{\prime}$ denote the resulting color
distribution and corresponding transfer matrix of the $i$-th reset.
Consequently, resulting adversarial examples can be obtained by:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathbf{x}}^{\prime} = {{{\mathbf{T}}_{{\mathbf{i}}^{\prime}}^{\prime}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{x}} - {\mathbf{μ}}_{\mathbf{x}}})}} + {\mathbf{μ}}_{{\mathbf{x}}_{{\mathbf{H}}_{{\mathbf{i}}^{\prime}}^{*}}}}}.$$      [(11)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------
:::

::: {#S3.SS3.p4 .ltx_para}
In Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3.3 Natural Color Fool ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
we show resulting adversarial examples generated by our proposed method
and recent works, i.e.,
cAdv [bhattad2020unrestricted](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
,
ColorFool [shamsabadi2020colorfool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}
, and
ACE [zhao2020adversarial](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}
(see Appendix [[G]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#A7 "Appendix G Visualization of Adversarial Examples ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
for more comparisons). Notably, despite our NCF implements significant
modifications to the color distribution of the original images, the
adversarial examples still look very natural.
:::

#### [4.2.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsubsection}Results on Defense Models {#results-on-defense-models .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsubsection}

::: {#S4.SS2.SSS2.p1 .ltx_para}
Threats posed by $L_{p}$-norm adversarial
attacks [goodfellow2015explaining](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}
;
[dong2018boosting](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref}
;
[gao2020patch](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}
;
[wang2021feature](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref}

defenses [madry2018towards](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}
;
[xie2019feature](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref}
;
[naseer2020a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib47){.ltx_ref}
. However, whether these defense mechanisms are robust against
unrestricted attacks has not been extensively explored. Therefore, in
this section, we consider both input pre-process defenses (i.e.,
JPEG [guo2018countering](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref}
, Gray,
R&P [xie2018mitigating](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib48){.ltx_ref}
,

,
ComDefend [jia2019comdefend](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref}
, and
NRP [naseer2020a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib47){.ltx_ref}
) and adversarially trained models (i.e., Inc-v3$_{ens3}$,
IncRes-v2$_{ens}$,
Res$_{B}$ [tramer2018ensemble](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref}
, Res$_{D}$, and
ResNext$_{DA}$ [xie2019feature](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref}
) to fully investigate the performance of our method.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.SSS2.p2 .ltx_para}
The black-box success rates of unrestricted attacks are reported in
Table [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S4.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 4.2.1 Results on Normally Trained Models ‣ 4.2 Transferability Comparison ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
Similar to Table [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S4.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 4.2.1 Results on Normally Trained Models ‣ 4.2 Transferability Comparison ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
our approach still consistently outperforms existing advanced methods by
a large margin. On average, our proposed NCF is capable of making
{#S4.SS2.SSS2.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} adversarial
examples fool these defenses, while SA, ReColorAdv, cAdv, ColorFool, and
ACE only obtain success rates of 
respectively. Another interesting observation from Table [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S4.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 4.2.1 Results on Normally Trained Models ‣ 4.2 Transferability Comparison ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
is that advanced defense algorithms are not necessarily effective when
faced with unrestricted adversarial examples. For example, even
white-box robust feature denoising
models [xie2019feature](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref}
are vulnerable, e.g., [60.1%]{#S4.SS2.SSS2.p2.1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} adversarial examples can fool ResNeXt$_{DA}$. We
speculate the main reason is that the defense mechanism is intermediate
feature denoising, while unrestricted color attacks mainly manipulate
the color and do not generate significant high-frequency noise.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.SSS2.p3 .ltx_para}
In addition, we notice that many input pre-process defenses with target
model VGG-19 cannot defend against our proposed NCF, but instead boost
the transferability of adversarial examples (compare to the VGG-19
results of Table [[1]{.ltx_text

To better analyze this abnormal phenomenon, we visualize the purified

Interestingly, we observe that all defenses have little effect on our
unrestricted color perturbations. It is mainly because our adversarial
examples are crafted using realistic colors as a guide and, therefore,
can circumvent these defenses designed for common perturbation
characteristics. Besides, we observe that JPEG and NRP suppress key
features of "Great egret", and ComDefend generates many irrelevant
repeating patterns. As discussed
in [zhang2022practical](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib66){.ltx_ref}

model concerning the true class, resulting in our adversarial examples
instead of boosting the transferability after being processed by these
approaches. This result also reminds us that the robustness evaluation
of defenses needs to be more comprehensive.

### [4.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Image Quality {#image-quality .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS3.p1 .ltx_para}
Following
ColorFool [shamsabadi2020colorfool](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}
, we quantitatively evaluate image quality by a non-reference perceptual
image quality measure called neural image assessment
(NIMA) [talebi2018nima](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref}
which is highly correlated with human perception. In our paper, NIMA is
based on the architecture of
MobileNet [howard2017mobilenets](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib67){.ltx_ref}
and is a composite of two models: one trains on
AVA [murray2012ava](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib68){.ltx_ref}
for image technical assessment and another trains on
TID2013 [ponomarenko2013color](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib69){.ltx_ref}
for image aesthetics assessment. As reported in Table [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S4.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 4.4 Ablation Studies ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
image quality assessment results of our NCF are on par with those of
state-of-the-art competitors. For NIMA (technical), both ACE and our NCF
obtain a high score of over [4.939]{#S4.SS3.p1.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}, which is just [0.094]{#S4.SS3.p1.1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} lower than clean images. The case of NIMA (aesthetic) is
similar, where aesthetic scores of our adversarial examples and clean
images differ by only [0.097]{#S4.SS3.p1.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold},
indicating that our NCF does not destroy the perceptual quality of
images.

## [5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Conclusion {#conclusion .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S5.p1 .ltx_para}
In this paper, we propose a black-box unrestricted color attack called
Natural Color Fool (NCF). Different from existing works which either
rely on intuition and objective metrics or use relatively small changes
to yield natural results, our method instead exploits color distribution
to craft human-imperceptible, flexible, and highly transferable
adversarial examples. Extensive experiments and visualizations
demonstrate the effectiveness of our NCF. Notably, our adversarial
examples can easily evade current $L_{p}$ robust defense techniques.
Furthermore, the transferability of our adversarial examples is even
boosted after being processed by several input pre-process defenses. We
hope our work can draw researchers' attention to unrestricted color
attacks.
:::

::: {#S5.p2 .ltx_para}
[Limitation.]{#S5.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} In our paper, for
each semantic class, we provide 20 different color distributions for
choosing. Therefore, this library is discrete, which may be insufficient
to model the whole color space. In the future, we will try to build a
continuous color space to expand the spatial range and more accurately
simulate the color range of objects.
:::

::: {#S5.p3 .ltx_para}
[Negative Societal Impacts.]{#S5.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}
Adversarial examples crafted by our proposed unrestricted color attack
look very natural but are with strong black-box transferability even
towards defenses. Therefore, unscrupulous people may adopt our method to
undermine real-world applications, which inevitably raises new concerns
about AI safety.
:::
:::

## [Appendix A ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Color Distribution Library {#appendix-a-color-distribution-library .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A1.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we explain how to build our color distribution library.
First, all semantic classes are extracted based on the images and
annotations of the ADE20K training
set \[[35](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}\]
(ADE20K contains 150 semantic classes). The semantic classes are
clustered into a set of 20 different dominant distribution sets
according to their color styles using hierarchical clustering, which is
the "distribution of color distribution"
(DoD) \[[50](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref}\].
Then, since the color distributions of semantic classes in each cluster
are similar, we select one object in each cluster to represent that
style. Finally, we extract the color distributions to form our color
distribution library. The pipeline is shown in Figure [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#A1.F5 "Figure 5 ‣ Appendix A Color Distribution Library ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

## [Appendix C ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Initialization Reset {#appendix-c-initialization-reset .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A3.p1 .ltx_para}
As mentioned in Section [[3.3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S3.SS3 "3.3 Natural Color Fool ‣ 3 Methodology ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
initialization reset (IR) avoids the optimization of convert matrix to
fall into local optimum. Therefore, we study the appropriate value for
the number of resets in this section. Our adversarial examples are
crafted via Inc-v3 by NCF with different resets, i.e., from 1 to 20. We
report the attack success rate on the victim model in
Figure [[6]{.ltx_text

As demonstrated in the figure, in the beginning, there is an approximate
positive relationship between the attack success rate and the number of
resets. Once $K = 10$ or larger, the attack success rate maintains
stable. We choose $K = 10$ to reduce the computational overhead, where
the attack successes rate is 84.0%, 57.7%, 57.6%, 56.8%, 40.1%, 47.6%
for
Inc-v3 \[[61](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib61){.ltx_ref}\],
Res-18 \[[2](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}\],

Dense-121 \[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]
and

## [Appendix D ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Transferability on ViTs {#appendix-d-transferability-on-vits .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A4.p1 .ltx_para}
Recent
work \[[70](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib70){.ltx_ref}\]
points out that the vision transformer models are more concerned with
low-frequency information, thus we further evaluate attack success rate
of black-box when Transformer models are used as substitute models.
Specifically, we generate adversarial examples using ViT-S/16
(ViT-S) \[[63](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib63){.ltx_ref}\]
and XCiT-N12/16
(XCiT-N12) \[[64](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib64){.ltx_ref}\]
as substitute models and evaluate the adversarial examples on multiple
Transformer models (including
ViT-S,XCiT-N12,DeiT-S \[[63](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib63){.ltx_ref}\],ViT-B \[[14](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref}\],Swin-T \[[65](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib65){.ltx_ref}\],PiT-Ti \[[71](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib71){.ltx_ref}\])
and multiple CNNs (including
Res-18 \[[2](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}\],
VGG-19 \[[60](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib60){.ltx_ref}\],
Mobile-v2 \[[62](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib62){.ltx_ref}\]).
Table [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#A4.T5 "Table 5 ‣ Appendix D Transferability on ViTs ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
summarizes the results on different black-box models. We can observe
that our NCF usually achieves the highest transferability on black-box.
In particular, when transferring from ViTs to CNNs, NCF achieves an
average success rate of 59.8%, but other attacks only achieve 48.3%
(SAE), 29.9% (ReColorAdv), 36.8% (cAdv), 35.1% (ColorFool), 27.3% (ACE).
:::

::: {#A4.p2 .ltx_para}
We observe that the transferability of adversarial examples between ViTs
is weaker than different CNNs (see Section [[4.2.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#S4.SS2.SSS1 "4.2.1 Results on Normally Trained Models ‣ 4.2 Transferability Comparison ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
This is consistent with the observation in
 \[[25](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref}\].
It may be since that ViTs have multiple self-attention blocks that can
generate class tokens independently. However, our loss only utilizes the
last logit (which is equivalent to utilizing only the last class token).
The discriminative information in the previous tokens is not directly
utilized, which results the poor transferability of the adversarial
examples between different ViTs.
:::

attack results).</figcaption>

## [Appendix J ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Attack by Selecting Random Colors {#appendix-j-attack-by-selecting-random-colors .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A10.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we discuss the difference between NCF-IR-NS and random
color attack. Formally, NCF-IR-NS does not mean selecting random colors
to attack. Specifically, it first generates a set of adversarial
examples with different color distributions and then selects the best
example from them based on the loss of the white-box model to attack.
Therefore, NCF-IR-NS is close to a white-box attack.
:::

::: {#A10.p2 .ltx_para}
To support our claim, we evaluate the performance of random color attack
(NCF-IR-NS-⁢), i.e., randomly select colors for each semantic class and
use the resulting adversarial examples to attack. As demonstrated in
Table [[8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.02041#A10.T8 "Table 8 ‣ Appendix J Attack by Selecting Random Colors ‣ Natural Color Fool: Towards Boosting Black-box Unrestricted Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
the performance of NCF-IR-NS-⁢ is much lower than NCF-IR-NS. For example,
NCF-IR-NS-⁢ only achieves a 27.3% (degraded from 51.6%) success rate on
Inc-v3. Thus, directly using random colors to generate adversarial
examples is ineffective.
:::

<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_table">Table 8: </span>The
attack success rate of using white-box information and not using it.

white-box attack)</figcaption>
