::: ltx_abstract
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

A fundamental challenge of over-parameterized deep learning models is
learning meaningful data representations that yield good performance on
a downstream task without over-fitting spurious input features. This
work proposes [MaskTune]{#id2.id1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, a
masking strategy that prevents over-reliance on spurious (or a limited
number of) features. [MaskTune]{#id2.id1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
forces the trained model to explore new features during a single epoch
finetuning by masking previously discovered features.
[MaskTune]{#id2.id1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, unlike earlier
approaches for mitigating shortcut learning, does not require any
supervision, such as annotating spurious features or labels for subgroup
samples in a dataset. Our empirical results on biased MNIST, CelebA,
Waterbirds, and ImagenNet-9L datasets show that [MaskTune]{#id2.id1.4
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} is effective on tasks that often suffer from
the existence of spurious correlations. Finally, we show that
[MaskTune]{#id2.id1.5 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} outperforms or
achieves similar performance to the competing methods when applied to
the selective classification (classification with rejection option)
task. Code for [MaskTune]{#id2.id1.6 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} is
available at
[https://github.com/aliasgharkhani/Masktune](https://github.com/aliasgharkhani/Masktune){.ltx_ref
.ltx_url .ltx_font_typewriter target="_blank"}.
:::

## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para}
Spurious correlations are coincidental feature associations formed
between a subset of the input and target variables, which may be caused
by factors such as data selection bias (Torralba and Efros,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref};
Jabri et al.,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
The presence of spurious correlations in training data can cause
over-parameterized deep neural networks to fail, often drastically, when
such correlations do not hold in test data (Sagawa et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref})
or when encountering domain shift (Arjovsky et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}).
Consider the classification problem of cows and camels (Beery et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}),
where most of the images of cows vs. camels are captured on green fields
vs. desert backgrounds due to selection bias (and perhaps the nature of
the problem that camels are often in the desert). A model trained on
such data may rely on the background as the key discriminative feature
between cows and camels, thus failing on images of cows on non-green
backgrounds or camels on non-desert backgrounds.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para}
In over-parameterized regimes, there are often several solutions with
almost identical loss values, and the optimizer (e.g., SGD) typically
selects a low-capacity one (Wilson et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib49){.ltx_ref};
Valle-Perez et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref};
Arpit et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref};
Kalimeris et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib20){.ltx_ref}).
In the presence of spurious correlations, the optimizer might choose to
leverage them as they generally demand less capacity than the expected
semantic cues of interest, e.g., relying on the local color or texture
of grass instead of the elaborate visual features that give a cow its
appearance (Bruna and Mallat,
[2013](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref};
Bruna et al.,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref};
Brendel and Bethge,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref};
Khani and Liang,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para}
In previous work, a supervised loss function has been employed to reduce
the effect of spurious correlations (Sagawa et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}).
However, identifying and annotating the spurious correlations in a large
dataset as a training signal is impractical. Other works have attempted
to force models to discard context and background (as spurious features)
through input morphing using perceptual similarities (Taghanaki et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref})
or learning casual variables (Javed et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref}).
Discarding context and background, however, is incompatible with the
human visual system that relies on contextual information when detecting
and recognizing objects (Palmer,
[1975](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref};
Biederman et al.,
[1982](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref};
Chun and Jiang,
[1998](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref};
Henderson and Hollingworth,
[1999](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref};
Torralba,
[2003](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref}).
In addition, spurious features may appear on the object itself (e.g.,
facial attributes). Thus discarding the context and background may be a
futile strategy in these cases.
:::

::: {#S1.p4 .ltx_para}
Instead of requiring contextual and background information to be
discarded or relying on a limited number of features, we propose a
single-epoch finetuning technique called [MaskTune]{#S1.p4.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} that prevents a model from learning only the "first"
simplest mapping (potentially spurious correlations) from the input to
the corresponding target variable. [MaskTune]{#S1.p4.1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} forces the model to explore other input variables by
concealing (masking) the ones that have already been deemed
discriminatory. As we finetune the model with a new set of masked
samples, we force the training to escape its myopic and greedy
feature-seeking approach and encourage exploring and leveraging more
input variables. In other words, as the previous clues are hidden, the
model is constrained to find alternative loss-minimizing input-target
mappings. [MaskTune]{#S1.p4.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} conceals the
first clues discovered by a fully trained model, whether they are
spurious or not. This forces the model to investigate and leverage new
complementary discriminatory input features. A model relying upon a
broader array of complementary features (some may be spurious while
others are not) is expected to be more robust to test data missing a
subset of these features.
:::

::: {#S1.p5 .ltx_para}
Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref}
visualizes how [MaskTune]{#S1.p5.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} works
via a schematic of the cow-on-grass scenario. Even in the absence of
spurious correlations, models tend to focus on the shortcut (e.g., ears
or skin texture of a cow), which can prevent models from generalizing to
scenarios where those specific parts are missing. However, the object is
still recognizable from the remaining parts. As an alternative,
[MaskTune]{#S1.p5.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} generates a diverse
set of partially masked training examples, forcing the model to
investigate a wider area of the input features landscape e.g., new
pixels.
:::

::: {#S1.p6 .ltx_para}
A further disadvantage of relying on a limited number of features is the
model's inability to know when it does not know. Let's go back to the
cow-camel classification example; if cows only appear on grass in the
training set then it is unclear which of the grass or the cow refers to
the \"cow\" label. A model that only relies on the grass feature can
confidently make a wrong prediction when some other object appear in the
grass in the test time. We need the model to predict only if both cow
and grass appear in the picture and [abstain]{#S1.p6.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} otherwise. One method used in the literature to
address this issue is selective classification (Geifman and El-Yaniv,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref},
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref};
Khani et al.,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref}),
which allows a network to reject a sample if it is not confident in its
prediction. Selective classification is essential in mission-critical
applications such as autonomous driving, medical diagnostics, and
robotics as they need to defer the prediction to human if they are
uncertain about the prediction. Learning different sets of
discriminatory features, in addition to reducing the effect of spurious
features, enables [MaskTune]{#S1.p6.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} to
be applied to the problem of selective classification.
:::

::: {#S1.p7 .ltx_para}
We apply [MaskTune]{#S1.p7.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} to two main
tasks: a) robustness to spurious correlations, and b) selective
classification. We cover four different datasets under (a) including
MNIST with synthetic spurious features, CelebA and Waterbirds with
spurious features in different subgroups (Sagawa et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}),
and the Background Challenge (Xiao et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref})
which is a dataset for measuring the reliance of methods on background
information for prediction. Under (b) we test [MaskTune]{#S1.p7.1.2
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al.,
[2009](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}),
SVHN (Netzer et al.,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}),
and Cats vs. Dogs (Geifman and El-Yaniv,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref})
datasets. On both tasks, we outperform or perform similarly to the
previous complex methods using our simple technique.
:::

::: {#S1.p8 .ltx_para}
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to present a
finetuning technique using masked data to overcome spurious
correlations. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
:::

## [2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Method {#method .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2.p1 .ltx_para}
[Setup.]{#S2.p1.4.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We consider the supervised
learning setting with inputs
$x \in \mathcal{X} \subset {\mathbb{R}}^{d}$ and corresponding labels
$y \in \mathcal{Y} = {\{ 1,\ldots,k\}}$. We assume having access to
samples $\mathcal{D}^{0} = {\{{(x_{i},y_{i})}\}}_{i = 1}^{n}$ drawn from
an unknown underlying distribution
$p_{d\operatorname{}a\operatorname{}t\operatorname{}a}\operatorname{}{(x,y)}$.
:::

::: {#S2.p2 .ltx_para}
Our goal is to learn the parameters $\theta \in \Theta$ of a prediction
model $m_{\theta}:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y}}$ that obtains low
classification error w.r.t some loss function (e.g., cross entropy)
$\ell:{{\Theta \times {({\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}})}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}}$.
Specifically, we minimize:\

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathcal{L}\operatorname{}{(\theta)}} = {{\mathbb{E}}_{{x,y} \sim {p_{data}\operatorname{}{(x,y)}}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{({m_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(x)}},y)}}\rbrack}} \approx {\frac{1}{n}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}{\ell\operatorname{}{({m_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}},y_{i})}}}}$$      [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
:::

::: {#S2.p3 .ltx_para}
where $n$ is the number of pairs in the training data.
:::

::: {#S2.p4 .ltx_para}
Besides having good prediction accuracy, we aim to develop a model which
does not solely rely on spurious or a limited number of input features.
We propose to mask the input training data to create a new set. We then
finetune ([for only one epoch]{#S2.p4.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}) a
fully trained ERM model with the new masked data to reduce over-reliance
on spurious or a limited number of features. The single epoch
fine-tuning is done using a small learning rate e.g., the last decayed
learning rate that the ERM has used in the first step. We found that
large learning rates or more than one epoch fine-tuning leads to
forgetting the discriminative features learned by the ERM.
:::

::: {#S2.p5 .ltx_para}
[Input Masking.]{#S2.p5.8.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} A key ingredient
of our approach is a masking function $\mathcal{G}$ that is applied
offline (i.e., after full training). The goal here is to construct a new
masked dataset by concealing the most discriminative features in the
input discovered by a model after full training. This should encourage
the model to investigate new features with the masked training set
during finetuning. As for $\mathcal{G}$, we adopt the
xGradCAM (Selvaraju et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref}),
which was originally designed for a visual explanation of deep models by
creating rough localization maps based on the gradient of the model loss
w.r.t. the output of a desired model layer. Given an input image of size
$H \times W \times C$, xGradCAM outputs a localization map $\mathcal{A}$
of size $H \times W \times 1$, which shows the contribution of each
pixel of the input image in predicting the most probable class, i.e., it
calculates the loss by choosing the class with highest logit value (not
the true label) as the target class. After acquiring the localization
map, for each sample $(x_{i},y_{i})$, where $x_{i} \in X$ and
$y_{i} \in Y$, we mask the locations with the most contribution as:
:::

::: {#S2.p6 .ltx_para}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\hat{x_{i}} = {{\mathcal{T}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{A}_{x_{i}};\tau)}} \odot x_{i}}};{\mathcal{A}_{x_{i}} = {\mathcal{G}\operatorname{}{({m_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}},y_{i})}}}$$      [(2)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
:::

::: {#S2.p7 .ltx_para}
where $\mathcal{T}$ refers to a thresholding function by the threshold
factor $\tau$ (i.e.,
$\mathcal{T} = \mathbb{1}_{\mathcal{A}_{x_{i}} \leq \tau}$), and $\odot$
denotes element-wise multiplication. As the resolution of $\mathcal{A}$
is typically coarser than that of the input data,
$\mathcal{T}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{A}_{x_{i}})}$ is up-sampled to the
size of the input.
:::

::: {#S2.p8 .ltx_para}
Procedurally, we first learn model $m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}}$ using
original unmasked training data $\mathcal{D}^{\text{initial}}$. Then we
use $m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}}$, $\mathcal{G}$ and $\mathcal{T}$ to
create the masked set $\mathcal{D}^{\text{masked}}$. Finally, the fully
trained predictor $m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}}$ is tuned using
$\mathcal{D}^{\text{masked}}$ to obtain $m_{\theta}^{\text{final}}$.
:::

::: {#S2.p9 .ltx_para}
As for the masking step, any explainability approach can be applied
(note that some may have more computational complexity, such as
ScoreCAM (Wang et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib48){.ltx_ref})).
We use xGradCAM (Selvaraju et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref})
as it is fast and produces relatively denser heat-maps than other
methods (Srinivas and Fleuret,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref};
Selvaraju et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref};
Wang et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib48){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S2.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [2.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}[MaskTune]{#S2.SS1.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} in Over-parameterized Regimes {#masktune-in-over-parameterized-regimes .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S2.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Consider the overparametrized regime, in which the model family has
sufficient complexity to fully fit the training data. It has been shown
that deep neural nets are overparametrized and can fit completely random
data (Zhang et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib53){.ltx_ref}).
The generalization ability of deep neural nets is still not clear, but
there are some speculation that connect the deep network generalization
to their tendency of choosing simple functions that fit the training
data (Valle-Perez et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref};
Arpit et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}).
However, this simplicity bias can cause side effects such as their poor
performance with respect to adversarial examples (Raghunathan et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib34){.ltx_ref})
or to distribution shifts (Khani and Liang,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref};
Shah et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S2.SS1.p2 .ltx_para}
Here we study the effect of masking input features on complexity of a
model in a situation where indeed the training procedure chooses the
least complex model that fits training data. We show that in this case
masking will result in learning a more complex model that discovers new
features as the previous ones are blocked.
:::

::: {#S2.SS1.p3 .ltx_para}
Formally, let $\mathcal{C}$ denote a function that measures model
complexity and assume that the masking function $\mathcal{T}$ (as
described in [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#S2.E2 "2 ‣ 2 Method ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref})
only returns binary values, i.e., an indicator function that only keeps
some features and zeros out the rest. We show that if training procedure
returns the least complex model then masking results in a more complex
model.
:::

::: {#Thmproposition1 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_proposition}
###### [[Proposition 1]{#Thmproposition1.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[.]{#Thmproposition1.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#proposition-1. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmproposition1.p1 .ltx_para}
[Consider an optimizing procedure that finds
$\min\mathcal{C}{(m_{\theta})},s.t.,\ell{(m_{\theta})} = 0$ as defined
in [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#S2.E1 "1 ‣ 2 Method ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref}.
Let masking function $\mathcal{T}$ return binary values. If both models
$m_{\theta}$ and $m_{\theta}^{\text{𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡𝑖𝑎𝑙}}$ fit the training data
(i.e., zero loss) then we have
${\mathcal{C}\operatorname{}{(m_{\theta}^{\text{𝑓𝑖𝑛𝑎𝑙}})}} \geq {\mathcal{C}\operatorname{}{(m_{\theta}^{\text{𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡𝑖𝑎𝑙}})}}$.]{#Thmproposition1.p1.5.5
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS1.1 .ltx_proof}
###### Proof. {#proof. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_font_italic .ltx_title_proof}

::: {#S2.SS1.1.p1 .ltx_para}
Note that both models belong to the model family
(${m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}},m_{\theta}^{\text{final}}} \in \Theta$),
and they both fit the training data. In the first step, training
procedure chooses $m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}}$ over
$m_{\theta}^{\text{final}}$; therefore according to our assumption
${\mathcal{C}\operatorname{}{(m_{\theta}^{\text{final}})}} \geq {\mathcal{C}\operatorname{}{(m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}})}}$.
∎
:::
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [2.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Adapting [MaskTune]{#S2.SS2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} for Selective Classification {#adapting-masktune-for-selective-classification .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S2.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
Here we show how to use [MaskTune]{#S2.SS2.p1.2.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} for the selective classification problem. In order to
make a more reliable prediction, we ensemble the original model
($m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}}$) and [MaskTune]{#S2.SS2.p1.2.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} ($m_{\theta}^{\text{final}}$) and only predict if both
models agree. As a result, if there exist two sets of features that can
predict the label, our method only predicts if both agree on the label
(e.g., grass and cow in Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p2 .ltx_para}
To get an intuition on the performance of [MaskTune]{#S2.SS2.p2.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} for selective classification, similar to
(Khani et al.,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref})
we analyze the noiseless overparametrized linear regression. We show
that [MaskTune]{#S2.SS2.p2.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} adaptation,
explained above, abstains in the presence of covariate shift, thus
leading to a more reliable prediction.
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p3 .ltx_para}
In particular, we show that [MaskTune]{#S2.SS2.p3.12.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} only predicts if the relationship between masked and
unmasked features in training data holds in the test time. For example,
if features describing "cow" are predictable of "grass" in training data
(i.e., they always co-occur) then we only predict if they co-occur in
the test data as well. Formally, let $s$ denote the masked feature after
the first round and $z$ denote the rest of the $d - 1$ features. As we
are in the overparametrized regime, $s$ can be predicted from $z$, let
$\beta$ be the min-norm solution for predicting $s$ from $z$, i.e.,
$s = {\beta^{\top}\operatorname{}z}$ for training data. We now show that
both models agree with each other for a new test set if
$s = {\beta^{\top}\operatorname{}z}$ in the test time as well. In other
words, there is not covariate shift between $s$ and $z$.
:::

::: {#Thmproposition2 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_proposition}
###### [[Proposition 2]{#Thmproposition2.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[.]{#Thmproposition2.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#proposition-2. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmproposition2.p1 .ltx_para}
[Let $S \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$ be the concatenation, across all $n$
training samples, of a single masked scalar feature, out of $d$ possible
features, and $Z \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n \times {({d - 1})}}$ be the
remaining $d - 1$ features. Let the model family $\Theta$ be the linear
functions, and the optimization function chooses the min $L_{2}$-norm
solution that fits the training data. The models trained with and
without $S$ agree on the predicted output for a new test set $(z,s)$,
iff
$s = {{({Z^{\top}\operatorname{}{({Z\operatorname{}Z^{T}})}^{- 1}\operatorname{}S})}^{\top}\operatorname{}z}$.]{#Thmproposition2.p1.10.10
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p4 .ltx_para}
The full proof is in the appendix. Note that
$({Z^{\top}\operatorname{}{({Z\operatorname{}Z^{T}})}^{- 1}\operatorname{}S})$
is the min-norm solution for predicting $s$ from $z$ in the training
data. This proposition states that the models agree only if the relation
between the masked feature ($s$) and remaining features ($z$) in
training holds in the test data as well.
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p5 .ltx_para}
In practice, we need to trade off between the coverage and precision.
Therefore, instead of a hard threshold and predicting only if both
models agree, we ensemble $m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}}$ and
$m_{\theta}^{\text{final}}$ by multiplying their probabilities. In order
to achieve the coverage goal, we find the desired threshold for
abstaining in the validation set (see Section [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#S4 "4 Experimental Results ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref}
for details).
:::
:::

## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Implementation Details {#implementation-details .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.p1 .ltx_para}
[Classification with Spurious Features.]{#S3.p1.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} For all of the datasets in Section [[4.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#S4.SS1 "4.1 Classification with Spurious Features ‣ 4 Experimental Results ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref},
we use SGD with a momentum of 0.9 and weight decay of 1e-4. For biased
MNIST, we used a simple convolutional neural network with four
convolutional layers and a linear head. We trained this model with a
batch size of 128 for 100 epochs with a learning rate of 0.01. We
decreased the learning rate by a factor of 0.5 every 25 epochs. In the
case of Background Challenge, we trained an ImageNet pre-trained
ResNet50 with a batch size of 1,024 for 100 epochs decaying the learning
rate by 0.1 after every 30 epochs. For the CelebA dataset, we trained an
ImageNet pre-trained ResNet50 with a batch size of 512 for 20 epochs
with a learning rate of 1e-4.
:::

::: {#S3.p2 .ltx_para}
[Selective Classification.]{#S3.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} For all
the datasets in Section [[4.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#S4.SS2 "4.2 Selective Classification ‣ 4 Experimental Results ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref},
we trained a ResNet32 from scratch. For CIFAR-10, we used SGD with a
momentum of 0.9, weight decay of 1e-4, learning rate of 0.1, and batch
size of 128. We trained it for 300 epochs and halved the learning rate
every 25 epochs. For SVHN, we used the same hyperparameters as CIFAR-10
with only two differences: the weight decay and batch size were 5e-4 and
1,024, respectively. For Cats vs. Dogs dataset, we used Adam optimizer
with a weight decay of 1e-4 and a learning rate of 0.001. We trained the
model for 200 epochs with a batch size of 128 and dropped the learning
rate by a factor of 0.1 on epoch 50.
:::

::: {#S3.p3 .ltx_para}
[Masking Threshold.]{#S3.p3.9.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} Our goal is to
mask the most important features, i.e., the core of the heat maps
generated by the explainability methods. Masking a few input variables
has almost no effect on the model's behavior, whereas large masks may
destroy useful signals in the input, resulting in very low training
accuracy. To reduce the search space of the masking threshold $\tau$
across all tasks, we experimented with
$\tau = {\{\mu_{i},{\mu_{i} + \sigma_{i}},{\mu_{i} + {2\operatorname{}\sigma_{i}}},{\mu_{i} + {3\operatorname{}\sigma_{i}}}\}}$
where $\mu_{i}$ and $\sigma_{i}$ represent mean and standard deviation
over the heatmap values for training sample $x_{i}$. We also
experimented with mean value over [all]{#S3.p3.9.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} training samples, using soft masks (i.e., no
threshold), and sorting and masking the K-top activated variables. We
found $\mu_{i} + {2\operatorname{}\sigma_{i}}$ works better in general
as $\mu_{i}$ and $\mu_{i} + \sigma_{i}$ remove a large portion of the
input while $\mu_{i} + {3\operatorname{}\sigma_{i}}$ removes very few
variables.
:::
:::

## [4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Experimental Results {#experimental-results .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S4.p1 .ltx_para}
We evaluate our [MaskTune]{#S4.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} method
on two main applications: a) classification with spurious
correlations---we expect [MaskTune]{#S4.p1.1.2 .ltx_text

### [4.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Selective Classification {#selective-classification .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
For selective classification task, we evaluated
[MaskTune]{#S4.SS2.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} on three datasets:
CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al.,
[2009](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}),
SVHN (Netzer et al.,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}),
and Cats vs. Dogs (Geifman and El-Yaniv,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}),
with coverage values of {90%, 95%, 100%}. For example 90% coverage means
abstaining 10% of the samples.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p2 .ltx_para}
Given input image $j$ and the original ($m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}}$)
and finetuned ($m_{\theta}^{\text{final}}$) models, let their respective
inference-time prediction probabilities for class $i$ be
$P_{i\operatorname{}j}^{\text{init}}$ and
$P_{i\operatorname{}j}^{\text{final}}$. Then [MaskTune]{#S4.SS2.p2.10.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} does not abstain and declares the class $i$
as the predicted class, iff
${P_{i\operatorname{}j}^{\text{init}} \cdot P_{i\operatorname{}j}^{\text{final}}} > \gamma$,
where $\gamma$ is a threshold that allows for controlling the target
coverage. To find the proper $\gamma$ for achieving the targeted
coverage, we iterate over the validation set and find a threshold that,
if applied to the validation set, would give the desired coverage (i.e.,
if our target coverage is 90%, we seek for a threshold which if we apply
for abstention on the validation set, we will abstain predicting 10% of
validation data). Note that the probabilities of the two models are
multiplied to ensure that as the value of either probability decreases,
the possibility of abstention increases.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p3 .ltx_para}
We compare our method to the other selective classification approaches
such as Softmax Response (SR) (Geifman and El-Yaniv,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}),
SelectiveNet (SN) (Geifman and El-Yaniv,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}),
Deep Gamblers (DG) (Liu et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}),
and One-sided Prediction (OSP) (Gangrade et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}).
Like the OSP, we first split the training data into train and validation
sets. After training, we search for the abstention threshold on the
validation set, then use it at the test time. As seen in
Table [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#S4.T4 "Table 4 ‣ 4.2 Selective Classification ‣ 4 Experimental Results ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref},
[MaskTune]{#S4.SS2.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} outperforms all
prior techniques on all three datasets on 95% and 100% coverage rates.
For results on more coverage rates refer to Appendix [[A]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#A1 "Appendix A Appendix ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref}.

::: {#S5 .section .ltx_section}
## [5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Related Work {#related-work .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S5.p1 .ltx_para}
In the following, we review the related works which have attempted to
mitigate the effect of spurious correlation in training deep models. We
also discuss previous methods that use attention-based online masking
and how our method differs.
:::

::: {#S5.p2 .ltx_para}
[Robustness to Spurious Correlations.]{#S5.p2.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} Distributionally robust optimization (DRO) (Ben-Tal
et al.,
[2013](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref};
Gao et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref};
Duchi et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref})
has been proposed to improve generalization to worst cases (minority
distributions) in a dataset. However, the DRO objective leads to
disproportionate attention to the worst cases, even if they are
implausible. To address this issue, Sagawa et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref})
proposed to leverage subgroup information during optimization. Although
this method reduces the likelihood of the worst-case failure, it is
based on prior solid information (i.e., subgroup labels), which is not
always available. Several efforts have been made to reduce the
subgroup-level supervision.  Sohoni et al.
([2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib40){.ltx_ref})
proposed a clustering-based method for obtaining sub-group information
to be used in DRO setup. However, determining the number of clusters
(sub-groups) in a dataset is not trivial since a small number may still
dismiss minor subgroups while a large number may lower overall accuracy.
Yaghoobzadeh et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib52){.ltx_ref})
proposed measuring sample accuracy throughout training to discover
forgettable instances, then fine-tuning models using such samples to
increase model resilience against spurious correlations. Chen et al.
([2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref})
demonstrated that self-training can also help decrease the effect of
spurious features, but only if the source classifier is very accurate
and there are not too many isolated sub-groups in the data. Liu et al.
([2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref})
discovered that training a model twice helps it become resistant to
spurious correlation. However, if a model reaches high classification
accuracy in the first run, this technique is no longer useful since
there is not enough misclassified data to retrain the model.
[MaskTune]{#S5.p2.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, on the other hand,
employs an orthogonal approach to learn robust representations through
gradient-based masking and fine-tuning and does not require
subgroup-level labels during training or model selection.
:::

::: {#S5.p3 .ltx_para}
[Online Attention-based Masking.]{#S5.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} A
large number of studies suggest using attention-based (soft) masks to
eliminate irrelevant information from input data during training (Sharma
et al.,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref};
Wang et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib47){.ltx_ref};
Xu et al.,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib51){.ltx_ref};
Zheng et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib54){.ltx_ref}).
Although these methods increase overall classification accuracy, they
are incapable of disregarding spurious correlations since attention may
be on an area of the input where the spurious correlations are dominant
during training. For concentrating attention only on the foreground,  Li
et al.
([2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref})
developed a guided attention model. Their technique, however, needs
additional annotations of object locations/masks. Nonetheless, none of
these methods aimed to reduce the effects of spurious correlations. Even
with complete supervision (i.e., masking the whole background), spurious
correlations might still occur in the foreground. [MaskTune]{#S5.p3.1.2
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, on the other hand, produces masks from a
fully trained model and uses them for a single epoch fine-tuning.
[MaskTune]{#S5.p3.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} does not require any
additional supervision such as object location annotations.
:::
:::

::: {#S6 .section .ltx_section}
## [6 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Conclusion {#conclusion .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S6.p1 .ltx_para}
In this work, we considered the problem of preventing models from
learning spurious correlations. We introduced a new fine-tuning
technique that is designed explicitly for spurious correlations. It
enforces a model to explore more variables in the input and map them to
the same target. Through experiments and theoretical analysis on
classification with nuisance background information which typically
suffers from the presence of spurious correlations in the data, we
showed that models trained with [MaskTune]{#S6.p1.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} outperform previous relevant methods. We also showed
that [MaskTune]{#S6.p1.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} helps to improve
the accuracy significantly in selective classification tasks. Adapting
[MaskTune]{#S6.p1.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} for non-image data,
such as sentiment analysis, can be an intriguing future work. Another
area for future work is [how]{#S6.p1.1.4 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
best to identify and use the most effective masked samples (rather than
all) when fine-tuning the final model, perhaps using uncertainty
information or active learning. Although [MaskTune]{#S6.p1.1.5 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} can help reduce the effect of many types of spurious
correlation, such as texture, color, and localized nuisance features
e.g., artifacts added to x-ray images by medical imaging devices, there
are some cases where [MaskTune]{#S6.p1.1.6 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
may not be effective, such as a small transformation in all pixel values
for some of the images in a dataset. This occurs in medical devices or
cameras that add almost imperceptible color (values) to captured images.
:::

Running [MaskTune]{#A1.SS1.SSS0.Px2.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}

We added two coloured patches to MNIST digits as two distinct spurious
features. We ran 1, 2, and 3 iterations of masking.
[MaskTune]{#A1.SS1.SSS0.Px2.p1.1.5 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} works
well when we do iterative accumulative masking (i.e., add new masks to
the previously masked samples), but when we apply masks from each
iteration to the raw input (non-accumulative), the results are not as
good as the accumulative version. To reduce the running time, we only
used one iteration of masking. One way to stop the accumulative masking
is to monitor the training accuracy. If the model is not able to fit the
data after a certain number of masking iterations (because there are no
useful features left), the training can be stopped.

::: {#A3.1 .ltx_proof}
###### Proof. {#proof.-1 .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_font_italic .ltx_title_proof}

::: {#A3.1.p1 .ltx_para}
Let $Y \in {\mathbb{R}}^{d}$ be the targets in training data. Let
${\hat{\theta}}^{+ s}$ denote the recovered coefficient for the first
$d - 1$ features and $\hat{w}$ be the coefficient for the feature $s$.
The parameters after the first round can be obtained by the following
optimization problem.

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${{\hat{\theta}}^{+ s},\hat{w}} = {{\underset{\theta,w}{\arg\operatorname{}\min}{\|\theta\|}_{2}^{2}} + w^{2}}$      [(3)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     $s.t.\mspace{21mu} Z\theta + Sw = Y,$                                                                                [(4)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $\hat{w}$ denotes the weight associated with the feature $s$.
Recall that we assumed that after training the first model we mask the
feature $s$. The parameters for the second model that does not use
feature $s$ can be obtained by solving the following optimization
problem, where ${\hat{\theta}}^{- s}$ denote the recovered coefficients.

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${\hat{\theta}}^{- s} = {\underset{\theta}{\arg\operatorname{}\min}{\|\theta\|}_{2}^{2}}$      [(5)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     $s.t.\mspace{21mu} Z\theta = Y,$                                                               [(6)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

The minimum norm solution in the first round is:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $\theta^{+ s} = {Z^{\top}\operatorname{}{({Z\operatorname{}Z^{\top}})}^{- 1}\operatorname{}{({Y - {S\operatorname{}\hat{w}}})}}$      [(7)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

The min-norm solution for the second round is:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $\theta^{- s} = {Z^{\top}\operatorname{}{({Z\operatorname{}Z^{\top}})}^{- 1}\operatorname{}Y}$      [(8)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

Now we show that for a new test set $(z^{\star},s^{\star})$, the models
$m_{\theta}^{\text{final}}$ and $m_{\theta}^{\text{initial}}$ predict
the same if there is no covariate shift between $z$ and $s$. We are
assuming that $\hat{w}$ is not zero meaning that the first model is
using the feature $s$.

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     ${{({Z^{\top}\operatorname{}{({Z\operatorname{}Z^{\top}})}^{- 1}\operatorname{}Y})}^{\top}\operatorname{}z^{\star}} = {{{({Z^{\top}\operatorname{}{({Z\operatorname{}Z^{\top}})}^{- 1}\operatorname{}{({Y - {S\operatorname{}\hat{w}}})}})}^{\top}\operatorname{}z^{\star}} + {\hat{w}\operatorname{}s^{\star}}}$      [(9)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     ${(Z^{\top}{(ZZ^{\top})}^{- 1}S\hat{w})})^{\top}z^{\star} = \hat{w}s^{\star}$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          [(10)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     ${(Z^{\top}{(ZZ^{\top})}^{- 1}S)})^{\top}z^{\star} = s^{\star}$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        [(11)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------

For proving the other direction, if we have
${(Z^{\top}{(ZZ^{\top})}^{- 1}S)})^{\top}z^{\star} = s^{\star}$ then we
can deduce from Equation [[11]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#A3.E11 "11 ‣ Proof. ‣ Appendix C Missing proofs ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref}
to Equation [[9]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.00055#A3.E9 "9 ‣ Proof. ‣ Appendix C Missing proofs ‣ MaskTune: Mitigating Spurious Correlations by Forcing to Explore"){.ltx_ref}.
