::: ltx_abstract
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

Predicting the pose of objects from a single image is an important but
difficult computer vision problem. Methods that predict a single point
estimate do not predict the pose of objects with symmetries well and
cannot represent uncertainty. Alternatively, some works predict a
distribution over orientations in ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$. However,
training such models can be computation- and sample-inefficient.
Instead, we propose a novel mapping of features from the image domain to
the 3D rotation manifold. Our method then leverages
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ equivariant layers, which are more sample
efficient, and outputs a distribution over rotations that can be sampled
at arbitrary resolution. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method
at object orientation prediction, and achieve state-of-the-art
performance on the popular PASCAL3D+ dataset. Moreover, we show that our
method can model complex object symmetries, without any modifications to
the parameters or loss function. Code is available at
[https://dmklee.github.io/image2sphere](https://dmklee.github.io/image2sphere){.ltx_ref
.ltx_url .ltx_font_typewriter target="_blank"}.
:::

## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Determining the pose of an object from an image is a challenging problem
with important applications in artificial reality, robotics, and
autonomous vehicles. Traditionally, pose estimation has been approached
as a point regression problem, minimizing the error to a single ground
truth 3D rotation. In this way, object symmetries are manually
disambiguated using domain knowledge (Xiang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref})
and uncertainty is not accounted for. This approach to pose estimation
cannot scale to the open-world setting where we wish to reason about
uncertainty from sensor noise or occlusions and model novel objects with
unknown symmetries.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Recent work has instead attempted to learn a distribution over poses.
Single rotation labels can be modeled as random samples from the
distribution over object symmetries, which removes the need for
injecting domain knowledge. For instance, a table with front-back
symmetry presents a challenge for single pose regression methods, but
can be effectively modeled with a bimodal distribution. The drawback to
learning distributions over the large space of 3D rotations is that it
requires lots of data, especially when modeling hundreds of instances
across multiple object categories.
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
This poor data efficiency can be improved by constraining the weights to
encode symmetries present in the problem (Cohen & Welling,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}).
The pose prediction problem exhibits 3D rotational symmetry, e.g. the
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ abstract group. That is, if we change the
canonical reference frame of an object, the predictions of our model
should transform correspondingly. For certain input modalities, such as
point clouds or 3D camera images, the symmetry group acts directly on
the input data via 3D rotation matrices. Thus, many networks exploit the
symmetry with end-to-end ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ equivariance to
achieve sample efficient pose estimation. However, achieving 3D rotation
equivariance in a network trained on 2D images is less explored.
:::

::: {#S1.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Thus, we present Image2Sphere, I2S, a novel method that learns
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$-equivariant features to represent
distributions over 3D rotations. Features extracted by a convolutional
network are projected from image space onto the half 2-sphere. Then,
spherical convolution is performed on the features with a learned filter
over the entire 2-sphere, resulting in a signal that is equivariant to
3D rotations. A final ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ group convolution
operation produces a probability distribution over
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ parameterized in the Fourier domain. Our
method can be trained to accurately predict object orientation and
correctly express ambiguous orientations for objects with symmetries not
specified at training time.
:::

::: {#S1.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
I2S achieves state-of-the-art performance on the PASCAL3D+ pose
estimation dataset, and outperforms all baselines on the
ModelNet10-SO(3) dataset. We demonstrate that our proposed architecture
for learning ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ equivariant features from images
empirically outperforms a variety of sensible, alternative approaches.
In addition, we use the diagnostic SYMSOL datasets to show that our
approach is more expressive than methods using parametric families of
multi-modal distributions at representing complex object symmetries.
:::

## [2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Related Work {#related-work .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
##### 6D Pose Estimation {#d-pose-estimation .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Predicting the 6D pose (e.g. 3D position and orientation) of objects in
an image has important applications in fields like robotics (Tremblay
et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}),
autonomous vehicles (Geiger et al.,
[2012](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}),
and microscopy (Levy et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref}).
Most popular methods use deep convolutional networks, which are robust
to occlusions and can handle multi-object scenes with a segmentation
module (He et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
Convolutional networks have been trained using a variety of output
formulations. Xiang et al.
([2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref})
regresses the 3D bounding box of the object in pixel space, while He
et al.
([2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref})
predicts 3D keypoints on the object with which the pose can be
extracted. Another line of work (Wang et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref};
Li et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref};
Zakharov et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref})
outputs a dense representation of the object's coordinate space. Most of
these methods are benchmarked on datasets with limited number of object
instances (Hinterstoisser et al.,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib20){.ltx_ref};
Xiang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref}).
In contrast, our method is evaluated on datasets that have hundreds of
object instances or novel instances in the test set. Moreover, our
method makes minimal assumptions about the labels, requiring only a 3D
rotation matrix per image regardless of underlying object symmetry.
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
##### Rotation Equivariance {#rotation-equivariance .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Symmetries present in data can be preserved using equivariant neural
networks to improve performance and sample efficiency. For the symmetry
group of 3D rotations, ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$, a number of
equivariant models have been proposed. Chen et al.
([2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref})
and Fuchs et al.
([2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref})
introduce networks to process point cloud data with equivariance to the
discrete icosahedral group and continuous ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$
group, respectively. Esteves et al.
([2019b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref})
combines images from structured viewpoints and then performs discrete
group convolution to classify shapes. Cohen et al.
([2018a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref})
introduces spherical convolution to process signals that live on the
sphere, such as images from 3D cameras. However, these methods are
restricted to cases where the ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ group acts on
the input space, which prevents their use on 2D images. Falorsi et al.
([2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref})
and Park et al.
([2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref})
extract 3D rotational equivariant features from images to model object
orientation, but were limited to simplistic datasets with a single
object. Similar to our work, Esteves et al.
([2019a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref})
learns ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ equivariant embeddings from image
input for object pose prediction; however, they use a supervised loss to
replicate the embeddings of a spherical convolutional network pretrained
on 3D images. In contrast, our method incorporates a novel architecture
for achieving ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ equivariance from image inputs
that can be trained end-to-end on the challenging pose prediction tasks.
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px3 .section .ltx_paragraph}
##### Uncertainty over ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ {#uncertainty-over-sooperatorname3 .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Due to object symmetry or occlusion, there may be a set of equivalent
rotations that result in the same object appearance, which makes pose
prediction challenging. Most early works into object pose prediction
have avoided this issue by either breaking the symmetry when labelling
the data (Xiang et al.,
[2014](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref})
or applying loss functions to handle to known symmetries (Xiang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref};
Wang et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref}).
However, this approach requires knowing what symmetries are present in
the data, and does not work for objects that have ambiguous orientations
due to occlusion (e.g. coffee mug when the handle is not visible).
Several works have proposed models to reason about orientation
uncertainty by predicting the parameters of von Mises (Prokudin et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}),
Fisher (Mohlin et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref}),
and Bingham (Gilitschenski et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref};
Deng et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref})
distributions. However, multi-modal distributions required for modeling
complex object symmetries which can be difficult to train. Recent work
by Murphy et al.
([2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref})
leverages an implicit model to produce a non-parametric distribution
over ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ that can model objects with large
symmetry groups. Our method also generates a distribution over
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ that can model symmetric objects, but uses
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$-equivariant layers to achieve higher accuracy
and sample efficiency.
:::
:::
:::

## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Background {#background .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Equivariance {#equivariance .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Equivariance formalizes what it means for a map $f$ to be symmetry
preserving. Let $G$ be a symmetry group, that is, a set of
transformations that preserves some structure. Assume $G$ acts on spaces
$\mathcal{X}$ and $\mathcal{Y}$ via $\mathcal{T}_{g}$ and
$\mathcal{T}_{g}^{\prime}$, respectively. For all $g \in G$, a map
$f:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y}}$ is equivariant to $G$ if

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${{f\operatorname{}{({\mathcal{T}_{g}\operatorname{}x})}} = {\mathcal{T}_{g}^{\prime}\operatorname{}f\operatorname{}{(x)}}}.$      [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

That is, if the input of $f$ is transformed by $g$, the output will be
transformed in a corresponding way. Invariance is a special case of
equivariance when $\mathcal{T}_{g}^{\prime}$ is the identity map (i.e.
applying group transformations to the input of an invariant mapping does
not affect the output).
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Group convolution over Homogeneous Spaces {#group-convolution-over-homogeneous-spaces .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The group convolution operation is a linear equivariant mapping which
can be equipped with trainable parameters and used to build equivariant
neural networks (Cohen & Welling,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}).
Convolution is performed by computing the dot product with a filter as
it is shifted across the input signal. In standard 2D convolution, the
shifting corresponds to a translation in pixel space. Group convolution
(Cohen & Welling,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref})
generalizes this idea to arbitrary symmetry groups, with the filter
transformed by elements of the group. Let $G$ be a group and
$\mathcal{X}$ be a homogeneous space, i.e. a space on which $G$ acts
transitively, for example, $\mathcal{X} = G$ or $\mathcal{X} = {G/H}$
for a subgroup $H$. We compute the group convolution between two
functions, ${f,\psi}:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{k}}$, as
follows:

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${{{\lbrack{f \star \psi}\rbrack}\operatorname{}{(g)}} = {\sum\limits_{x \in \mathcal{X}}{{{f\operatorname{}{(x)}} \cdot \psi}\operatorname{}{({\mathcal{T}_{g}^{- 1}\operatorname{}x})}}}}.$      [(2)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

Note that the output of the convolution operation is defined for each
group element $g \in G$, while the inputs, $f$ and $\psi$, are defined
over a homogeneous space of $G$. By parameterizing either $f$ or $\psi$
using trainable weights, group convolution may be used as a layer in an
equivariant model.
:::

::: {#S3.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$--Equivariance {#sooperatorname3equivariance .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
For reasoning about physical objects, such as pose detection or part
segmentation, it is desirable to learn functions that are equivariant to
the group ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ of 3D rotations. In order to build
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$-equivariant neural networks using group
convolution, it is necessary to efficiently parameterize the space of
signals $\{ f:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}}\}$ over the
homogenous spaces $\mathcal{X} = S^{2}$ or
$\mathcal{X} = {{SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}}$. Cohen et al.
([2018b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref})
give an effective and compact solution in terms of the truncated Fourier
transform. Analogous to the Fourier basis over the circle, it is
possible to give a Fourier basis for signals defined over $S^{2}$ in
terms of the spherical harmonics $Y_{k}^{l}$ and for signals over
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ in terms of Wigner $D$-matrix coefficients
$D_{m\operatorname{}n}^{l}$. Writing
$f:{{{SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}}$ in terms of the
$D_{m\operatorname{}n}^{l}$ and then truncating to a certain frequency
$l \leq L$ we obtain an approximate representation
${f\operatorname{}{(g)}} \approx {\sum_{l = 0}^{L}{\sum_{m = 0}^{{2\operatorname{}l} + 1}{\sum_{n = 0}^{{2\operatorname{}l} + 1}{c_{m\operatorname{}n}^{l}\operatorname{}D_{m\operatorname{}n}^{l}\operatorname{}{(g)}}}}}$.
:::

::: {#S3.SS3.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ group convolution ([[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#S3.E2 "2 ‣ 3.2 Group convolution over Homogeneous Spaces ‣ 3 Background ‣ Image to Sphere: Learning Equivariant Features for Efficient Pose Prediction"){.ltx_ref})
can be efficiently computed in the Fourier domain using the convolution
theorem[^1^[[^1^[1]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_note}See Cohen et al.
([2018a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref})
for explanation and visuals describing efficient
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ group
convolution.]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote1 .ltx_note
.ltx_role_footnote}. Namely, the convolution of two functions is
calculated as the element-wise product of the functions in the Fourier
domain. For functions over ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$, the Fourier
domain is described by the Wigner $D$-matrix coefficients, corresponding
to a block diagonal matrix with a
${({{2\operatorname{}l} + 1})} \times {({{2\operatorname{}l} + 1})}$
block for each $l$ (Knapp,
[1996](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref}).
A functions over $S^{2}$ in the Fourier domain is described by a vector
of coefficients of spherical harmonics and convolution is performed
using an outer product (Cohen et al.,
[2018a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}).
This generates the same block diagonal matrix output as
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ convolution (e.g. both $S^{2}$ and
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ convolution generate signals that live on
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$). See Geiger et al.
([2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref})
for efficient implementation of Fourier and inverse Fourier transforms
of $S^{2}$ and ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ signals.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS4 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ Distributions with the Fourier Basis {#sooperatorname3-distributions-with-the-fourier-basis .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS4.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The output of the above group convolution is a signal
$f:{{{SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}}$ defined as a
linear combination of Wigner $D$-matrices. While this signal is useful
for efficient group convolution, it cannot be directly normalized to
produce a probability distribution. Instead, the signal can be queried
at discrete points in ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ and then normalized
using a softmax. To reduce errors introduced by the discretization,
points should be taken from an equivolumetric grid. Equivolumetric grids
over ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ can be generated using an extension of
the HEALPix method developed by Yershova et al.
([2010](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref}).
The HEALPix method (Gorski et al.,
[2005](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref})
starts with 12 'pixels' that cover the sphere, and recursively divides
each pixel into four sections. The latitude and longitude of each pixel
describe two Euler angles of a rotation. The extension to
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ specifies the final Euler angle in a way that
the grid is equally-spaced in ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$. The resolution
of the final grid can be controlled by the number of recursions.
:::
:::
:::

::: {#S4 .section .ltx_section}
## [4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Method {#method .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S4.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Our method, Image2Sphere (I2S), is designed to predict object pose from
images under minimal assumptions. Much previous work assumes objects
with either no symmetry or known symmetry, and thus it is sufficient to
output a single point estimate of the pose. Our method, in contrast,
outputs a distribution over poses, which allows us to represent
uncertainty due to partial observability and the inherent ambiguity in
pose resulting from object symmetries. Recent work using distribution
learning has suffered from difficulty in training multi-modal
distributions and high data requirements. I2S circumvents these
challenges by reasoning about uncertainty in the Fourier basis of
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$, which is simple to train over and allows us
to leverage equivariant layers for better data efficiency.
:::

::: {#S4.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The I2S network consists of an encoder, a projection step, and a 2-layer
spherical pose predictor (see Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.2 Group convolution over Homogeneous Spaces ‣ 3 Background ‣ Image to Sphere: Learning Equivariant Features for Efficient Pose Prediction"){.ltx_ref}).
I2S generates ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ equivariant features from
traditional convolutional network encoders. Our method then maps
features from image space to the 2-sphere using an orthographic
projection operation. Then, we perform two spherical convolution
operations. Importantly, the output of the spherical convolution is a
signal over the Fourier basis of ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$, which can
be queried in the spatial domain to create highly expressive
distributions over the space of 3D rotations.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Mapping from Image to Sphere {#mapping-from-image-to-sphere .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
We use orthographic projection to map the output image feature map
$f:{{\mathbb{R}}^{2}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{h}}$ from the ResNet to a
spherical signal $\Psi:{S^{2}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{h}}$. Orthographic
projection $P:{S^{2}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{2}}$ defined
${P\operatorname{}{(x,y,z)}} = {(x,y)}$ maps a hemisphere onto the unit
disk. By positioning the feature map $f$ around the unit disk before
projecting, we get a localized signal
${\Psi\operatorname{}{(x)}} = {f\operatorname{}{({P\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}$
supported over one hemisphere. The advantage of this method is that it
preserves the spatial information of features in the original image.
Practically, to compute $\Psi$, a HEALPix grid is generated over a
hemisphere ${\{ x_{i}\}} \subset S^{2}$ and mapped to positions
$\{{P\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}}\}$ in the image feature map. The value of
$\Psi\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}$ is interpolated from the value of $f$ at
pixels near position $P\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}$. This process is
illustrated in Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 4.1 Mapping from Image to Sphere ‣ 4 Method ‣ Image to Sphere: Learning Equivariant Features for Efficient Pose Prediction"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
We then move $\Psi$ to the frequency domain using fast Fourier transform
to express $\Psi$ as a linear combination of spherical harmonics.
Operating in the frequency domain allows us to efficiently convolve
continuous signals over the sphere ($S^{2}$) and the group of 3D
rotations (${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$). We truncate the Fourier series
for $\Psi$ at frequency $L$ as
${\Psi\operatorname{}{(x)}} \approx {\sum_{l = 0}^{L}{\sum_{k = 0}^{{2\operatorname{}l} + 1}{c_{k}^{l}\operatorname{}Y_{k}^{l}\operatorname{}{(x)}}}}$.
This truncation may cause sampling error if the spatial signal has high
frequency. We mitigate this error by: (1) tapering the magnitude of the
projected features toward the edge of the image to avoid a discontinuity
on the 2-sphere, and (2) performing dropout of the HEALPix grid such
that a random subset of grid points is used for each projection.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Learning ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ Equivariant Features {#learning-sooperatorname3-equivariant-features .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Once the learned features are projected to the sphere, we use operations
that preserve the 3D symmetry of the representation. First, the
spherical signal $\Psi$ is processed with an $S^{2}$-equivariant group
convolution operation. Unlike traditional convolutional layers that use
locally supported filters, we elect to use a filter with global support.
Intuitively, we view this operation similarly to template matching where
the projected hemisphere signal $\Psi$ is the template and we are
looking for the rotation transformation that results in the best match
with the filter. Conveniently, using a globally supported filter gives a
global receptive field after one layer, allowing for a shallower
network. This is important since each spherical convolution operation is
fairly computation and memory intensive due to the high band limit $L$
necessary to model potentially complex distributions on the output.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
After the $S^{2}$-convolution, we perform one
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$-equivariant group convolution before
outputting the probability density. Using a locally supported filter,
this operation serves as a refinement step and results in slightly
higher prediction accuracy (see comparison in Appendix [[C.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#A3.SS2 "C.2 Effect of Additional SO⁢(3) Convolutions ‣ Appendix C Additional Experiments ‣ Image to Sphere: Learning Equivariant Features for Efficient Pose Prediction"){.ltx_ref}).
Following Spherical CNN (Cohen et al.,
[2018a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}),
we apply non-linearities between convolution layers by mapping the
signal to the spatial domain, applying a ReLU, and then mapping back to
the Fourier domain.
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Loss Function {#loss-function .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The output of the spherical convolution operation is a signal
$f:{{{SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}_{\geq 0}}$ that
indicates the probability that the image corresponds to a particular
object orientation. This output is queried using an equivolumetric grid
over ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ then normalized with a softmax operation
to produce a categorical distribution, which can be optimized using a
cross-entropy loss. We find that this loss is effective even when
training on images with ambiguous orientation or symmetric objects. For
these cases, the model learns to predict high probabilities for the set
of equivalently likely orientations. Additionally, we find that the
model can accurately predict likelihoods for grids of higher resolution
than it was trained on (e.g. train with a grid resolution of 5 degrees
and test with a resolution of 2.5 degrees).
:::
:::
:::

## [5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Experiments {#experiments .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S5.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Datasets {#datasets .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
To demonstrate the strengths of our method, we evaluate it on several
challenging object orientation estimation datasets. Additional details
can be found in Appendix [[B.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#A2.SS2 "B.2 Dataset Preparation ‣ Appendix B Implementation Details ‣ Image to Sphere: Learning Equivariant Features for Efficient Pose Prediction"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S5.SS1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The first dataset, [ModelNet10-SO(3)]{#S5.SS1.p2.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} (Liao et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}),
is composed of rendered images of synthetic, untextured objects from
ModelNet10 (Wu et al.,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib34){.ltx_ref}).
The dataset includes 4,899 object instances over 10 categories, with
novel camera viewpoints in the test set. Each image is labelled with a
single 3D rotation matrix, even though some categories, such as desks
and bathtubs, can have an ambiguous pose due to symmetry. For this
reason, the dataset presents a challenge to methods that cannot reason
about uncertainty over orientation.
:::

::: {#S5.SS1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Next, [PASCAL3D+]{#S5.SS1.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}, (Xiang
et al.,
[2014](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}),
is a popular benchmark for pose estimation that includes real images of
objects from 12 categories. The dataset labels symmetric object
categories in a consistent manner (e.g. bottles are symmetric about the
z-axis, so label zero rotation about the z-axis), which simplifies the
task. To improve accuracy, we follow the common practice of augmenting
the training data with synthetic renderings from Su et al.
([2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}).
Nevertheless, PASCAL3D+ still serves as a challenging benchmark due to
the high variability of natural textures and presence of novel instances
in the test set.
:::

::: {#S5.SS1.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Lastly, the [SYMSOL]{#S5.SS1.p4.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} dataset
was recently introduced by Murphy et al.
([2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref})
to evaluate the expressivity of methods that model distributions over 3D
rotations. It includes synthetic renderings of objects split into two
groups: geometric objects like the tetrahedron or cylinder with complex
symmetries (SYMSOL I), and simple objects with single identifying
feature such that the pose is ambiguous when the feature is occluded
(SYMSOL II). The dataset provides the full set of equivalent rotation
labels for each image so methods can be evaluated on how well they
capture the distribution. Note that Murphy et al.
([2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref})
generates results using 100k renderings per shape, which we report as a
baseline, but the publicly released dataset that we train on only
includes 50k renderings per shape.
:::
:::

::: {#S5.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Evaluation Metrics {#evaluation-metrics .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The goal of pose prediction is to minimize the angular error between the
predicted 3D rotation and the ground truth 3D rotation. Two commonly
used metrics are the median rotation error ([MedErr]{#S5.SS2.p1.3.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}) and the accuracy within a rotation error
threshold (e.g. [Acc@15]{#S5.SS2.p1.3.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} is
the fraction of predictions with 15 degrees or less rotation error).
However, these metrics assume that there exists a single, ground truth
3D rotation, which is not valid for symmetric objects or images with
pose ambiguity. For ModelNet10-SO(3) and PASCAL3D+, only a single
rotation is provided so these metrics must be used. However, when the
full set of equivalent rotation labels are provided, like with SYMSOL, a
more informative measure is the average log likelihood, computed as the
expected log likelihood that the model, $p_{\theta}$, assigns to
rotations sampled from the distribution of equivalent rotations
$p_{G\operatorname{}T}$:
${\mathbb{E}}_{R \sim p_{G\operatorname{}T}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{{\log p_{\theta}}\operatorname{}{(\left. R \middle| x \right.)}}\rbrack}$.
Achieving high log likelihood requires modelling all symmetries of an
object.
:::
:::

::: {#S5.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Network and Training Details {#network-and-training-details .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
I2S uses a residual network (He et al.,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref})
with weights pretrained on ImageNet (Deng et al.,
[2009](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref})
to extract dense feature maps from 2D images. We use a ResNet50 backbone
for ModelNet10-SO(3) and SYMSOL, and ResNet101 for PASCAL3D+. The
orthographic projection uses a HEALPix grid with recursion level of 2,
out of which 20 points are randomly selected during each forward pass.
We parameterize the learned $S^{2}$ filter in the Fourier domain, e.g.
learn weights for each spherical harmonic. The filter in the
${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ convolutional layer is locally supported over
rotations up to 22.5 degrees in magnitude. We query the signal in the
spatial domain using ${SO}\operatorname{}{(3)}$ HEALPix grids with 36k
points (7.5 degree spacing) during training and 2.4M (1.875 degree
spacing) during evaluation. We use the same maximum frequency ($L = 6$)
on all datasets. Additional details on the architecture can be found in
Appendix [[B.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#A2.SS1 "B.1 Architecture ‣ Appendix B Implementation Details ‣ Image to Sphere: Learning Equivariant Features for Efficient Pose Prediction"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S5.SS3.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
I2S is instantiated with PyTorch and the [e3nn]{#S5.SS3.p2.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_typewriter} library (Geiger et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref}).
It is trained using SGD with Nesterov momentum of 0.9 for 40 epochs
using a batch size of 64. The learning rate starts at 0.001 and decays
by factor of 0.1 every 15 epochs.
:::
:::

::: {#S5.SS4 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Baselines {#baselines .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS4.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
We compare our method against competitive baselines including regression
methods and distribution learning methods. Zhou et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref})
and Brégier
([2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref})
predict valid 3D rotation matrices using differentiable
orthonormalization processes, Gram-Schmidt and Procrustes, respectively,
that preclude discontinuities on the rotation manifold. Liao et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref})
and Mahendran et al.
([2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref})
use unique classification-regression losses to predict rotation.
Prokudin et al.
([2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref})
represents rotation uncertainty with a mixture of von Mises
distributions over each Euler angle, while Mohlin et al.
([2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref})
predicts the parameters for a matrix Fisher distribution. Gilitschenski
et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref})
and Deng et al.
([2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref})
both predict multi-modal Bingham distributions. Lastly, Murphy et al.
([2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.13926#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref})
trains an implicit model to generate a non-parametric distribution over
3D rotations. All baselines use the same sized, pretrained ResNet
encoders for each experiment. Results are from the original papers when
available.
