# A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning {#a-rotated-hyperbolic-wrapped-normal-distribution-for-hierarchical-representation-learning .ltx_title .ltx_title_document}

::: ltx_authors
[ [$\text{Seunghyuk Cho}^{1}$ &$\text{Juyong Lee}^{1}$
&$\text{Jaesik Park}^{1,2}$ &$\text{Dongwoo Kim}^{1,2}$
$\text{CSED POSTECH}^{1}$ &$\text{GSAI POSTECH}^{2}$
]{.ltx_personname}]{.ltx_creator .ltx_role_author}
:::

::: ltx_abstract
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

We present a rotated hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution (RoWN), a
simple yet effective alteration of a hyperbolic wrapped normal
distribution (HWN). The HWN expands the domain of probabilistic modeling
from Euclidean to hyperbolic space, where a tree can be embedded with
arbitrary low distortion in theory. In this work, we analyze the
geometric properties of the *diagonal* HWN, a standard choice of
distribution in probabilistic modeling. The analysis shows that the
distribution is inappropriate to represent the data points at the same
hierarchy level through their angular distance with the same norm in the
Poincaré disk model. We then empirically verify the presence of
limitations of HWN, and show how RoWN, the proposed distribution, can
alleviate the limitations on various hierarchical datasets, including
noisy synthetic binary tree, WordNet, and Atari 2600 Breakout. The code
is available at
[https://github.com/ml-postech/RoWN](https://github.com/ml-postech/RoWN){.ltx_ref
.ltx_url .ltx_font_typewriter target="_blank"}.
:::

::: {#S1 .section .ltx_section}
## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para}
Hyperbolic space has served as an effective medium to learn parsimonious
representations of hierarchical data, including vocabulary with
relationships (Nickel and Kiela,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref},
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref};
Tifrea et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}),
knowledge graphs (Chami et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref};
Sun et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}),
and social networks (Zhao et al.,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref};
Shavitt and Tankel,
[2008](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}).
Recent studies reveal that the underlying anatomy in much complex data
is non-Euclidean, supporting the success of representation learning in
hyperbolic space (Bronstein et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}).
However, due to the absence of well-defined distribution that is easy to
sample and has an analytic density function in hyperbolic space, earlier
studies have focused on the non-probabilistic learning framework.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para}
Hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution (HWN) has been recently proposed
as an alternative to the celebrated normal distribution in Euclidean
space. With an analytic density function and easiness of sampling, the
HWN expands the domain of probabilistic modeling from Euclidean to
hyperbolic space. The HWN has been successfully applied in various
probabilistic models, including VAE (Kingma and Welling,
[2014](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref})
and probabilistic word embeddings (Vilnis and McCallum,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}).
However, unlike the normal distribution in Euclidean space, the
geometric characteristics of the HWN have not been fully understood so
far. Therefore, figuring out what can be done or not with the HWN is
difficult.
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para}
In this work, we analyze the geometric properties of the *diagonal* HWN,
a standard choice of distribution in many probabilistic models (Mathieu
et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref};
Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
Based on the observation that the principal axes of the diagonal normal
distribution in Euclidean space have a parallel structure with the
standard bases, we also focus on the structure of the principal axes of
HWN in hyperbolic space to delve into a deeper understanding. Our
analysis shows that the principal axes of the diagonal HWN are locally
parallel to the standard bases in the Poincaré disk model.
:::

::: {#S1.p4 .ltx_para}
[[Figure 1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S2.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} (a) shows a common understanding on learned
representation of hierarchical data in the Poincaré disk. The
hierarchical structure spreads out like the spokes of a wheel. The local
variation in a hierarchy can then be represented as an angular
difference between nodes at the same level of the norm, i.e., the
principal axis representing local variation is orthogonal to the radial
axis. However, our analysis of the geometric property reveals that the
local variation can only be modeled along with the standard bases with
the diagonal HWN.
:::

::: {#S1.p5 .ltx_para}
To fix the structure of the principal axes in the diagonal HWN, we
propose a simple yet effective alteration of HWN, named a rotated
hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution (RoWN). By rotating the diagonal
covariance matrix before parallel transportation of HWN, we could
resolve the limitations in local variation structure while keeping the
valuable properties, such as easy sampling and tractable density of the
original HWN.
:::

::: {#S1.p6 .ltx_para}
We verify the representation learned with RoWN agrees with the common
characteristic of representation in hyperbolic space, which is barely
observable with the diagonal and the full covariance HWN, by using
*synthetic noisy binary tree* dataset. We demonstrate the usefulness of
RoWN on the benchmark datasets: WordNet and Atari 2600 breakout. We
summarize our contributions as follows:

1.  [[1.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i1}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    We provide an analysis of the geometric properties of HWN and its
    potential limitations in representation learning.
    :::
2.  [[2.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i2}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    We propose a novel and efficient method of using hyperbolic
    distribution, namely RoWN, and apply it to probabilistic models.
    :::
3.  [[3.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i3}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i3.p1 .ltx_para}
    We demonstrate the performance of RoWN through the comparison with
    the Euclidean normal distribution, diagonal covariance HWN, and full
    covariance HWN on one synthetic dataset and two benchmark datasets.
    :::
:::
:::

### [2.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}The Lorentz model {#the-lorentz-model .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S2.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Hyperbolic space is a non-Euclidean space, having a constant negative
Gaussian curvature. [[Figure 1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S2.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} illustrates two models for hyperbolic space,
among four standard equivalent models: (1) Klein model, (2) the Poincaré
disk model, (3) the Lorentz (hyperboloid) model, and (4) the Poincaré
half-plane model. In particular, the Lorentz model is famous for its
numerical stability in computing the distance and comes with a simpler
closed form of geodesics (Nickel and Kiela,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref}).
The Lorentz model ${\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ is the Riemannian manifold
consisting of the set of points ${\mathbf{z}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n + 1}$
satisfying
${\langle{\mathbf{z}},{\mathbf{z}}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}} = {- 1}$ and
$z_{0} > 0$, where the Lorentizan product
${\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle}_{\mathcal{L}}$ is defined as:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --
     $${{\langle{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{y}}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}}:={{- {x_{0}\operatorname{}y_{0}}} + {\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}{x_{i}\operatorname{}y_{i}}}}},$$   
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --

which also works as the metric tensor on hyperbolic space, i.e., the
metric tensor $g$ of the Lorentz model is
${g\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{x}})}} = {{diag}{\lbrack{- 1},1,\cdots,1\rbrack}}$
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [2.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Tangent space of the Lorentz model {#tangent-space-of-the-lorentz-model .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S2.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
We denote the tangent space of ${\mathbf{x}} \in {\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ as
$\mathcal{T}_{\mathbf{x}}\operatorname{}{\mathbb{L}}^{n}$, which is a
set of points satisfying the orthogonality relation with $\mathbf{x}$ in
terms of the Lorentzian product:
${{T_{\mathbf{x}}\operatorname{}{\mathbb{L}}^{n}}:=\left\{ {\mathbf{u}}:{{\langle{\mathbf{u}},{\mathbf{x}}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}} = 0} \right\}}.$
The metric tensor $g$ induces an inner product of two tangent vectors
from a tangent space. Geodesic
$\gamma:{{\lbrack 0,1\rbrack}\rightarrow{\mathbb{L}}^{n}}$ generalizes
straight lines in the Riemannian manifold which is the shortest curve
between two points. The exponential map
$\exp_{\mathbf{x}}:{{\mathcal{T}_{\mathbf{x}}\operatorname{}{\mathbb{L}}^{n}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{L}}^{n}}$
maps a tangent vector
${\mathbf{u}} \in {\mathcal{T}_{\mathbf{x}}\operatorname{}{\mathbb{L}}^{n}}$
onto ${\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ as:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\exp_{\mathbf{x}}{({\mathbf{u}})}}:={{{\cosh{({\|{\mathbf{u}}\|}_{\mathcal{L}})}}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{x}}} + {{\sinh{({\|{\mathbf{u}}\|}_{\mathcal{L}})}}\operatorname{}\frac{\mathbf{u}}{{\|{\mathbf{u}}\|}_{\mathcal{L}}}}}},$$      [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

such that
${{\exp_{\mathbf{x}}{({\mathbf{u}})}} = {\mathbf{y}}},{{{\gamma\operatorname{}{(0)}} = {\mathbf{x}}},{{\gamma\operatorname{}{(1)}} = {\mathbf{y}}}}$.
The log map, inverse of the exponential map, is defined as
${\log_{\mathbf{x}}{({\mathbf{y}})}}:={\exp_{\mathbf{x}}^{- 1}{({\mathbf{u}})}}$.
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p2 .ltx_para}
Parallel transport is an operation that transports a tangent vector in
the tangent space at $\mathbf{x}$ to another vector in the tangent space
at $\mathbf{y}$ along the geodesic from $\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathbf{y}$
without losing the parallel property. The parallel transport in the
Lorentz model is given by:

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{PT}_{{\mathbf{x}}\rightarrow{\mathbf{y}}}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}:={{\mathbf{v}} + {\frac{{\langle{{\mathbf{y}} - {\alpha\operatorname{}{\mathbf{x}}}},{\mathbf{v}}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}}}{\alpha + 1}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{x}} + {\mathbf{y}}})}}}},$$      [(2)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where
$\alpha = {- {\langle{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{y}}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}}}$.
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [2.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution {#hyperbolic-wrapped-normal-distribution .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S2.SS3.p1 .ltx_para}
One of the key challenges in adopting hyperbolic space to probabilistic
models is finding a distribution on hyperbolic space that is easy to
sample and has a closed-form density function. The two most common
distributions of hyperbolic space used in previous work are Riemannian
normal distribution (Mathieu et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref};
Said et al.,
[2014](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref})
and hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution (HWN) (Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
Our work is mainly based on the HWN because sampling in Riemannian
normal distribution is limited with only a unit variance.
:::

::: {#S2.SS3.p2 .ltx_para}
The sampling process with the HWN follows:

1.  [[1.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S2.I1.i1}

    ::: {#S2.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    Sample ${\mathbf{v}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$ from a Gaussian
    distribution $\mathcal{N}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{0},\Sigma)}$ in
    Euclidean space.
    :::
2.  [[2.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S2.I1.i2}

    ::: {#S2.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    Parallel transport the vector
    ${\lbrack 0,{\mathbf{v}}\rbrack} \in {\mathcal{T}_{\mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}}}\operatorname{}{\mathbb{L}}^{n}}$
    to the tangent space.
    :::
3.  [[3.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S2.I1.i3}

    ::: {#S2.I1.i3.p1 .ltx_para}
    Project the transported tangent vector to ${\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ using
    exponential mapping.
    :::

The density of a sample can be measured via the change of variable
method. For convenience, we denote the procedures 2 and 3 as a single
operation:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}:={\exp_{\mathbf{μ}}{({{PT}_{\mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}}\rightarrow{\mathbf{μ}}}\operatorname{}{({\lbrack 0,{\mathbf{v}}\rbrack})}})}}},$$      [(3)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

with given ${\mathbf{μ}} \in {\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ and
${\mathbf{v}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$, and
$\mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}} = {\lbrack 1,\ldots,0\rbrack}$ stands for the
origin of ${\mathbb{L}}^{n}$.
:::
:::
:::

## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution {#rotated-hyperbolic-wrapped-normal-distribution .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.p1 .ltx_para}
This section introduces several observations on the geometric properties
of the HWN distribution transformation from the Euclidean space to
hyperbolic space. We show the limitations of the diagonal HWN for
representation learning of hierarchical data. Then, we propose a simple
yet effective modification of the HWN, called a rotated hyperbolic
wrapped normal distribution.
:::

::: {#S3.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
First, we investigate the changes in the normal distribution during the
transformation from Euclidean to hyperbolic space by
[[Equation 3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S2.E3 "3 ‣ 2.3 Hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. As the principal axes characterize the
covariance structure of the normal distribution in the Euclidean space,
we investigate how the principal axes are transformed in hyperbolic
space. Before deriving our main proposition, we first show that the
straight lines that pass through the origin in the Euclidean space are
transformed to the geodesics in hyperbolic space by
[[Equation 3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S2.E3 "3 ‣ 2.3 Hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}.
:::

::: {#Thmproposition1 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_proposition}
###### [[Proposition 1]{#Thmproposition1.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[.]{#Thmproposition1.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#proposition-1. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmproposition1.p1 .ltx_para}
[Suppose
${\ell_{\mathbf{s}}\operatorname{}{(t)}} = {t\operatorname{}\mathbf{s}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$
be a line passing through the origin, where
$\mathbf{s} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$ is a directional vector. Then the
curve
$f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{({\ell_{\mathbf{s}}\operatorname{}{(t)}})}$
in the Lorentz model ${\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ becomes a
geodesic.]{#Thmproposition1.p1.4.4 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS1.p2 .ltx_para}
The proof of the proposition is provided in [[Appendix A]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A1 "Appendix A Proof of Proposition 1 ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. The proposition indicates that every straight
line that passes through the origin including the principal axes, is
transformed into a geodesic in hyperbolic space.
:::

::: {#S3.SS1.p3 .ltx_para}
Based on the first proposition, we provide our main proposition, which
fully characterizes the structure of principal axes when projected to
the Poincaré disk model:
:::

::: {#Thmproposition2 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_proposition}
###### [[Proposition 2]{#Thmproposition2.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[.]{#Thmproposition2.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#proposition-2. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmproposition2.p1 .ltx_para}
[Define ${Proj}{(\mathbf{u})}$ to be the projection function from the
Lorentz model to the Poincaré model, i.e.,
${{{Proj}{(\mathbf{u})}} = \frac{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{u})}}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{u})}} + 1}},{{\forall\mathbf{u}} \in {\mathbb{L}}^{n}}$.
If $\ell_{\mathbf{s}}$ is a principal axis of the normal distribution
defined in ${\mathbb{R}}^{n}$ and $\mathbf{μ}$ the mean of HWN in
${\mathbb{L}}^{n}$, then $\mathbf{s}$ is the tangent vector of
${Proj}{({f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{(\ell_{\mathbf{s}})}})}$ on
${Proj}{({\mathbf{μ}})}$.]{#Thmproposition2.p1.9.9 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS1.p4 .ltx_para}
The proof of the proposition is provided in [[Appendix B]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A2 "Appendix B Proof of Proposition 2 ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. The proposition reveals that the principal axes
of the HWN are locally parallel to the standard bases in the Poincaré
disk model. To visualize the proposition, we plot the contour line and
the principal axes of the two-dimensional diagonal normal distribution
before and after the transformation in [[Figure 2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3.1 Observations on the hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. We observe that the tangent lines of the
transformed principal axes are parallel to the standard bases in
hyperbolic space.
:::

::: {#S3.SS1.p5 .ltx_para}
When a popular diagonal normal distribution is employed as a variational
distribution, the locally parallel principal axes might be problematic
in learning hierarchical representations. For example, suppose one tries
to represent the variability along the radial direction or in angular
differences. In the case, both the major (red) and minor (blue) axes in
[[1(b)]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S3.F1.sf2 "1(b) ‣ Figure 2 ‣ 3.1 Observations on the hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} cannot model the variability properly.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Rotated hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution {#rotated-hyperbolic-wrapped-normal-distribution-1 .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
Based on the observation, we propose a simple yet effective alternative
to the diagonal HWN, a rotated hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution
(RoWN). Rotating the covariance matrix to the direction of $\mathbf{μ}$
enables aligning the major axis of the normal distribution in the
Euclidean space to the radial axis in hyperbolic space as visualized in
the [[Figure 2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3.1 Observations on the hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}.
:::

${\mathbf{μ}} \in {\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ and a diagonal covariance matrix

normal distribution as follows:

1.  [[1.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S3.I1.i1}

    ::: {#S3.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    Compute the rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}$ that rotates the x-axis
    (${\lbrack{\pm 1},\ldots,0\rbrack} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$) to
    ${\mathbf{μ}}_{1:}$.
    :::
2.  [[2.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S3.I1.i2}

    ::: {#S3.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    Substitute the covariance matrix of Gaussian normal with
    ${\mathbf{R}}\operatorname{}\Sigma\operatorname{}{\mathbf{R}}^{T}$.
    :::

Thus, the rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}$, which rotates a unit vector from
$\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathbf{y}$, can be computed as:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathbf{R}} = {{\mathbf{I}} + {({{{\mathbf{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{x}}} - {{\mathbf{x}}^{T}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{y}}}})} + {\frac{1}{1 + {\langle{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{y}}\rangle}}\operatorname{}{({{{\mathbf{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{x}}} - {{\mathbf{x}}^{T}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{y}}}})}^{2}}}}.$$      [(4)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.p3 .ltx_para}
The pseudo-code of the sampling process of RoWN is in
[[Algorithm 1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#alg1 "Algorithm 1 ‣ 3.2 Rotated hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. Note that the construction is straightforward
but still keeps the following benefits of the HWN: 1) The sampling can
be done efficiently, and 2) the computation of the probability density
of the samples is tractable. As the HWN provides a tractable probability
density function for any kind of covariance matrix, we can easily
compute the probability density of a given sample from RoWN. See
Appendix [[C]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A3 "Appendix C Details of Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref}
for more details.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.p4 .ltx_para}

distributions in [[Figure 3]{.ltx_text

.ltx_refmacro_autoref}.

### [4.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Applications of the hyperbolic distribution {#applications-of-the-hyperbolic-distribution .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Hyperbolic VAE. {#hyperbolic-vae. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
The hyperbolic VAE, whose latent space is hyperbolic space, has been
shown to be efficient for capturing the hierarchical structure of the
data (Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
Mathieu et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}).
The evidence lower bound of the hyperbolic VAE can be written as:

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --
     $${{\mathcal{L}_{ELBO}\operatorname{}{(\theta,\phi)}}:={{{\mathbb{E}}_{{q_{\phi}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{z}} \mid {\mathbf{x}}})}} \cdot \sqrt{\det{(g)}}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{{\log p_{\theta}}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{x}} \mid {\mathbf{z}}})}}\rbrack}} - {D_{KL}\operatorname{}\left( {{{q_{\phi}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{x}} \mid {\mathbf{z}}})}} \cdot \sqrt{\det{(g)}}} \parallel {{p\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{z}})}} \cdot \sqrt{\det{(g)}}}} \right)}}},$$   
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --

where $q_{\phi}$ is the encoder, $p_{\theta}$ is the decoder, $g$ is the
metric tensor of the chosen model of hyperbolic space, and
$p\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{z}})}$ is a prior distribution. The
distributions defined on hyperbolic spaces, such as HWN and RoWN, are
used to define encoder
${q_{\phi}\operatorname{}{({{\mathbf{z}} \mid {\mathbf{x}}})}} \cdot \sqrt{\det{(g)}}$
and prior ${p\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{z}})}} \cdot \sqrt{\det{(g)}}$.
In hyperbolic VAEs, due to the absence of the closed-form KL divergence
in the hyperbolic distributions, the KL divergence between the encoder
and the prior is usually approximated with Monte-Carlo sampling (Nagano
et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
Mathieu et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}).
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Probabilistic hyperbolic word embedding model. {#probabilistic-hyperbolic-word-embedding-model. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
The probabilistic word embedding models aim to learn probabilistic
representations of words (Vilnis and McCallum,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref};
Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
Tifrea et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}).
The embeddings learned on hyperbolic spaces have shown better
performance than the Euclidean counterpart (Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
Tifrea et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}).
Given the hypernymy relationships between the words, the probabilistic
hyperbolic embedding model learns the probabilistic representation of
words by minimizing the following objective:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathcal{L}_{word}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{θ}})}}:={{\mathbb{E}}_{({{s \sim t},{s\operatorname{\sim\not{}}t^{\prime}}})}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\max{(0,{{m + {D_{KL}\operatorname{}{({q_{s} \parallel q_{t}})}}} - {D_{KL}\operatorname{}{({q_{s} \parallel q_{t^{\prime}}})}}})}}\rbrack}}},$$      [(5)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $m$ is a margin, $q_{i}$ is a distribution for word $i$
parameterized by $\theta$, and $s \sim t$ and
$s\operatorname{\sim\not{}}t$ denote the presence and absence of
hypernymy relation between word pair $s$ and $t$, respectively.
:::
:::
:::

We train a probabilistic word embedding model with WordNet
dataset (Fellbaum,

which consists of 82,115 nouns and 743,241 hypernymy relationships. We
have initialized the embeddings from
$\mathcal{N}\operatorname{}{(0,{0.01\operatorname{}I})}$, which are then
moved to the Lorentz model using the exponential map. We use the
learning rate warm-up proposed in (Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
We evaluate the learned representations by computing the average rank of
all the hypernymies. The rank of a given pair of words $s$ and $t$ is
computed among the distances between all possible pairs of the words $s$
and $t^{\prime}$ without hypernymy. [[Table 2]{.ltx_text

.ltx_refmacro_autoref} shows the empirical performances of representing
the word data. We report the performance with the mean rank (MR) and the
mean average precision (mAP). RoWN preserves the hierarchical structure
better than the other distributions, while the full covariance HWN often
performs worse than RoWN.
:::

::: {#S4.SS3.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Failure of the full covariance HWN. {#failure-of-the-full-covariance-hwn. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS3.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
In theory, the full covariance HWN needs to have at least a similar
performance to RoWN since the RoWN is the particular case of the full
covariance. The performance of the full covariance HWN often performs
worse with the probabilistic hyperbolic word embedding models than the
hyperbolic VAEs. We speculate that reason is because of the relatively
simple prior in the hyperbolic VAE, whereas the probabilistic word
embedding models need to compute the KL divergence between the full
covariance HWNs. Our additional experiments reported in
[[Table 11]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A4.T11 "Table 11 ‣ Optimization issue in training full covariance HWN. ‣ D.3 WordNet ‣ Appendix D Experimental Details ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} confirm our speculation as the number of training
samples for the KL divergence increases, the performance increases
slightly.
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS3.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Discussion of the root placement. {#discussion-of-the-root-placement. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS3.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}

placed anywhere in hyperbolic space. In other words, infinitely many
sets of embeddings preserve the same pairwise distances between the
nodes. To place the root near the origin, we initialize all embeddings
with the zero mean distribution as done in (Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
We find that this initialization helps the root placed near the origin.
A study on the effects of the initialization methods is shown in
[[Table 3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S4.T3 "Table 3 ‣ Discussion of the root placement. ‣ 4.3 WordNet ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref

.ltx_refmacro_autoref}.

### [4.4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Atari 2600 Breakout {#atari-2600-breakout .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

<figure id="S4.T4" class="ltx_table">
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_table"><span id="S4.T4.21.1.1"
class="ltx_text" style="font-size:90%;">Table 4</span>: </span><span
id="S4.T4.22.2" class="ltx_text" style="font-size:90%;">Results of <span
id="S4.T4.22.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">Atari 2600
Breakout</span>. The results are averaged over 5 runs. We measure the
correlation between the score of an image and the Poincaré norm of the
variational mean. The HWN with the isotropic covariance can be viewed as
a variant of RoWN.</span></figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S4.SS4.p1 .ltx_para}
Trajectories of some Atari 2600 games can be structured as a tree-like
hierarchy along the time horizon as Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref})
points out. For example, given Atari 2600 Breakout, the root node can be
the starting state of the game where no blocks have been broken. As the
game progresses the states of the blocks form a hierarchical structure
depending on which blocks have been broken. To learn the implicit
hierarchy that can be observed from the trajectories of Breakout, we
train the VAE models with the Atari 2600 Breakout images.
:::

::: {#S4.SS4.p2 .ltx_para}
The images of Breakout are collected by using a pre-trained Deep
Q-network (Mnih et al.,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref})
and divided into training set and test set with 90,000 and 10,000 images
respectively. We label each image with the score obtained from the game
environment. So the labels are correlated to the number of broken
blocks. To train VAE, we use a DCGAN-based architecture, which was
originally used to evaluate HWN in Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
The detailed architecture is provided in Appendix [[D.4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A4.SS4.SSS0.Px1 "Experimental setting. ‣ D.4 Atari 2600 Breakout ‣ Appendix D Experimental Details ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref}.
To evaluate the models, we measure the correlation between the Poincaré
norm of the test images and the labeled scores. The evaluation results
and the generated images from the trained models are reported in
[[Table 4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S4.T4 "Table 4 ‣ 4.4 Atari 2600 Breakout ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} and [[Figure 5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S4.F5 "Figure 5 ‣ 4.4 Atari 2600 Breakout ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}, respectively.
:::

::: {#S4.SS4.p3 .ltx_para}
In learning Breakout images, RoWN and the full covariance HWN outperform
the diagonal HWN. The isotropic HWN, where the covariance matrix is
invariant to any rotation matrix, shows a better correlation than the
others in all the settings. However, as reported in
[[Table 14]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A4.T14 "Table 14 ‣ Results. ‣ D.4 Atari 2600 Breakout ‣ Appendix D Experimental Details ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}, the test ELBO values are relatively worse than
the others. While the isotropic HWN shows a high correlation but
relatively lower test ELBO, RoWN shows competitive test ELBO to the
others and well aligns the hierarchical structures with respect to the
norm in low latent dimensions.
:::

## [5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Related work {#related-work .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Hyperbolic space for hierarchical representation learning. {#hyperbolic-space-for-hierarchical-representation-learning. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Earlier studies on the hierarchical representation learning have focused
on modeling explicit hierarchical structures through the Bayesian
non-parametrics (Griffiths et al.,
[2003](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref};
Larsen et al.,
[2002](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref};
Salakhutdinov et al.,
[2012](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref};
Teh et al.,
[2007](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref};
Ghahramani et al.,
[2010](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref};
Heller and Ghahramani,
[2005](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref})
or embedding the hierarchical structure into Euclidean space (Nickel
et al.,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib20){.ltx_ref};
Grover and Leskovec,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref};
Nguyen et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref}).
Euclidean space is later shown to require an excessive number of
dimensions to embed the original hierarchical structure without any
distortion (Linial et al.,
[1994](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p2 .ltx_para}
Hyperbolic space has been proposed as an alternative medium to embed the
hierarchical data. Theoretically, any tree-structured data can be
embedded in hyperbolic space with arbitrarily low distortion (Sarkar,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref}).
Based on the theory, learning representation of hierarchical structure
in hyperbolic space has been shown great success in various datasets
including WordNet hierarchy, graph-structured data, and social network
data (Nickel and Kiela,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref},
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref};
Chami et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref};
Sun et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref};
Zhao et al.,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref};
Shavitt and Tankel,
[2008](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}).
However, these studies are only limited to modeling the explicit
hierarchy, which means that the dataset contains an explicit relation
between data points. Furthermore, the learning frameworks are limited in
the non-probabilistic setting because of the absence of well-behaved
distribution in hyperbolic space.
:::
:::

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Distributions in hyperbolic space. {#distributions-in-hyperbolic-space. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
The probabilistic model enables measuring the uncertainty and provides a
principled way of learning. To extend the probabilistic learning
framework from Euclidean to hyperbolic space or Riemannian manifold in
general, one needs to define a well-behaved distribution that has a
tractable density and is easy to sample from. Recently, a few studies
have proposed probabilistic learning schemes in hyperbolic
space (Mathieu et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref};
Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
Mathieu et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref})
introduces parametrizable sampling schemes for the two canonical
Gaussian generalizations defined on the Poincaré disk model. The scheme
is used to train a hyperbolic VAE and show an improved generalization
performance with high interpretability. Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref})
suggests a method of integrating the Bayesian framework with hyperbolic
space in the Lorentz model where the simpler closed form of geodesics is
defined. Normalizing flow (Rezende and Mohamed,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref})
can be also used to define the hyperbolic distribution in the
probabilistic learning framework. Bose et al.
([2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref})
propose two normalizing flows defined on hyperbolic space, which show
improvements in learning hierarchical structures in graph data. Mathieu
and Nickel
([2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref})
elevate the continuous normalizing flow (Chen et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref})
defined on the Euclidean space to arbitrary Riemannian manifold,
including the hyperbolic space. Based on Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}),
we analyze the geometric properties of the distribution lying on the
Lorentz model and show the limitations of the existing method.
:::
:::
:::

::: {#S6 .section .ltx_section}
## [6 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Limitations {#limitations .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S6.p1 .ltx_para}
We explore a better method of representing hierarchical data in
hyperbolic space. To this end, we propose a simple yet effective
alternative of hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution. However, the
proposed distribution is limited only to hyperbolic space, and no
generalization method for Riemannian space is studied yet. To explore
the usefulness of alternative Riemannian spaces, finding a common
distribution that can work well in any Riemannian space will be
necessary.
:::

::: {#S6.p2 .ltx_para}
RoWN is a subset of the full covariance HWN. However, in many cases,
RoWN outperforms the full covariance HWN in our experiments. In general,
optimizing the covariance matrix requires learning the quadratic number
of parameters with respect to the dimensionality. We conjecture the
hardness of optimization leads to the poor performance of the full
covariance HWN. To overcome this limitation, a search for a better
optimization algorithm in hyperbolic space needs to be explored.
:::
:::

::: {#S7 .section .ltx_section}
## [7 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Conclusions {#conclusions .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S7.p1 .ltx_para}
In this work, we propose a novel method of using RoWN for representing
the data with a hierarchical structure. With an in-depth analysis of the
geometric properties of HWN, we demonstrate why the common choice of the
diagonal covariance matrix for HWN may be inappropriate but the rotated
covariance matrix. Our empirical results present that RoWN exhibits
better representation ability, both qualitatively and quantitatively,
compared to all the baselines: Euclidean normal distribution, diagonal
HWN, and full covariance HWN. We hope that our method helps better
understanding the anatomy of hyperbolic space and be a promising
technique for efficient representation learning.
:::

## [Appendix A ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Proof of Proposition 1 {#appendix-a-proof-of-proposition-1 .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A1.p1 .ltx_para}
We prove the first proposition in [[Section 3.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S3.SS1 "3.1 Observations on the hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} in this section. Through the proposition, we
first show that straight lines that pass through the origin in the
Euclidean space are transformed into the geodesics in hyperbolic space
by [[Equation 3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S2.E3 "3 ‣ 2.3 Hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}.
:::

::: {#A1.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Proposition 1. {#proposition-1.-1 .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#A1.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Suppose
${\ell_{\mathbf{s}}\operatorname{}{(t)}} = {t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$
be a line passing through the origin, where
${\mathbf{s}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$ is a directional vector. Then the
curve
$f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{({\ell_{\mathbf{s}}\operatorname{}{(t)}})}$
in the Lorentz model ${\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ becomes a geodesic.
:::

::: {#A1.SS0.SSS0.Px1.1 .ltx_proof}
###### Proof. {#proof. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_font_italic .ltx_title_proof}

::: {#A1.SS0.SSS0.Px1.1.p1 .ltx_para}
Let
${\mathbf{u}}:={\lbrack{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{u}})}},{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{u}})}}\rbrack} \in {\mathbb{L}}^{n}$
be given, where $x_{0}:{{\mathbb{L}}^{n}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}}$ and
$x_{1:}:{{\mathbb{L}}^{n}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{n}}$ denotes the
projections, i.e., ${x_{i}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{u}})}} = u_{i}$ .
Then,

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --
     ${PT}_{\mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}}\rightarrow{\mathbf{μ}}}\operatorname{}{({\lbrack 0,{t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}}\rbrack})}$   $= {{\lbrack 0,{t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}}\rbrack} + {\frac{1}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1}\operatorname{}{\langle{{\mathbf{μ}} - {{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} \cdot \mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}}}},{\lbrack 0,{t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}}\rbrack}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}} + {\mathbf{μ}}})}}}$                            
                                                                                                                                    $= {{\lbrack 0,{t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}}\rbrack} + {\frac{1}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1}\operatorname{}{\langle{\lbrack 0,{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}}\rbrack},{\lbrack 0,{t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}}\rbrack}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1},{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}}\rbrack}}}$   
                                                                                                                                    $= {{\lbrack 0,{t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}}\rbrack} + {\frac{1}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1}\operatorname{}{\langle{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}},{t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}}\rangle}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1},{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}}\rbrack}}}$                                                       
                                                                                                                                    ${= {t\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\langle{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}},{\mathbf{s}}\rangle},{{\mathbf{s}} + {{\langle{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}},{\mathbf{s}}\rangle}\operatorname{}\frac{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1}}} \right\rbrack}}.$                                                                        
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --

Now, let
${\mathbf{v}}:=\left\lbrack {\langle{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}},{\mathbf{s}}\rangle},{{\mathbf{s}} + {{\langle{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}},{\mathbf{s}}\rangle}\operatorname{}\frac{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1}}} \right\rbrack$
and $c:=\sqrt{{\langle{\mathbf{v}},{\mathbf{v}}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}}}$.
Then,

  -- --------------------------------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --
     $f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{(l_{\mathbf{s}})}$   $= {\exp_{\mathbf{μ}}{({{PT}_{\mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}}\rightarrow{\mathbf{μ}}}\operatorname{}{({(0,{t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{s}}})})}})}}$   
                                                         $= {\exp_{\mathbf{μ}}{({t\operatorname{}{\mathbf{v}}})}}$                                                                                    
                                                         ${= {{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{μ}}} + {{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}\frac{\mathbf{v}}{c}}}}.$   
  -- --------------------------------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --

Recall that the geodesic of the Lorentz model is
${{\cosh{(t)}}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{x}}} + {{\sinh{(t)}}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{y}}}$
where
${{\mathbf{x}} \in {\mathbb{L}}^{n}},{{\langle{\mathbf{y}},{\mathbf{y}}\rangle}_{\mathcal{L}} = 1}$
and
${\mathbf{y}} \in {\mathcal{T}_{\mathbf{x}}\operatorname{}{\mathbb{L}}^{n}}$ (Robbin
and Salamon,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}).
∎
:::
:::

::: {#A1.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p2 .ltx_para}
The proposition indicates that every straight line that passes through
the origin including the principal axes, is transformed into a geodesic
in hyperbolic space.

## [Appendix B ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Proof of Proposition 2 {#appendix-b-proof-of-proposition-2 .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A2.p1 .ltx_para}
We prove the second proposition in [[Section 3.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S3.SS1 "3.1 Observations on the hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} to show the geometrical properties of HWN. Based
on the first proposition, we provide our main proposition, which fully
characterizes the structure of principal axes when projected to the
Poincaré disk model:
:::

::: {#A2.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Proposition 2. {#proposition-2.-1 .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#A2.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Let ${Proj}{({\mathbf{u}})}$ be the projection function from Lorentz
model to Poincaré model, i.e.,
${{{Proj}{({\mathbf{u}})}} = \frac{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{u}})}}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{u}})}} + 1}},{{\forall{\mathbf{u}}} \in {\mathbb{L}}^{n}}$.
Let $\ell_{\mathbf{s}}$ be a principal axis of the normal distribution
defined in ${\mathbb{R}}^{n}$ and $\mathbf{μ}$ the mean of HWN in
${\mathbb{L}}^{n}$, then $\mathbf{s}$ is the tangent vector of
${Proj}{({f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{(\ell_{\mathbf{s}})}})}$ on
${Proj}{({\mathbf{μ}})}$.
:::

::: {#A2.SS0.SSS0.Px1.1 .ltx_proof}
###### Proof. {#proof.-1 .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_font_italic .ltx_title_proof}

::: {#A2.SS0.SSS0.Px1.1.p1 .ltx_para}
First, we project the transformed principal axis to the Poincaré disk
model as:

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --
     ${Proj}\operatorname{}\left( {f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{(l_{\mathbf{s}})}} \right)$   $= {{Proj}\operatorname{}\left( {{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{μ}}} + {{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}\frac{\mathbf{v}}{c}}} \right)}$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        
                                                                                               $= {{Proj}\operatorname{}\left( \left\lbrack {{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}\frac{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}{c}}},{{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}\frac{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}{c}}} \right\rbrack \right)}$   
                                                                                               ${= \frac{{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}/c}}{{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}/c} + 1}}.$                                                                     
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --

Then, the derivative of the projected curve with respect to $t$ is
derived as:

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --
     $\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\operatorname{}{Proj}\operatorname{}\left( {f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{(l_{\mathbf{s}})}} \right)$   $= {\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\operatorname{}\frac{{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}/c}}{{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}/c} + 1}}$                                                                                                                                                                                                   
                                                                                                                                         $= \frac{{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}\operatorname{}c} + {{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}}{{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}/c} + 1}$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   
                                                                                                                                         ${- \frac{{({{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}\operatorname{}c} + {{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}})}\operatorname{}{({{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}/c}})}}{\left( {{{\cosh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + {{{\sinh{({c\operatorname{}t})}}\operatorname{}x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}/c} + 1} \right)^{2}}}.$   
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --

As ${Proj}{({\mathbf{μ}})}$ is the point of the curve at $t = 0$, the
tangent vector of the curve on ${Proj}{({\mathbf{μ}})}$ can be computed
by substituting $t = 0$:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --
     $\left. {\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\operatorname{}{Proj}\operatorname{}\left( {f_{\mathbf{μ}}\operatorname{}{(l_{\mathbf{s}})}} \right)} \right|_{t = 0}$   $= {\frac{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1} - \frac{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{v}})}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}}{{({{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1})}^{2}}}$                                                                                                                                                                                                  
                                                                                                                                                                  $= {{\frac{\mathbf{s}}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1} + \frac{{\langle{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}},{\mathbf{s}}\rangle}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}}{{({{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1})}^{2}}} - \frac{{\langle{x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}},{\mathbf{s}}\rangle}\operatorname{}x_{1:}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}}{{({{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1})}^{2}}}$   
                                                                                                                                                                  $= \frac{\mathbf{s}}{{x_{0}\operatorname{}{({\mathbf{μ}})}} + 1}$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       
                                                                                                                                                                  ${\propto {\mathbf{s}}}.$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --

∎
:::
:::

::: {#A2.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p2 .ltx_para}
The proposition reveals that the principal axes of the HWN are locally
parallel to the standard bases in the Poincaré disk model. To visualize
the proposition, we plot the contour line and the principal axes of the
two-dimensional diagonal covariance normal distribution before and after
the transformation in [[Figure 2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3.1 Observations on the hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. We observe that the tangent lines of the
transformed principal axes are parallel to the standard bases in
hyperbolic space.

## [Appendix C ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Details of Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution {#appendix-c-details-of-rotated-hyperbolic-wrapped-normal-distribution .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A3.p1 .ltx_para}
The details of RoWN, i.e. sampling and the probability density
computation, are described in this section. We start with a mean vector
${\mathbf{μ}} \in {\mathbb{L}}^{n}$ and a diagonal covariance matrix
$\Sigma$ as in the standard HWN. Based on the mean vector, we construct
RoWN by rotating the covariance matrix as follows:

1.  [[1.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#A3.I1.i1}

    ::: {#A3.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    Compute the rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}$ that rotates the x-axis
    (${\lbrack{\pm 1},\ldots,0\rbrack} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}$) to
    ${\mathbf{μ}}_{1:}$.
    :::
2.  [[2.]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#A3.I1.i2}

    ::: {#A3.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    Substitute the covariance matrix of Gaussian normal with
    ${\mathbf{R}}\operatorname{}\Sigma\operatorname{}{\mathbf{R}}^{T}$.
    :::

Thus, the rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}$, which rotates a unit vector from
$\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathbf{y}$, can be computed as:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathbf{R}} = {{\mathbf{I}} + {({{{\mathbf{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{x}}} - {{\mathbf{x}}^{T}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{y}}}})} + {\frac{1}{1 + {\langle{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{y}}\rangle}}\operatorname{}{({{{\mathbf{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{x}}} - {{\mathbf{x}}^{T}\operatorname{}{\mathbf{y}}}})}^{2}}}}.$$      [(6)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

Algorithm [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#alg2 "Algorithm 2 ‣ Appendix C Details of Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref}
shows the entire algorithm of constructing RoWN. Note that the
construction is straightforward but still keeps the following benefits
of the HWN: 1) The sampling can be done efficiently, and 2) the
computation of the probability density of the samples is tractable. As
the HWN provides a tractable probability density function for any kind
of covariance matrix, we can easily compute the probability density of a
given sample from RoWN. For the details of sampling and probability
density computation, see Algorithm [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#alg3 "Algorithm 3 ‣ Appendix C Details of Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref}
& [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#alg4 "Algorithm 4 ‣ Appendix C Details of Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref}.

## [Appendix D ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Experimental Details {#appendix-d-experimental-details .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A4.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we provide the details of the experiments in Section
[[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S4 "4 Experiments ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref}.

::: {#A4.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [D.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Baselines {#d.1-baselines .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
For all the experiments, we compare the performance of RoWN with four
baselines: the normal distribution in the Euclidean space, the isotropic
HWN (Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}),
the diagonal HWN, and the full covariance HWN. In the process of
constructing the distributions for each application, i.e. the
variational distribution of VAE and the embedding distribution of
probabilistic word embedding, the distributions except the full
covariance HWN have a diagonal matrix as an input, and then the softplus
operation is used to make it positive. The full covariance HWN has a 2D
matrix $\Sigma \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n \times n}$ as an input and constructs
a covariance matrix as
${\Sigma\operatorname{}\Sigma^{T}} + {\epsilon\operatorname{}{\mathbf{I}}}$,
to match the positive-definite property, where $\epsilon$ is set to
${1\operatorname{}e} - 9$ in our experiments. For the mean value, we
concatenate zero at the first dimension and transport it to the Lorentz
model using $\exp_{\mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}}}$. For the hyperbolic VAE
models, we use $\log_{\mathbf{0}_{\mathcal{L}}}$ to transform the input
of the decoder to the Euclidean space, as suggested in Mathieu et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}).
:::
:::

### [D.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Noisy synthetic binary tree {#d.2-noisy-synthetic-binary-tree .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS2.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Experimental setting. {#experimental-setting. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#A4.SS2.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
A synthetic binary tree dataset is first used to show the performance of
representing hierarchy in Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}),
where each node in a tree corresponds to a sequence of binary values.
[[3(a)]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S4.F3.sf1 "3(a) ‣ Figure 4 ‣ 4.2 Noisy synthetic binary tree ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} shows an example of the depth three binary tree,
where a parent and child only differ in one digit. We add spherical
noises to the nodes in the same level of hierarchy as described in
[[3(a)]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S4.F3.sf1 "3(a) ‣ Figure 4 ‣ 4.2 Noisy synthetic binary tree ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} as the noisy samples. With the data points with
additional noises, we can create a dataset containing a local-level
variation in the hierarchy. For the experiments, we uniformly sample the
spherical noise from $\lbrack 0,{\pi/4}\rbrack$. We train hyperbolic VAE
on [noisy synthetic binary tree]{#A4.SS2.SSS0.Px1.p1.5.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} with varying depth from 4 to 7. For each depth $d$, we
use a three-layer fully connected neural network as the architecture
where the number of hidden units is $2^{d + 3}$ and the latent dimension
is $d$. We use ReLU as the activation function for each layer except the
last layer of the encoder and decoder. The overall architecture is shown
in [[Table 6]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A4.T6 "Table 6 ‣ Experimental setting. ‣ D.2 Noisy synthetic binary tree ‣ Appendix D Experimental Details ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} and [[Table 6]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A4.T6 "Table 6 ‣ Experimental setting. ‣ D.2 Noisy synthetic binary tree ‣ Appendix D Experimental Details ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. We use a Gaussian negative log-likelihood loss
for the reconstruction loss with fixed $\sigma = 0.01$, which is
selected for sufficient reconstruction performance on the train set.

### [D.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}WordNet {#d.3-wordnet .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS3.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Experimental setting. {#experimental-setting.-1 .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#A4.SS3.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
We train a probabilistic word embedding model with WordNet
dataset (Fellbaum,
[1998](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}).
We initialize the mean and variance parameters with
$\mathcal{N}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{0},0.01)}$. For the full covariance
model, we use a learning rate $0.01$. For the other models, we set the
learning rate $0.015$ for the first 100 epochs and then set the learning
rate to $0.6$ for the remaining steps as done in (Nickel and Kiela,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref};
Nagano et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
We evaluate the learned representations by computing the average rank of
all the hypernymies. The rank of a given pair of words $s$ and $t$ is
computed among the distances between all possible pairs of the words $s$
and $t^{\prime}$ without hypernymy.
:::
:::

#### Experimental setting. {#experimental-setting.-2 .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#A4.SS4.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
To learn the implicit hierarchy that can be observed from the
trajectories of Breakout, we train the VAE models with the Atari 2600
Breakout images. The images of Breakout are collected by using a
pre-trained Deep Q-network (Mnih et al.,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref})
and divided into a training set and test set with 90,000 and 10,000
images respectively. We label each image with the score obtained from
the game environment. So the labels are correlated to the number of
broken blocks. To train VAE, we use a DCGAN-based architecture, which
was originally used to evaluate HWN in Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
The detailed architecture is provided in [[Table 13]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A4.T13 "Table 13 ‣ Experimental setting. ‣ D.4 Atari 2600 Breakout ‣ Appendix D Experimental Details ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref} and [[Table 13]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A4.T13 "Table 13 ‣ Experimental setting. ‣ D.4 Atari 2600 Breakout ‣ Appendix D Experimental Details ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. We use binary cross-entropy loss for the
reconstruction loss.
:::

::: {#A4.SS4.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
To evaluate the models, we measure the correlation between the norm of
the test images and the labeled scores. For the norm of the hyperbolic
embeddings, we use the Poincaré norm, which can be calculated by
projecting the Lorentz model embedding to the Poincaré disk model. The
results are reported in [[Table 14]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#A4.T14 "Table 14 ‣ Results. ‣ D.4 Atari 2600 Breakout ‣ Appendix D Experimental Details ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}. While all the models show similar representation
power with respect to ELBO, RoWN and the full covariance HWN outperform
the diagonal HWN. Especially, RoWN aligns the hierarchical structures
better with respect to the norm in low latent dimensions. Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref})
report a higher score of correlation in latent dimension 20, but we find
some issues with the result. First, Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref})
compute the correlation between the labeled scores and the norm in the
tangent space ($\mathbf{v}$ vector from Algorithm [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#alg4 "Algorithm 4 ‣ Appendix C Details of Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref}),
not the Poincaré norm. The projection function in Proposition
[[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#Thmproposition2 "Proposition 2. ‣ 3.1 Observations on the hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref}
depends on the first element of the input vector. Thus the Poincaré norm
is not proportional to the $\mathbf{v}$ norm, and computing the
correlation with the $\mathbf{v}$ norm will show different behavior
compared to the correlation with the Poincaré norm. Second, the
reproduction results obtained from the code by the official
repository[^1^[[^1^[1]{.ltx_tag
.ltx_tag_note}[https://github.com/pfnet-research/hyperbolic_wrapped_distribution](https://github.com/pfnet-research/hyperbolic_wrapped_distribution){.ltx_ref
.ltx_url .ltx_font_typewriter
target="_blank"}]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote1
.ltx_note .ltx_role_footnote} are far from the reported correlation. Our
reproduction results of the correlation between the labeled scores and
the $\mathbf{v}$ norm show 0.616, and between the Poincaré norm show
0.501 averaged over four runs.
:::

::: {#A5 .section .ltx_appendix}
## [Appendix E ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Discussion on Root Placement {#appendix-e-discussion-on-root-placement .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A5.p1 .ltx_para}
Note that due to the isometry of the geometry, the root node can be
placed anywhere in hyperbolic space. In other words, infinitely many
sets of embeddings preserve the same pairwise distances between the
nodes. This reveals that finding the appropriate isometry, where the
root node is placed near the origin, is important for using RoWN as the
distribution. In this section, we discuss the techniques we used to
place the root node near the origin of each application.
:::

::: {#A5.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [E.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Probabilistic word embedding model {#e.1-probabilistic-word-embedding-model .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A5.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
In our experiments, to place the root node near the origin, we have
initialized embeddings from
$\mathcal{N}\operatorname{}{(0,{0.01\operatorname{}I})}$, which are then
moved to the Lorentz model using the exponential map, with learning rate
warm-up Nagano et al.
([2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
The results with different initialization method are shown in
[[Table 3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S4.T3 "Table 3 ‣ Discussion of the root placement. ‣ 4.3 WordNet ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}.
:::
:::

::: {#A5.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [E.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Hyperbolic VAE {#e.2-hyperbolic-vae .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A5.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
When RoWN is used as a variational distribution in the hyperbolic VAE,
the application of RoWN is different from the probabilistic word
embedding model since the KL divergence encourages all variational means
to be close to the prior mean. Suppose we only focus on nodes at a
certain depth in a tree. In that case, it can be easily identified that
it would be beneficial to have all nodes at the same level of the norm
to minimize the geometric mean between the prior and posterior means.
Here, we assume that each pair of nodes requires to have a certain
amount of distance to minimize the reconstruction error. As shown in
[[2(a)]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S3.F2.sf1 "2(a) ‣ Figure 3 ‣ 3.2 Rotated hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}, the original HWN is difficult to have the nodes
at the same depth with similar norms since the local variation cannot be
modeled through the radial direction. Eventually, the root node slightly
deviates from the prior mean. With RoWN, as shown in [[2(c)]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2205.13371#S3.F2.sf3 "2(c) ‣ Figure 3 ‣ 3.2 Rotated hyperbolic wrapped normal distribution ‣ 3 Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution ‣ A Rotated Hyperbolic Wrapped Normal Distribution for Hierarchical Representation Learning"){.ltx_ref
.ltx_refmacro_autoref}, all the nodes at the same depth can be placed at
a similar norm while preserving their local variations. If this is
indeed the case, the root node is likely to be placed near the prior
mean since the nodes with different depths will be placed at different
levels of norms in the space.
