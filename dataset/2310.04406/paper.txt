::: ltx_abstract
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

While large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive
performance on a range of decision-making tasks, they rely on simple
acting processes and fall short of broad deployment as autonomous
agents. We introduce LATS (Language Agent Tree Search), a general
framework that synergizes the capabilities of LLMs in planning, acting,
and reasoning. Drawing inspiration from Monte Carlo tree search in
model-based reinforcement learning, LATS employs LLMs as agents, value
functions, and optimizers, repurposing their latent strengths for
enhanced decision-making. What is crucial in this method is the use of
an environment for external feedback, which offers a more deliberate and
adaptive problem-solving mechanism that moves beyond the limitations of
existing techniques. Our experimental evaluation across diverse domains,
such as programming, HotPotQA, and WebShop, illustrates the
applicability of LATS for both reasoning and acting. In particular, LATS
achieves for programming on HumanEval with GPT-4 and an average
score of for web browsing on WebShop with GPT-3.5, demonstrating
the effectiveness and generality of our method.
:::

## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
General autonomous agents capable of reasoning and decision-making in a
variety of environments (Wooldridge & Jennings,
[1995](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib51){.ltx_ref})
have been of longstanding interest in the field of artificial
intelligence. While this has traditionally been studied in reinforcement
learning, the recent rise of large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref};
Chowdhery et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref};
Touvron et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref};
OpenAI,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref})
with strong reasoning and general adaptability offers an alternative
paradigm. Not only have LLMs excelled on standard NLP tasks such as text
summarization (Nallapati et al.,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref})
or natural language inference (Bowman et al.,
[2015](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref}),
but they have been adapted to an increasingly diverse set of tasks that
often require advanced common-sense reasoning or quantitative skills
(Cobbe et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref};
Saparov & He,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref}).
LLMs are also capable of performing in complex environments that involve
knowledge and reasoning, such as web navigation (Yao et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref};
Deng et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref}),
tool-use (Schick et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref}),
or open-ended games (Fan et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Reasoning and acting abilities have also been improved by prompting
techniques that augment LLMs with feedback or observations from an
external environment (Yao et al.,
[2023b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref};
Gao et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref};
Shinn et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}).
This eliminates the need to rely entirely on the base abilities of the
Language Model (LM), enhancing it through external tools or semantic
feedback. Despite this strength, these methods are reflexive and fall
short of the deliberate and thoughtful decision-making characteristics
that humans employ to solve problems (Sloman,
[1996](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref};
Evans,
[2010](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref}).
In particular, such methods fail to consider multiple reasoning paths or
to plan ahead. Recent search-guided LLM works (Xie et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib53){.ltx_ref};
Yao et al.,
[2023a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref};
Hao et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref})
address this issue by searching over multiple chains of thought. While
these methods enable planning, they operate in isolation and do not
incorporate external feedback that can improve reasoning.
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
To help address these issues, we propose LATS (Language Agent Tree
Search), a general framework for decision-making and reasoning with
language models. LATS unifies LM planning, acting, and reasoning
strategies by expanding ReAct (Yao et al.,
[2023b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref})
into a search over a combinatorial space of possible reasoning and
acting steps. We adapt Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) from model-based
reinforcement learning (Silver et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref};
Anthony et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref};
Jiang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref})
to language agents, repurposing a pretrained LLM as an agent, value
function, and optimizer. Utilizing the strong natural language
understanding and in-context learning ability of modern LMs, we use text
as an interface between each component of the framework, allowing LATS
to adapt planning to environmental conditions without additional
training. To the best of our knowledge, *LATS is the first framework
that combines reasoning, acting, and planning to enhance LLMs*. Notably,
LATS doubles the performance of GPT-3.5 on HotPotQA (Yang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib54){.ltx_ref})
over ReAct (Yao et al.,
[2023b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref})
and raises the average score by $22.1$ on WebShop (Yao et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref}).
When used with GPT-4, LATS achieves a $94.4$ Pass@1 rate for programming
on HumanEval (Chen et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref}),
setting the state of the art. To summarize, our
[contributions]{#S1.p3.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} are the following:

-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i1}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    We introduce an LM-based Monte Carlo tree search variant to
    deliberately construct the best trajectory from sampled actions,
    enabling more flexible and adaptive problem-solving compared to
    reflexive prompting methods. This is guided by heuristics from the
    LM.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i2}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    By integrating external feedback and self-reflection, LATS enhances
    model sensibility and enables agents to learn from experience,
    surpassing reasoning-based search methods.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i3}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
    Through experiments across diverse domains like programming,
    interactive QA, and web navigation, we demonstrate the versatility
    of LATS in harnessing LLMs for autonomous reasoning and
    decision-making. [^1^[[^1^[1]{.ltx_tag
    .ltx_tag_note}[https://andyz245.github.io/LanguageAgentTreeSearch/](https://andyz245.github.io/LanguageAgentTreeSearch/){.ltx_ref
    .ltx_url .ltx_font_typewriter
    target="_blank"}]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote1
    .ltx_note .ltx_role_footnote}
    :::
:::

::: {#S2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[LLMs for reasoning.]{#S2.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} For LLMs,
reasoning typically involves decomposing complex inputs into sequential
intermediate steps before producing a final answer (Cobbe et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}),
commonly demonstrated with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref})
and its variants (Wei et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref};
Kojima et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref};
Wang et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib49){.ltx_ref}).
However, these methods, which create chains auto-regressively in a
single step, often suffer from error propagation as the number of steps
increases (Guo et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib20){.ltx_ref};
Chen et al.,
[2022b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref}),
in which errors tend to compound. Various advancements have aimed to
mitigate this issue; some approaches, such as Self-Consistency (Wang
et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib49){.ltx_ref}),
employ majority voting over sampled chains, while others focus on
multi-step decomposition, such as least-to-most prompting (Zhou et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib59){.ltx_ref}),
or use of external tools such as a scratchpad (Nye et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib34){.ltx_ref})
or compiler (Gao et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref}).
More recently, CoT has been improved with search algorithms (Yao et al.,
[2023a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref};
Hao et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref};
Besta et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref})
that can sample trajectories more effectively. Tree-of-thought (ToT)
prompting (Yao et al.,
[2023a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref})
uses DFS or BFS-based search guided by an LM-generated heuristic while
Reasoning via Planning (RAP) (Hao et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref})
uses MCTS with rollouts simulated by the LM. Despite using a search
algorithm, these frameworks rely solely on the internal knowledge of the
LM and cannot adapt to external inputs that could enhance the reasoning
process.
:::

::: {#S2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[LLMs for decision-making.]{#S2.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} The
strong reasoning and common-sense abilities of LLMs have also been
adapted for decision-making tasks as a policy model in interactive
environments. In the realm of robotics LLMs have been employed as
high-level controllers of control policies (Ahn et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref};
Huang et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref};
Driess et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref}).
Similar work (Baker et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref};
Wang et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib48){.ltx_ref};
Zhu et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib60){.ltx_ref})
has also adapted LLM agents to complex multimodal games such as
Minecraft (Guss et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref};
Fan et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}).
LLMs are particularly useful in text-based environments (Liu et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref};
Shridhar et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib40){.ltx_ref};
Liu et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}),
where acting-based prompting techniques such as ReAct (Yao et al.,
[2023b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref})
have seen success. Similar to CoT, ReAct is limited by its simplicity
and cannot effectively adapt to environment conditions. Many extensions
have been proposed to address this, including Self-refine (Madaan
et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref})
and Reflexion (Shinn et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref};
Yao et al.,
[2023c](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib58){.ltx_ref}),
which uses self-reflection to enhance reasoning and decision-making, and
AdaPlanner (Sun et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref}),
which incorporates both positive and negative environmental feedback.
However these methods focus on refining an individual plan or trajectory
and do not consider alternative choices at each step. Alternatively to
pure decision-making environments, the reasoning and practical abilities
of LLMs have been enhanced by access to external tools, such as APIs,
search engines, calculators, or other models (Schick et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref};
Shen et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref};
Surís et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref}).
Contrary to reasoning-based approaches, these methods have not been
improved with planning, limiting their effectiveness.
:::

::: {#S2.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Tree-based search.]{#S2.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} Tree-based
search, where multiple branches of outcomes are explored during search,
is widely used in many planning algorithms (Świechowski et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref};
LaValle et al.,
[2001](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref})
and Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Hafner et al.,
[2019](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref};
Du et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref};
Wu et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib52){.ltx_ref})
algorithms for its good exploration-exploitation trade-off. Though
tree-based search requires an environment model that can expand from
arbitrary state (Vodopivec et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib47){.ltx_ref}),
which often requires extra training in RL (Hafner et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}),
such problem does not exist for LM tasks as we can conveniently backup
to any state by setting the input to be the context and corresponding
previous output by the LM. Thus, we work on the tree-based framework and
use MCTS (Świechowski et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref})
to fully release the potential of LMs, while avoiding the cost of
training a value function over language descriptions by leveraging the
in-context learning (Brown et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref})
abilities of LLMs.
:::
:::

::: {#S3 .section .ltx_section}
## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Preliminaries {#preliminaries .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Before describing LATS, we first define our problem and outline a few
established methods that leverage large language models for reasoning or
decision-making. In LM reasoning or decision making, we are given an
input $x$ in natural language and a pretrained language model
$p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(x)}$ parameterized by $\theta$; our goal is
to generate a final output $y \sim {p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(x)}}$
corresponding to the answer (reasoning) or completes the task
(decision-making). Both $x$ and $y$ are language [sequences]{#S3.p1.16.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, which are comprised of a list of
[tokens]{#S3.p1.16.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} (the basic elements of
natural language, often words), denoted as
$x = {({x\operatorname{}{\lbrack 1\rbrack}},\ldots,{x\operatorname{}{\lbrack n\rbrack}})}$
and
$y = {({y\operatorname{}{\lbrack 1\rbrack}},\ldots,{y\operatorname{}{\lbrack n\rbrack}})}$.
The LM decodes text autoregressively, i.e., without other inputs, the
probability for an LM to generate a sequence $x$ is given by
${p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(x)}} = {\prod_{i = 1}^{n}{p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. {x\operatorname{}{\lbrack i\rbrack}} \middle| {x\operatorname{}{\lbrack{{1\operatorname{}\ldots\operatorname{}i} - 1}\rbrack}} \right.)}}}$.
Usually, to improve the LM, [prompts]{#S3.p1.16.3 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} are provided along with the input $x$, which are
specific instructions or few-shot input-output examples. We denote the
generic process where an input $x$ is transformed into an output $y$ by
LM:
$y \sim {p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| {\text{𝚙𝚛𝚘𝚖𝚙𝚝}_{I\operatorname{}O}\operatorname{}{(x)}} \right.)}}$,
where $\text{𝚙𝚛𝚘𝚖𝚙𝚝}_{I\operatorname{}O}\operatorname{}{(x)}$ denotes
the input $x$ along with the prompts.
:::

::: {#S3.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Chain-of-thought (CoT) Prompting]{#S3.p2.9.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} (Wei et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref})
was introduced to cater to scenarios where direct mapping from $x$ to
$y$ is intricate, such as when $x$ is from a mathematical query or
challenging question. This method hinges on creating
[thoughts]{#S3.p2.9.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} $z_{1},\ldots,z_{n}$
that act as stepping stones between $x$ and $y$; each thought $z_{i}$ is
a language sequence. To employ CoT prompting, thoughts are extracted
sequentially as
$z_{i} \sim {p_{\theta}^{C\operatorname{}o\operatorname{}T}\operatorname{}{(\left. z_{i} \middle| {x,z_{{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i} - 1}} \right.)}}$,
with the final output being
$y \sim {p_{\theta}^{C\operatorname{}o\operatorname{}T}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| {x,z_{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}n}} \right.)}}$.
:::

::: {#S3.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Tree-of-thought (ToT) Prompting]{#S3.p3.6.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} (Yao et al.,
[2023a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref})
extends CoT prompting by exploring multiple reasoning paths over
thoughts. It frames problems as a search over a tree where each node
$s = {\lbrack x,z_{1 \cdot i}\rbrack}$ represents a partial solution
state comprising the original input $x$ and thought sequence
$z_{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i}$. Thoughts $z_{i}$ are
generated by proposal or sampling with CoT
$z_{i} \sim {p_{\theta}^{C\operatorname{}o\operatorname{}T}\operatorname{}{(\left. z_{i} \middle| {x,z_{{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i} - 1}} \right.)}}$.
Deliberate search algorithms like breadth-first or depth-first search
are used to systematically explore the tree, guided by heuristics based
on language model evaluations $V\operatorname{}{(s)}$ of each state.
:::

::: {#S3.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Reasoning via Planning]{#S3.p4.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} (RAP) (Hao
et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref})
is similar to ToT, except that MCTS is used over DFS or BFS. Heuristics
are designed from an LM, such as the likelihood or confidence of an
action, and the LM is used as a world model to predict subsequent states
during the simulation step.
:::

::: {#S3.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[ReAct]{#S3.p5.9.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} (Yao et al.,
[2023b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref})
extends language models to tasks where the mapping from $x$ to $y$ is
enhanced by or requires interactions with an external environment, such
as a game or API. This technique constructs an action space
$\hat{A} = {A \cup Z}$ that adds permissible actions $a$ to the
reasoning traces $z$ from CoT. Observations $o$ from the environment are
used to improve both reasoning and acting. To solve problems with ReAct,
after each observation, actions are generated from $p_{\theta}$
sequentially as
$a_{i} \sim {p_{\theta}^{R\operatorname{}e\operatorname{}A\operatorname{}c\operatorname{}t}\operatorname{}{(\left. a_{i} \middle| {x,o_{{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i} - 1},a_{{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i} - 1}} \right.)}}$,
with the final output being
$y \sim {p_{\theta}^{R\operatorname{}e\operatorname{}A\operatorname{}c\operatorname{}t}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| {x,o_{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}n},a_{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}n}} \right.)}}$.
:::

::: {#S3.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
While the previously described prompting techniques improve LM
performance on reasoning tasks, they falter on difficult tasks that
involve multifaceted decision-making due to several shortcomings: 1)
[Flexibility]{#S3.p6.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}: Base prompting
methods (CoT or ReAct) autoregressively sample from the LM, neglecting
potential alternative continuations from specific states. 2)
[Sensibility]{#S3.p6.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}: Reasoning-based
methods (CoT, RAP, or ToT) rely solely on the internal representations
of the LM and cannot consider external observations. This dependency
risks fact hallucination and error propagation while setting a
performance ceiling. 3) [Adaptability]{#S3.p6.1.3 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}: Current planning frameworks (RAP or ToT) use simple
search algorithms such as BFS or cannot leverage environmental feedback
to improve planning. Additionally, the agent is static and cannot reuse
previous experience or learn from trial and error. While RAP also adopts
MCTS, it is constrained to tasks where the LM can become a world model
and accurately predict states. These shortcomings limit the ability of
LMs to be deployed as general problem-solving agents and form the
motivation for LATS.
:::
:::

::: {#S4 .section .ltx_section}
## [4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Unifying Planning, Reasoning, and Acting {#unifying-planning-reasoning-and-acting .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S4.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}LM Agent {#lm-agent .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
LATS supports sequential reasoning or decision-making tasks on the basis
of ReAct. At time step $t$, an agent receives an observation
$o_{t} \in O$ from the environment and takes an action $a_{t} \in A$
following some policy
$\pi\operatorname{}{(\left. a_{t} \middle| {x,o_{{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i} - 1},a_{{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i} - 1}} \right.)}$,
where $x$ consists of the task instruction and a number of few-shot
examples. We initialize the agent with $p_{\theta}$ to leverage the
useful language representations of an LM as a base decision-maker. We
follow the ReAct instantiation in which the action space
$\hat{A} = {A \cup Z}$ consists of both the space of permissible actions
$A$ and language space of reasoning traces $Z$. Actions directly affect
the environment and result in observation, while thoughts are used to
formalize decisions by organizing information, planning future actions,
or injecting internal knowledge. The exact instantiation of the action
space depends on the particular environment; for decision-making tasks
actions might consist of commands on a website while for reasoning tasks
the action space might be limited to a few external tools or APIs.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Instead of greedily decoding one trajectory or solution, we sample $n$
actions from $p_{\theta}$ using the current state. This is based on the
intuition that for complex decision-making tasks, there is likely to be
a range of potential trajectories or reasoning paths that are correct
(Evans,
[2010](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref}).
Sampling a diverse set of candidates at each step mitigates the
stochastic nature of LM text generation and enables greater exploration
in both the decision-making and reasoning space. We wrap $p_{\theta}$
within our proposed search algorithm to deliberately construct the best
trajectory from sampled actions.
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}LATS {#lats .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

<figure id="S4.F3" class="ltx_figure">
<img src="./lats_files/x5.png" id="S4.F3.g1"
class="ltx_graphics ltx_centering ltx_img_landscape" width="830"
height="415" alt="Refer to caption" />
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_figure">Figure 3: </span>An
overview of the six operations of LATS. A node is <span id="S4.F3.7.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">selected</span>, <span id="S4.F3.8.2"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">expanded</span>, <span id="S4.F3.9.3"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">evaluated</span>, then <span
id="S4.F3.10.4" class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">simulated</span> until
a terminal node is reached, then the resulting value is <span
id="S4.F3.11.5" class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">backpropagated</span>.
If the trajectory fails, a <span id="S4.F3.12.6"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">reflection</span> is generated and used
as additional context for future trials. These operations are performed
in succession until the budget is reached or task is
successful.</figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S4.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The main component of LATS is a search algorithm that controls the
overall problem-solving process with deliberate planning. In order to
find the most promising trajectory and systemically balance exploration
with exploitation, we adopt a variant of Monte Carlo Tree Search that
frames decision-making as a tree search, in which each node
$s = {\lbrack x,a_{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i},o_{1\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}i}\rbrack}$
represents a state comprising the original input $x$, action sequence
$a_{1 \cdot i}$, and observation sequence $o_{1 \cdot i}$. With
instructions and in-context learning, LATS repurposes $p_{\theta}$ as an
agent, state evaluator, and feedback generator, leveraging the useful
language priors of modern LMs to facilitate planning. LATS consists of a
series of operations, [selection, expansion, evaluation, simulation,
backpropagation, and reflection]{#S4.SS2.p1.5.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}, performed in succession until the task is
successfully completed or a predetermined computational limit is
reached. The full psuedocode of LATS can be found in Sec. [[A]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#A1 "Appendix A LATS Pseudocode ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
in the Appendix.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Selection.]{#S4.SS2.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} In the first
operation, the algorithm identifies a segment of the current tree most
suitable for subsequent expansion. Starting from the root node, denoted
as the initial state $s_{0}$, a child node is selected at each tree
level until a leaf node is reached. To balance exploration and
exploitation, we use the *Upper Confidence bounds applied to Trees
(UCT)* algorithm (Kocsis & Szepesvári,
[2006](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref})
to select nodes

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --
     $${{\text{UCT}\operatorname{}{(s)}} = {\frac{V\operatorname{}{(s)}}{M\operatorname{}{(s)}} + {w\operatorname{}\sqrt{\frac{{\ln N}\operatorname{}{(p)}}{M\operatorname{}{(s)}}}}}},$$   
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- --

where $N\operatorname{}{(s)}$ is the number of visits to a node $s$,
$V\operatorname{}{(s)}$ is the value of $s$, $w$ is the exploration
weight, and $p$ is the parent node of $s$. At each level, the child node
with the highest UCT value is selected.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Expansion.]{#S4.SS2.p3.3.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} After selecting a
node, the second operation expands the tree by sampling $n$ actions from
$p_{\theta}$, as described in the prior section. The environment
receives each action and returns corresponding feedback as an
observation. This results in $n$ new child nodes added to the tree. This
tree is stored in an external long-term memory structure.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Evaluation.]{#S4.SS2.p4.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} The third
operation assigns a scalar value to each new child node to be used for
selection and backpropagation. This value effectively quantifies the
agent's progress in task completion, serving as a heuristic to steer the
search algorithm towards the most promising regions of the tree.
Following Yao et al.
([2023a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref})
we repurpose $p_{\theta}$ into a value function by prompting it to
reason about a given state. To obtain a scalar value, we instruct
$p_{\theta}$ to end its reasoning trace with a score indicating the
correctness of the trajectory. This method offers enhanced flexibility
over programmed heuristics (Campbell et al.,
[2002](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref})
and greater efficiency than learned heuristics (Silver et al.,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Simulation.]{#S4.SS2.p5.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} The fourth
operation expands the currently selected node until a terminal state is
reached. At each depth level we sample and evaluate nodes with the same
operations, but prioritize nodes of highest value. Reaching a terminal
state provides objective feedback on the correctness of a trajectory. If
the task is completed successfully, then LATS terminates the search. If
the solution is partially successful or unsuccessful, then we perform
two additional operations as described below.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Backpropagation.]{#S4.SS2.p6.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} This
operation updates the values of the tree based on the outcome of a
trajectory. For each node in the trajectory, its value is updated to
reflect the outcome of the simulation.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p7 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Reflection.]{#S4.SS2.p7.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} In addition to
the environmental feedback, we also leverage
[self-reflection]{#S4.SS2.p7.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} to further
refine the decision-making process (Shinn et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref};
Madaan et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}).
Upon encountering an unsuccessful terminal node, $p_{\theta}$ is
prompted with the trajectory and final reward to provide a verbal
self-reflection that summarizes the errors in the reasoning or acting
process and proposes superior alternatives. We store both failed
trajectories and corresponding reflections in the memory. In subsequent
iterations, these are integrated as additional context to the agent and
value function, refining both through in-context learning. This imparts
a semantic gradient signal more useful than a scalar value, enabling the
agent to learn from trial and error without the cost of expensive
optimization processes such as reinforcement learning.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p8 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Conceptually, LATS has the following advantages as a general framework
for reasoning and decision-making with LM agents. (1)
[Generality]{#S4.SS2.p8.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}: LATS supports
both reasoning and decision-making tasks by defining a shared space of
thoughts and actions. (2) [Deliberate]{#S4.SS2.p8.1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}: The use of MCTS and LM value function ensures a
principled search that selects options with high value while exploring
promising alternatives. (3) [Adaptability]{#S4.SS2.p8.1.3 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}: LATS is designed around the use of external feedback
through observations and self-reflection, enabling greater adaptation
during problem-solving. (4) [Flexibility]{#S4.SS2.p8.1.4 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}: LATS can accommodate different scenarios,
environments, and resource stipulations by modifying state design and
tree dimensions. (5) [Modularity]{#S4.SS2.p8.1.5 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}: The base LM agent, reflection generator, and value
function can be independently altered and adapted to individual LM
properties.
:::
:::
:::

## [5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Experiments {#experiments .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S5.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
To demonstrate the general applicability of LATS, we evaluate our method
on a variety of decision-making domains: programming (Chen et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref};
Austin et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}),
HotPotQA (Yang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib54){.ltx_ref}),
and WebShop (Yao et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S5.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}HotPotQA {#hotpotqa .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
For a task that can be approached with both reasoning-based and
acting-based strategies, we consider HotPotQA (Yang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib54){.ltx_ref}),
a multi-hop question-answering benchmark that requires retrieval over
two or more Wikipedia passages. For the action space, in addition to LM
thoughts we follow the setup from Yao et al.
([2023b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref}),
which provides the agent with API calls to search and lookup
information. The output of these API calls and self-generated
reflections form the observation space. We use a subset of 100 questions
and three few-shot examples for each method. For ToT, we use DFS as the
base search algorithm and scoring with the LM as the heuristic. For all
methods that involve sampling, including LATS, we sample $k = 50$
trajectories. More details and prompts can be found in
Sec. [[C]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#A3 "Appendix C Environment Details ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
and Sec. [[D]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#A4 "Appendix D HotPotQA Prompts ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
in the Appendix.
:::

::: {#S5.SS1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
We evaluate internal reasoning strategies by removing actions and
observations from the context, corresponding to CoT (Wei et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref})
and its variants, CoT-SC (Wang et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib49){.ltx_ref}),
ToT (Yao et al.,
[2023a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref}),
and RAP (Hao et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}).
These methods rely solely on the agent's existing knowledge to answer
the question. We also consider acting-based methods ReAct, Reflexion,
and LATS, which augment the agent with the interactive API environment
and primarily evaluate its information retrieval abilities. While LATS
is designed for scenarios where external feedback can enhance reasoning,
we also implement a reasoning-only version with CoT as the base prompt.
We also combine internal and external reasoning in LATS by first
prompting with a CoT-based prompt, then switching to a ReAct-based
prompt upon failure. This is closer to how humans might approach this
task, by using tools to lookup additional information only when the
answer is not already known.
:::

::: {#S5.SS1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Results.]{#S5.SS1.p3.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We observe in Tab.
[[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#S5.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 5.1 HotPotQA ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
that both internal reasoning and external retrieval strategies perform
well on HotPotQA. Due to their large-scale training corpus, modern LLMs
already encode factual knowledge and can often directly answer the
question correctly. While CoT can slightly enhance performance on
questions that require reasoning, larger gains are observed with search
methods ToT and RAP, which can sample and explore a greater number of
outputs. We observe similar results for acting-based methods. LATS
surpasses ReAct, even when sampling the same number of trajectories, by
expanding more nodes with principled search. This is demonstrated when
modifying $n$, the number of nodes expanded during each iteration.
Increasing $n$ can consistently improve performance, although at greater
computational and inference costs. LATS is also competitive to RAP on
internal reasoning, but performs worse than acting. Combining internal
and external reasoning in LATS surpasses the supervised state of the
art, indicating the importance of external feedback to augment reasoning
even in tasks the base LM can already perform.

### [5.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Programming {#programming .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

<figure id="S5.T3" class="ltx_table">
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_table">Table 3: </span>GPT-3.5
and GPT-4 Pass@1 accuracy on HumanEval (Chen et al., <a

class="ltx_ref">2021</a>) and MBPP (Austin et al., <a

::: {#S5.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
To demonstrate the importance of external observations for complex
reasoning tasks, we evaluate the baselines and LATS on programming with
Humaneval (Chen et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref})
and MBPP (Austin et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}).
Both datasets measure the correctness of synthesized programs in Python
from natural language docstrings. We use individual solutions as the
action space and test suite and compiler feedback as the external
observation. We follow Chen et al.
([2022a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref})
and use an LLM to generate a synthetic test suite of syntactically valid
"assert" statements for each question. For each step, the solution is
evaluated on this test suite, and the results including successful and
failed tests and compiler output, are added to the context as an
observation. We use the same test suite for Reflexion.
:::

::: {#S5.SS2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
For this task, the reasoning and acting baselines share an action space,
but acting methods are able to incorporate observations as additional
context. For LATS, since each action corresponds to a complete solution,
we skip the simulation step of LATS and directly use the percentage of
passed tests as the backpropagated reward. We use $k = 8$ iterations,
set the number of generated tests at $4$, and sample $n = 5$ solutions
during expansion. After the search is completed, we select the solution
with the highest value and evaluate it on the real test suite for the
pass@1 accuracy evaluation. More details and prompts can be found in
Sec. [[C]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#A3 "Appendix C Environment Details ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
and Sec. [[E]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#A5 "Appendix E Programming Prompts ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
in the Appendix.
:::

::: {#S5.SS2.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Results.]{#S5.SS2.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We find in Tab
[[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#S5.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 5.2 Programming ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
that both search and semantic feedback are crucial for better
performance. Despite not using observations, ToT and RAP are competitive
with Reflexion. LATS has the highest performance on both datasets. Since
RAP uses a similar search algorithm as LATS, this reveals the importance
of external feedback for difficult reasoning tasks such as programming.
With GPT-4, using LATS sets the state of the art for HumanEval, showing
LATS can be used with more advanced LLMs for higher performance.
:::
:::

::: {#S5.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Webshop {#webshop .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
For a complex decision-making environment with practical applications,
we consider WebShop (Yao et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref}),
an online shopping environment composed of a website with 1.18M
real-world products and 12k human instructions. Agents must navigate a
website through a variety of commands to purchase an item matching a
user specification. We use the preconstructed action space of search and
click commands and browser feedback and reflections for the observation.
The performance is gauged using two metrics: an average score,
reflecting the percentage of user-specified attributes met by the
selected product, and a success rate, indicating the frequency with
which the chosen product fulfills all given conditions. We compare
against acting-based prompting methods and reinforcement-learning-based
approaches. We evaluate on 50 instructions, expand $n = 5$ children for
LATS, and set $k = 30$ for LATS, ReAct best of $k$, and Reflexion. More
details and prompts can be found in Sec. [[C]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#A3 "Appendix C Environment Details ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
and Sec. [[F]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#A6 "Appendix F WebShop Prompts ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
in the Appendix.
:::

::: {#S5.SS3.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Results.]{#S5.SS3.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We find in Tab.
[[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#S5.T5 "Table 5 ‣ 5.4 Additional Observations ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
that GPT-3.5 with ReAct is competitive to imitation learning, and can
exceed reinforcement learning techniques with stronger prompting
strategies. Sampling $k = 30$ trajectories with ReAct and Reflexion
results in a similar performance, suggesting the semantic feedback is
not as helpful in complex environments like WebShop. Indeed like in
Shinn et al.
([2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}),
we find that generated reflections are often generic and do not provide
useful feedback, resulting in a tendency for the agent to become stuck
in local minima. However, using LATS indeed results in a noticeable
improvement, indicating a more effective exploration for the same number
of iterations.
:::
:::

We also conduct additional experiments on HotPotQA to demonstrate the

incorporates both reasoning (through thoughts) and acting (through API
calls); the results are shown in Tab. [[5]{.ltx_text

::: {#S5.SS4.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[LM value function.]{#S5.SS4.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} The LM
value function scores states based on expected future reward. Without
this heuristic, the only signal to guide search would be from
environment rewards for completed trajectories, which are scarce and
often binary. We observe a dramatic $- 0.34$ drop in performance when we
remove the evaluation operation.
:::

::: {#S5.SS4.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Self-reflection.]{#S5.SS4.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We use
self-reflection to provide an additional semantic signal for the agent
and search algorithm. We observe a small $0.04$ drop in performance when
removed from LATS, suggesting this is useful. This is a smaller gain
Reflexion (Shinn et al.,
[2023](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref})
observes over ReAct (Yao et al.,
[2023b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref})
as shown in Tab. [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#S5.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 5.1 HotPotQA ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref},
suggesting there is overlap between the types of questions where there
is an improvement with self-reflection and search.
:::

::: {#S5.SS4.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Search Algorithm.]{#S5.SS4.p4.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} While MCTS
is a more principled search algorithm than variants like A\* or DFS
search, it is slower and requires more LM inference calls. We observe
the effects of using DFS, and incorporate the LM-based heuristic used in
ToT (Yao et al.,
[2023a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref})
in which branches with low values are pruned. This removes the selection
and backpropagation operations, and we observe a significant $- 0.19$
drop in performance.

::: {#S6 .section .ltx_section}
## [6 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Conclusion {#conclusion .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S6.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
In this work, we introduce Language Agent Tree Search (LATS), the first
framework to unify planning, acting, and reasoning for enhanced LLM
problem solving. By deliberately constructing trajectories with search
algorithms, incorporating external feedback, and enabling agents to
learn from experience, LATS addresses key limitations of prior prompting
techniques. Our evaluations demonstrate the ability of LATS to harness
LLM capabilities for a variety of reasoning and decision-making tasks
without additional training. The proposed synergies between search,
interaction, and reflection offer a versatile approach to autonomous
decision-making, highlighting the potential of LLMs as generalist
agents. A full discussion of the limitations and broader impacts of LATS
can be found in Sec. [[B]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#A2 "Appendix B Discussion ‣ Language Agent Tree Search Unifies Reasoning Acting and Planning in Language Models"){.ltx_ref}
in the Appendix.
:::

## [Appendix B ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Discussion {#appendix-b-discussion .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Limitations.]{#A2.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} Although LATS can
improve reasoning and decision-making, this arrives at a higher
computational cost. The search process takes more time than standard
prompting or simpler techniques, and requires greater inference costs.
In practice, we recommend using LATS for difficult tasks like
programming or for situations where performance is prioritized over
efficiency. We hope that continued advancements in LLMs will reduce
costs and increase the practicality of LATS.
:::

::: {#A2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Broader impact.]{#A2.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} LATS is a
framework that enhances LLM performance through interactions with an
environment. This improvement in autonomous decision-making may
facilitate harmful uses of LLMs. Alternatively, LATS enhances
interpretability and the potential for greater alignment, as it
generates understandable, high-level linguistic reasoning and actions
through several rounds of decision-making and reflection, rather than
relying on implicit, low-level token values.
:::
:::

## [Appendix C ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Environment Details {#appendix-c-environment-details .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A3.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [C.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}HotPotQA {#c.1-hotpotqa .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A3.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
HotPotQA (Yang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib54){.ltx_ref})
is a question-answering dataset that requires reasoning over multiple
supporting documents to answer questions. It contains 113k
Wikipedia-based question-answer pairs crafted by crowdworkers to be
diverse, multi-hop, and explainable. Questions cover a range of types
like entities, locations, dates, and comparison of shared properties
between two entities. Crowdworkers also provide supporting facts from
the documents that justify the answer. We use the HotPotQA benchmark
setting with all the Wikipedia paragraphs to test retrieval. We use a
randomly selected subset of 100 questions for our experiments and a
maximum depth limit of 6.
:::

::: {#A3.SS1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Action Space.]{#A3.SS1.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We adopt the
Wikipedia web API proposed in Yao et al.
([2023b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref}),
with three types of actions to support interactive information
retrieval:
:::

::: {#A3.SS1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(1\) [search]{#A3.SS1.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter
.ltx_font_bold}\[[entity]{#A3.SS1.p3.1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_typewriter}\], which returns the first 5 sentences from the
corresponding [entity]{#A3.SS1.p3.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter}
wiki page if it exists, or else suggests top-5 similar entities from the
Wikipedia search engine,
:::

::: {#A3.SS1.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(2\) [lookup]{#A3.SS1.p4.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter
.ltx_font_bold}\[[string]{#A3.SS1.p4.1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_typewriter}\], which returns the next sentence in the page
containing [string]{#A3.SS1.p4.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter},
:::

::: {#A3.SS1.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(3\) [finish]{#A3.SS1.p5.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter
.ltx_font_bold}\[[answer]{#A3.SS1.p5.1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_typewriter}\], which finishes the current task with
[answer]{#A3.SS1.p5.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter}.
:::

::: {#A3.SS1.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
These API calls and free-form thoughts form the action space for this
environment.
:::
:::

::: {#A3.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [C.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Programming {#c.2-programming .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A3.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The HumanEval dataset (Chen et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref})
is a collection of 164 handwritten programming problems introduced to
evaluate the functional correctness of models for synthesizing programs
from natural language descriptions. Each problem includes a function
signature, docstring description, reference implementation, and multiple
unit tests, with an average of 7.7 tests per problem. The programming
tasks assess comprehension of natural language, reasoning, algorithms,
and basic mathematics, at a difficulty level comparable to simple
software interview questions. Pass rates are evaluated with the pass@k
metric, where k samples are generated per problem and a problem is
considered solved if any sample passes all tests. We use all 164
problems for our experiments and a maximum depth limit of 8.
:::

::: {#A3.SS2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The Mostly Basic Programming Problems (MBPP) Austin et al.
([2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref})
benchmark contains 974 short Python functions designed to evaluate
program synthesis techniques. The dataset was constructed by
crowdsourcing from workers with basic Python knowledge. Each data point
consists of a natural language description of a programming task, a
reference solution implementation, and three test cases for functional
correctness. The natural language prompts are typically short,
one-sentence descriptions. Solutions cover common programming constructs
including mathematical operations, list processing, string manipulation,
and usage of the Python standard library. On average, solutions are 6.8
lines of code. The dataset is also supplemented with an additional set
of 426 problems that were manually verified for unambiguous
specifications, standard function signatures, and accurate test cases.
We use a randomly selected subset of 397 problems for our experiments.
:::
:::

::: {#A3.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [C.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}WebShop {#c.3-webshop .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A3.SS3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
WebShop (Yao et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2310.04406#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref})
is an interactive web-based environment designed to evaluate agents on
grounded language understanding and decision-making. It simulates an
e-commerce shopping task by providing agents with over 1 million
real-world products scraped from Amazon, spanning 5 categories and 113
subcategories. These products contain rich linguistic information, with
an average text length of 262 words and a vocabulary size of 224k. In
addition, there are over 800k unique product options available for
customization. The environment renders webpages in two modes: HTML mode
provides pixel-level observations with interactive elements, while
simple mode converts the raw HTML into a structured text observation
more amenable for training agents. The action space consists of query
searches and button clicks, which transition between 4 page types:
search, results, item and item-detail. Instructions are crowdsourced
natural language specifying product attributes and options, with a total
of 12k collected. Automatic rewards are computed by comparing the
product purchased by the agent against the attributes and options
specified in the instruction, using both lexical matching and semantic
similarity metrics.\
:::

<figure id="A3.T6" class="ltx_table">
</table>
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_table">Table 6: </span>Action
space of webshop.</figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#A3.SS3.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
There are two evaluation metrics used in WebShop: (1) [Task
Score]{#A3.SS3.p2.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}: defined as
$({100 \times \text{avg. reward}})$, which captures the average reward
obtained across episodes; and (2) [Success Rate (SR)]{#A3.SS3.p2.2.2
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} defined as the portion of instructions where
$r = 1$. The reward is calculated based on the number of attributes
satisfied by the selected item. We use 50 environments for our
experiments and a maximum depth limit of 15.
:::
:::
:::

### [D.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Base Acting Prompt {#d.1-base-acting-prompt .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Solve a question answering task with interleaving Thought, Action,
Observation steps. Thought can reason about the current situation, and
Action can be three types:
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(1\) Search\[entity\], which searches the exact entity on Wikipedia and
returns the first paragraph if it exists. If not, it will return some
similar entities to search.
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(2\) Lookup\[keyword\], which returns the next sentence containing
keyword in the current passage.
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(3\) Finish\[answer\], which returns the answer and finishes the task.
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
After each observation, provide the next Thought and next Action.
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Here are some examples:
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p7 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Question: Which magazine was started first Arthur's Magazine or First
for Women?
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p8 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Thought 1: I need to search Arthur's Magazine and First for Women, and
find which was started first.
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p9 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Action 1: Search\[Arthur's Magazine\]
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p10 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Observation 1: Arthur's Magazine (18440̆0e20̆0800̆0931846) was an American
literary periodical published in Philadelphia in the 19th century.
Edited by Timothy Shay Arthur, it featured work by Edgar A. Poe, J.H.
Ingraham, Sarah Josepha Hale, Thomas G. Spear, and others.\[1\]\[2\] In
May 1846 it was merged into Godey's Lady's Book.\[3\]
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p11 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Thought 2: Arthur's Magazine was started in 1844. I need to search First
for Women next.
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p12 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Action 2: Search\[First for Women\]
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p13 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Observation 2: First for Women is a woman's magazine published by Bauer
Media Group in the USA.\[1\] The magazine was started in 1989. It is
based in Englewood Cliffs, New Jersey.\[2\] In 2011 the circulation of
the magazine was 1,310,696 copies.\[3\]
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p14 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Thought 3: First for Women was started in 1989. 1844 (Arthur's Magazine)
¡ 1989 (First for Women), so Arthur's Magazine was started first.
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.p15 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Action 3: Finish\[Arthur's Magazine\]
:::

### [D.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Base Reasoning Prompt {#d.2-base-reasoning-prompt .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Solve a question answering task by having a Thought, then Finish with
your answer. Thought can reason about the current situation.
Finish\[answer\] returns the answer and finishes the task. You will be
given context that you should use to help you answer the question. Start
your response with either Action or an indexed Thought
:::

::: {#A4.SS2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Here are some examples:
:::

::: {#A4.SS2.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Question: What is the elevation range for the area that the eastern
sector of the Colorado orogeny extends into?
:::

::: {#A4.SS2.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Let's think step by step.
:::

::: {#A4.SS2.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Thought 1: The eastern sector of Colorado orogeny extends into the High
Plains.
:::

::: {#A4.SS2.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Thought 2: High Plains rise in elevation from around 1,800 to 7,000 ft

:::

### [D.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Value Function Prompt {#d.3-value-function-prompt .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Analyze the trajectories of a solution to a question answering task. The
trajectories are labeled by environmental observations about the
situation, thoughts that can reason about the current situation and
actions that can be three types:
:::

::: {#A4.SS3.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(1\) Search\[entity\], which searches the exact entity on Wikipedia and
returns the first paragraph if it exists. If not, it will return some
similar entities to search.
:::

::: {#A4.SS3.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(2\) Lookup\[keyword\], which returns the next sentence containing
keyword in the current passage.
:::

::: {#A4.SS3.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
\(3\) Finish\[answer\], which returns the answer and finishes the task.
:::

::: {#A4.SS3.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Given a question and a trajectory, evaluate its correctness and provide
your reasoning and analysis in detail. Focus on the latest thought,
action, and observation. Incomplete trajectories can be correct if the
thoughts and actions so far are correct, even if the answer is not found
yet. Do not generate additional thoughts or actions. Then at the last
line conclude "Thus the correctness score is s", where s is an integer
from 1 to 10.
:::

::: {#A4.SS3.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Question: Which magazine was started first Arthur's Magazine or First
for Women?
:::

::: {#A4.SS3.p7 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}

find which was started first.
:::

Observation 1: Arthur's Magazine (18440̆0e20̆0800̆0931846) was an American
literary periodical published in Philadelphia in the 19th century.
Edited by Timothy Shay Arthur, it featured work by Edgar A. Poe, J.H.
Ingraham, Sarah Josepha Hale, Thomas G. Spear, and others.\[1\]\[2\] In
May 1846 it was merged into Godey's Lady's Book.\[3\]
:::

::: {#A4.SS3.p10 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
This trajectory is correct as it is reasonable to search for the first
magazine provided in the question. It is also better to have simple
searches corresponding to a single entity, making this the best action.
:::

::: {#A4.SS3.p11 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Thus the correctness score is 10
