::: ltx_abstract
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

Adversarial training (AT) with imperfect supervision is significant but
receives limited attention. To push AT towards more practical scenarios,
we explore a brand new yet challenging setting, i.e., AT with
complementary labels (CLs), which specify a class that a data sample
does not belong to. However, the direct combination of AT with existing
methods for CLs results in consistent failure, but not on a simple
baseline of two-stage training. In this paper, we further explore the
phenomenon and identify the underlying challenges of AT with CLs as
*intractable adversarial optimization* and *low-quality adversarial
examples*. To address the above problems, we propose a new learning
strategy using gradually informative attacks, which consists of two
critical components: 1) Warm-up Attack (Warm-up) gently raises the
adversarial perturbation budgets to ease the adversarial optimization
with CLs; 2) Pseudo-Label Attack (PLA) incorporates the progressively
informative model predictions into a corrected complementary loss.
Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of
our method on a range of benchmarked datasets. The code is publicly
available at:
[https://github.com/RoyalSkye/ATCL](https://github.com/RoyalSkye/ATCL){.ltx_ref
.ltx_url .ltx_font_typewriter target="_blank"}.
:::

## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para}
Deep neural networks are vulnerable to adversarial
examples \[[15](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref}\],
which motivates the development of various defensive
methods \[[30](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref};
[21](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref};
[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref};
[2](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}\]
to mitigate the arisen security issue. As one of the most effective and
practical defensive methods, adversarial training
(AT) \[[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}\]
has been widely
studied \[[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref};
[5](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref};
[10](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref}\].
Specifically, it formulates the problem as min-max optimization, which
generates adversarial data during training and minimizes the training
loss of the generated adversarial variants. In this way, the models are
equipped with adversarial robustness against human-imperceptible
perturbations within the neighborhood of inputs. Although AT with
perfect supervision (e.g., ordinary labels) has been thoroughly studied
by previous
works \[[25](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref};
[33](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref};
[44](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref};
[21](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref};
[34](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib34){.ltx_ref};
[43](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref};
[11](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref}\],
the more common learning scenarios with imperfect
supervision \[[45](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref};
[42](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref}\]
has only received limited attention.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para}
Complementary labels
(CLs) \[[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref}\],
which convey partial label information by identifying a class that a
data sample does not belong to, are one of those practical and promising
imperfect supervision in weakly supervised
learning \[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref};
[27](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref}\].
Several
works \[[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref};
[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref};
[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref};
[38](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref};
[14](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref}\]
recently focused on learning with CLs, where the model trained only with
CLs through their designed losses (i.e., complementary loss
$\overline{\ell}$) is expected to predict ordinary labels accurately
during inference. Their success illustrates the possibility of training
ordinary classifiers even when all the labels given for training are
wrong (except that the data and labels are statistically independent).
Due to the low acquisition cost and privacy preservability, it has been
applied to online
learning \[[19](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref}\]
and medical image
segmentation \[[31](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}\].
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para}
To push AT towards more practical scenarios, we explore a brand new yet
challenging setting, i.e., [AT with CLs]{#S1.p3.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}. In this paper, our interest is [how to equip machine
learning models with adversarial robustness when all given labels in a
dataset are wrong (i.e., CLs)]{#S1.p3.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic},
which is of scientific interests to both research areas of weakly
supervised learning and adversarial learning. To conduct AT with CLs, a
straightforward attempt is to regard $\overline{\ell}$ as the training
objective of the min-max
optimization \[[15](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref};
[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}\].
:::

::: {#S1.p4 .ltx_para}
However, the direct combination of AT with existing
methods \[[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref};
[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref};
[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]
for learning with CLs results in consistent failure, but not on a simple
baseline of two-stage training (as shown in Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
To be specific, we mainly consider the single complementary label (SCL)
with the uniform
assumption \[[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref};
[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\],
where labels other than the true label are chosen as a CL with the same
probability. In complementary learning, the complementary losses
$\overline{\ell}$ are obtained by certain correction
techniques \[[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref};
[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}\],
which guarantee the training with $\overline{\ell}$ on complementary
datasets achieves similar performance to the training with the ordinary
loss $\ell$ on ordinary datasets. In contrast, when conducting AT with
CLs, most experiments (as shown in Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
with different $\overline{\ell}$ and optimizers) consistently show
failed training curves except for a simple two-stage baseline, which
consists of a natural complementary learning phase and an AT phase using
the predicted labels of the first-stage model.
:::

::: {#S1.p5 .ltx_para}
The above fact motivated us to further investigate AT with CLs, and we
identified its unique challenges as *intractable adversarial
optimization* and *low-quality adversarial examples* (as verified in
Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
Specifically, we first theoretically prove that conducting AT with the
complementary risk is statistically consistent to that with the ordinary
risk when training with the general backward corrected
loss \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\].
Although the guarantee holds with sufficient data, it is not preserved
any more with a limited number of CLs given in
practice \[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\]
(see Eq. ([[8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.E8 "8 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
in Section [[4.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.SS1 "4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
Empirically, we analyze the training dynamics of AT with CLs (e.g.,
gradient directions and norms), and observe that adversarial
optimization with CLs through $\overline{\ell}$ suffers from large
gradient variance, with the gradient vanishing issue at the early stage
of training. Moreover, we also find that the complementary loss as the
objective of adversarial optimization fails to generate high-quality
adversarial examples due to the less informative
nature \[[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref}\]
of CLs and the implicit way of attacking ordinary labels (i.e.,
attacking
$\sum_{j \neq \overline{y}}{p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| x \right.)}}$
instead).
:::

::: {#S1.p6 .ltx_para}
To mitigate the problems, we propose a new learning strategy using
gradually informative attacks (in Algorithm [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#alg1 "Algorithm 1 ‣ 4.3 Methodology ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
for AT with CLs, which consists of two critical components, i.e.,
*Warm-up Attack (Warm-up)* and *Pseudo-Label Attack (PLA)*. We introduce
the Warm-up to ease the intractable optimization with CLs, which is
gradually transferred from natural training to AT by controlling the
adversarial budget. We design the PLA to improve the low-quality
adversarial generation, which incorporates the informative predictions
from the progressively discriminative model itself. Overall, we propose
a unified framework using a flexible scheduler to adjust two critical
components during training.
:::

## [2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Related Work {#related-work .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Complementary Learning. {#complementary-learning. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Learning with CLs was introduced by
\[[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref}\],
where the SCL with the uniform assumption was firstly considered. An
unbiased risk estimator (URE) was derived by modifying a specific loss
function (e.g., one-versus-all or pairwise comparison) that satisfies a
symmetric condition. Continuing with this work,
\[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\]
derived a general URE for arbitrary loss functions and models, and
further proposed non-negative correction and gradient ascent methods to
cope with the overfitting issue of URE in practice. Although URE has
good statistical properties, it has inferior empirical performance due
to huge gradient variance.
\[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\]
mitigated this problem by proposing a surrogate complementary loss
framework, which is a biased risk estimator trading zero bias with
reduced variance. Other than the uniform assumption,
\[[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}\]
considered the biased CLs, where labels other than the true label are
chosen as a CL with different probabilities due to the annotator's bias.
In addition to the SCL setting,
\[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]
derived an URE of the ordinary risk for multiple complementary labels,
and further improved it by minimizing properly chosen upper bounds.
Moreover, there are also several works less related to the loss
correction in complementary learning, such as introducing
regularization \[[38](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref}\]
or
reweighting \[[14](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref}\]
during the training process.
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Adversarial Training. {#adversarial-training. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
As one of the most effective defensive methods, AT has been widely
studied to improve the robustness of deep learning
models \[[1](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref};
[12](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref};
[37](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref};
[36](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref};
[26](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref};
[35](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref};
[6](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref}\].
The standard
AT \[[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}\]
formulated the problem as min-max optimization.
\[[4](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}\]
proposed CAT to mitigate the catastrophic forgetting and the
generalization issues of AT.
\[[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}\]
decomposed the prediction error into the natural and boundary errors,
and proposed TRADES to balance the classification performance between
the natural and adversarial examples.
\[[43](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref}\]
resolved the trade off between robustness and accuracy via reweighting
adversarial data by their geometrical information to the decision
boundary. In contrast to previous works, only limited effort has been
made to explore AT with imperfect
supervision \[[27](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref};
[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\].
\[[45](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref}\]
explored the interaction of AT with noisy labels (NLs), and found that
AT itself is NLs correction since the number of PGD steps could be used
to improve sample selection quality.
\[[42](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref}\]
investigated the effects of NLs injection on the adversarial
optimization, and proposed NoiLIn to mitigate the robust overfitting
issue based on the empirical observation that NLs are not always
detrimental.
:::
:::

::: {#S3 .section .ltx_section}
## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Preliminaries {#preliminaries .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we provide preliminaries for ordinary learning,
complementary learning and AT. For complementary learning, we consider
the SCL setting with the uniform
assumption \[[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref};
[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\].
For AT, we focus on the standard method proposed
in \[[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}\].
:::

::: {#S3.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Ordinary Learning {#ordinary-learning .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Let $\mathcal{X} \subset {\mathbb{R}}^{d}$ be the input space, and
$\mathcal{Y} \in {\lbrack K\rbrack}:={{\{ 1,2,\ldots,K\}}\operatorname{}{({K > 2})}}$
be the label space. The data ${\{{(x_{i},y_{i})}\}}_{i = 1}^{n}$ is
sampled independently and identically from the joint distribution
$\mathcal{D}$ with density $p\operatorname{}{(X,Y)}$, which could be
further decomposed into
$p\operatorname{}{(X)}\operatorname{}p\operatorname{}{(\left. Y \middle| X \right.)}$.
We define a class-probability function
${\eta_{i}\operatorname{}{(x)}} = {p\operatorname{}{({Y = \left. i \middle| X \right. = x})}}$.
The decision and loss functions are defined as
$g:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{K}}$ and
$\ell:{{\mathcal{Y} \times {\mathbb{R}}^{K}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{+}}$,
respectively. The goal of ordinary learning is to train a classifier
${f\operatorname{}{(x)}}:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y}}$, which is
expected to predict the correct label by
${\arg{\max_{i}g}}\operatorname{}{(x)}_{i}$. The ordinary risk is
defined as

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{R\operatorname{}{(g;\ell)}} = {E_{{(x,y)} \sim \mathcal{D}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{(y,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}\rbrack}}}.$$      [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Complementary Learning {#complementary-learning .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
In complementary learning, the label space is transformed into
$\overline{\mathcal{Y}}$, where each ordinary label $y$ is flipped into
$\overline{y} \in \overline{\mathcal{Y}}$ with a class-dependent
probability
$p\operatorname{}{(\left. \overline{y} \middle| y \right.)}$. Then, the
data is sampled from a different joint distribution
$\overline{\mathcal{D}}$ with density
$p\operatorname{}{(X,\overline{Y})}$. We assume the transition matrix
$Q \in {\mathbb{R}}^{K \times K}$ is invertible, where
$Q_{i\operatorname{}j} = {p\operatorname{}{({\overline{Y} = \left. j \middle| Y \right. = i})}}$.
With the uniform assumption, it takes 0 on diagonals and
$\frac{1}{K - 1}$ on non-diagonals. The class-probability function is
then written as

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{\overline{\eta}}_{i}\operatorname{}{(x)}} = {p\operatorname{}{({\overline{Y} = \left. i \middle| X \right. = x})}} = {\sum\limits_{j \neq i}{p\operatorname{}{({\overline{Y} = \left. i \middle| Y \right. = j})}\operatorname{}p\operatorname{}{({Y = \left. j \middle| X \right. = x})}}} = {\sum\limits_{j \neq i}{Q_{j\operatorname{}i}\operatorname{}\eta_{j}\operatorname{}{(x)}}}}.$$      [(2)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

The complementary risk is defined as
${\overline{R}\operatorname{}{(g;\overline{\ell})}} = {E_{{(x,\overline{y})} \sim \overline{\mathcal{D}}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}{(\overline{y},{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}\rbrack}}$,
where $\overline{\ell}$ is a complementary loss function. Typically, it
can be derived by the loss correction, which is a technique that ensures
risk or classifier consistency in the weakly supervised
setting \[[27](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref};
[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref}\].
The backward correction is a risk consistent algorithm that ensures
$\overline{R}$ is a statistically consistent risk estimator of the
ordinary risk $R$, while the forward correction is a classifier
consistent algorithm that guarantees the classifier learned from
$\overline{\mathcal{D}}$ using $\overline{\ell}$ converges to the
optimal one learned from $\mathcal{D}$ using $\ell$. In complementary
learning,
 \[[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref};
[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]
focused on the backward correction that multiplies the loss by
$Q^{- 1}$, while
\[[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}\]
proposed a forward correction method via multiplying the model
prediction by $Q$. Other methods (e.g., a bounded loss) can also be used
to derive complementary
losses \[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref};
[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\].
The detailed complementary loss functions are provided in Table
[[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A2.T2 "Table 2 ‣ Appendix B Loss Functions of Complementary Learning ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
of Appendix [[B]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A2 "Appendix B Loss Functions of Complementary Learning ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### General Backward Correction. {#general-backward-correction. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Among this line of research, a notable
work \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\]
derived an URE of the ordinary risk, without any restriction on the used
loss and model. Given the notations borrowed from
 \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\]:
${\ell\operatorname{}{({g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}} = {\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{(1,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}},{\ell\operatorname{}{(2,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}},\ldots,{\ell\operatorname{}{(K,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}\rbrack}$
and $e_{i}$ is the one-hot column vector with one on the $i_{th}$ entry,
the URE is derived as follows:
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Proposition 1. {#proposition-1. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
*The ordinary risk can be expressed in terms of CLs as follows,*

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{R\operatorname{}{(g;\ell)}} = {E_{{(x,y)} \sim \mathcal{D}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{(y,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}\rbrack}} = {E_{{(x,\overline{y})} \sim \overline{\mathcal{D}}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}{(\overline{y},{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}\rbrack}} = {\overline{R}\operatorname{}{(g;\overline{\ell})}}},$$      [(3)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

*when $\overline{\ell}$ is rewritten as*

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}{(\overline{y},{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}} = {e_{\overline{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}{(Q^{- 1})}\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}{({g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}},$$      [(4)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

*With the uniform assumption, $\overline{\ell}$ can be further rewritten
as*

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}{(\overline{y},{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}} = {{- {{({K - 1})}\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}{(\overline{y},{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}} + {\sum\limits_{j = 1}^{K}{\ell\operatorname{}{(j,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}}}}.$$      [(5)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

With this expression, we can obtain an URE of the ordinary risk only
from CLs.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Adversarial Training {#adversarial-training .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS3.p1 .ltx_para}
AT has been a commonly used defensive technique that equips deep
learning models with adversarial robustness against imperceptible
perturbations within a small neighborhood of input. Here, we consider
conducting AT with ordinary labels (e.g., $\ell$ is the cross-entropy
loss). Generally, it can be formulated into a min-max optimization
problem:

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{{\min\limits_{\theta}E_{{(x,y)} \sim \mathcal{D}}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{(y,{g\operatorname{}{(\overset{\sim}{x})}})}}\rbrack}},{\text{with}\operatorname{}\overset{\sim}{x}}} = {\arg{\max\limits_{{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}{\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{(y,{g\operatorname{}{({\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i})}})}}\rbrack}}}},$$      [(6)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where
${\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}} = {\{\overset{\sim}{x}:{{\parallel{x - \overset{\sim}{x}}\parallel}_{p} \leq \epsilon}\}}$
is the closed ball centered at input $x$ with radius $\epsilon > 0$
under $l_{p}$-norm threat models, and $\overset{\sim}{x}$ is the
adversarial data found within
$\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}$. Empirically,
the solution of inner maximization is approximated by projected gradient
descent (PGD) as follows:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${x^{({t + 1})} = {\Pi_{\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{x^{(t)} + {\alpha\operatorname{}\text{sign}\operatorname{}{({{\nabla_{x^{(t)}}\ell}\operatorname{}{(y,{g\operatorname{}{(x^{(t)})}})}})}}}\rbrack}}},$$      [(7)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $\alpha$ is the step size, $\Pi$ is the projection operator that
projects the adversarial data back to the epsilon ball, $x^{(t)}$ is the
adversarial data found at step $t$, and $x^{(0)}$ is initialized by
natural data or natural data with a small Gaussian or uniformly random
perturbation. The adversarial data is updated iteratively in the
direction of loss maximization until a stop criterion is satisfied.
:::
:::
:::

::: {#S4 .section .ltx_section}
## [4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels {#adversarial-training-with-complementary-labels .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S4.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we first provide some theoretical insights for AT with
CLs. Then, we empirically analyze the experimental failure and identify
the critical challenges as *intractable adversarial optimization* and
*low-quality adversarial examples* when conducting AT with corrected
complementary losses. Accordingly, we propose a new learning strategy
with gradually informative attacks.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Theoretical Analysis {#theoretical-analysis .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Without loss of generality, below we analyze the general backward
correction \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\]
theoretically, and other popular complementary loss functions on the SCL
setting.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Proposition 2. {#proposition-2. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
*For the general backward correction, conducting AT on the complementary
risk is equivalent to that on the ordinary risk. However, this is not
the case on their empirical risks:*

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $$\begin{aligned}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            [(8)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}E_{y \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{Y|X} = x})}}}}}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{(y,{g\operatorname{}{(\overset{\sim}{x})}})}}\rbrack}} & {{= {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}E_{\overline{y} \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{\overline{Y}|X} = x})}}}}}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}{(\overline{y},{g\operatorname{}{(\overset{\sim}{x})}})}}\rbrack}}},} \\      
     {{\min\limits_{\theta}\frac{1}{n}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}{\max\limits_{{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x_{i}\rbrack}}}{\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{(y_{i},{g\operatorname{}{({\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i})}})}}\rbrack}}}} & {{\neq {{\min\limits_{\theta}\frac{1}{n}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}{\max\limits_{{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x_{i}\rbrack}}}{\lbrack{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}{({\overline{y}}_{i},{g\operatorname{}{({\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i})}})}}\rbrack}}}}}.}                                                                                                        
     \end{aligned}$$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

The detailed proof is provided in Appendix [[A.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A1.SS2 "A.2 Proof of Proposition 2 ‣ Appendix A Proofs ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
In brief, when considering the general backward corrected loss, the
maximization induced by AT keeps the statistical properties of the
complementary risk, and it still yields a consistent risk estimator. In
theory, the adversarial optimization of the complementary risk with the
general backward corrected loss is statistically consistent to that of
the ordinary risk with the ordinary loss. But in practice, we can only
obtain limited CLs (e.g., the sample size of
$E_{\overline{y} \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{\overline{Y}|X} = x})}}}$ is
one on the SCL setting), which causes the inconsistency between their
empirical risks (i.e., the solutions to their inner maximization are
different), and hence leads to a difficult adversarial optimization (as
verified in Appendix [[D.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4.SS2 "D.2 Empirical Justification for Proposition 2 ‣ Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
The solution to inner maximization of the complementary risk is
equivalent to that of the ordinary risk if and only if maximizing the
weighted loss over all candidate CLs, that is
$E_{\overline{y} \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{\overline{Y}|X} = x})}}}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}{(\overline{y},{g\operatorname{}{(\overset{\sim}{x})}})}}\rbrack}$.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px1.p2 .ltx_para}
From the perspective of AT, we expect to find the adversarial examples
$\overset{\sim}{x}$ within the neighborhood of input $x$ that make the
model predictions on the ordinary label
$p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \overset{\sim}{x} \right.)}$
as low as possible. When we have ordinary labels, it is quite
straightforward to achieve this objective by maximizing the
cross-entropy
$\ell = {- {{\log p_{\theta}}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \overset{\sim}{x} \right.)}}}$.
However, if we further consider recent proposed methods in addition to
the general backward correction in complementary learning, most of
$\overline{\ell}$ mathematically try to maximize
$\sum_{j \in {\mathcal{Y} \smallsetminus {\{\overline{y}\}}}}{p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| \overset{\sim}{x} \right.)}}$
(or equivalently minimize
$p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. \overline{y} \middle| \overset{\sim}{x} \right.)}$)
as shown in Table [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A2.T2 "Table 2 ‣ Appendix B Loss Functions of Complementary Learning ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
Maximizing $\overline{\ell}$ leads to the minimization of
$\sum_{j \in {\mathcal{Y} \smallsetminus {\{\overline{y}\}}}}{p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| \overset{\sim}{x} \right.)}}$,
which cannot guarantee the minimization of
$p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \overset{\sim}{x} \right.)}$.
In such a case, the inner maximization may serve as a random attacker
without prior knowledge. Hence, corrected complementary losses fail to
achieve the objective of AT, and low-quality adversarial examples may be
generated, which is detailedly discussed in the following part.
:::

### [4.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Empirical Analysis {#empirical-analysis .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
We conduct the experiment on Kuzushiji to justify the previous
theoretical insights. For the upper panels of Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
we focus on the issue of *intractable adversarial optimization* by
adversarially training the model using several loss functions
separately, with three random trials. For the lower panels of Figure
[[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
we focus on the issue of *low-quality adversarial examples* by measuring
the different adversarial examples generated by various loss functions,
given the same optimization model (i.e., the *oracle* model, which is
trained using AT with ordinary labels). We show the average results over
different seeds and training samples, respectively. Without loss of
generality, here we only show the results of one complementary loss
(i.e.,
LOG \[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]),
and our proposed method (i.e., *Warm-up+PLA* in Section [[4.3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.SS3 "4.3 Methodology ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
The detailed experiment settings and more comprehensive empirical
results with other complementary losses and datasets are provided in
Appendix [[C]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A3 "Appendix C Empirical Analysis ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Intractable Adversarial Optimization. {#intractable-adversarial-optimization. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS2.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
In the outer minimization of AT, we first collect the gradient direction
(with respect to the model parameters) of each data in one mini-batch
(i.e., 256), and then compute the trace sum of their covariance matrix
(the average result for each epoch is shown in the upper-left panel of
Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
A larger value reflects a group of gradient directions with a bigger
variance. Note that the result of LOG nearly reaches the maximum trace
sum of covariance matrix of the gradient directions, which results in
its consistent failure at the early stage of training. We also compute
the average $l_{2}$-norm of stochastic gradients of the last and first
layer as shown in the upper-middle and upper-right panels of Figure
[[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
respectively. Although some gradient signals exist for LOG in the last
layer, the gradients back-propagated to the first layer is so small that
gradient vanishing problem occurs at the early stage of training. This
phenomenon is similar to that observed in the standard AT, which is
conjectured as the prevalence of dead neurons caused by a large radius
of epsilon
ball \[[23](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}\].
Moreover, considering AT with CLs, it is expected that the gradient norm
tends to be small since the observed variance of gradient directions in
one mini-batch is extremely large. The above observations indicate the
adversarial optimization with CLs is difficult, and it is rational to
use the method (i.e., our Warm-up+PLA) to ease the optimization.
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Low-quality Adversarial Examples. {#low-quality-adversarial-examples. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
In the inner maximization of AT, given the same optimization model, we
measure the quality of generated adversarial examples by computing the
cosine similarity of gradient directions (with respect to the same data)
and the $l_{1}$-distance of constructed adversarial examples, with the
oracle examples generated by ordinary label (in the lower-left and
lower-middle panels of Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
A larger cosine similarity and a lower $l_{1}$-distance indicate a
stronger resemblance to the oracle, but not necessarily a stronger
attack (or high-quality adversarial examples). Hence, we further compute
the difference of model predictions of natural data and adversarial data
on the ordinary label during training (in the lower-right panel of
Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
A bigger difference explicitly demonstrates a stronger attack. The
results show that the complementary loss as the objective of AT with CLs
fails to generate high-quality adversarial examples as our analysis in
Section [[4.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.SS1 "4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Methodology {#methodology .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS3.p1 .ltx_para}
To address the previous problems, we accordingly propose a new strategy
using gradually informative attacks to ease the intractable adversarial
optimization and improve the quality of adversarial examples,
respectively. Our algorithm is concretely presented in Algorithm
[[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#alg1 "Algorithm 1 ‣ 4.3 Methodology ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
Specifically, it consists of Warm-up Attack and Pseudo-Label Attack,
whose dynamics are controlled by a flexible scheduler:

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathbb{T}}{(a,e,E_{s})} = \left\{ \begin{array}{cl}                                                                 [(9)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
      & {{\frac{a}{2} \cdot {({1 - {\cos{({{\min{(\frac{e}{E_{s}},1)}} \cdot \pi})}}})}}\quad\text{Warm-up Attack,}} \\      
      & {{a \cdot {({1 - {\min{(\frac{e}{E_{s}},1)}}})}}\mspace{54mu}\text{Pseudo-Label Attack,}}                            
     \end{array} \right.$$                                                                                                   
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $a$ is the maximum value of the scheduled variable (i.e.,
$\epsilon_{\max} = \epsilon$ in Warm-up Attack and $\gamma_{\max} = 1$
in Pseudo-Label Attack), $e$ is the epoch index, and $E_{s}$ is the
duration of the scheduler. Both the proposed attacks can be controlled
by a flexible scheduler with the increasing and decreasing trends
respectively (e.g., see the example in Figure [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S5.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ Baseline. ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
Below, we describe each component in detail.

#### Warm-up Attack. {#warm-up-attack. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS3.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Adversarial optimization is harder compared with ordinary
optimization \[[23](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}\],
and CLs further strengthen the degree of hardness as observed in our
empirical analysis. The major difference of AT with CLs from the
standard AT is the labels used in the adversarial generation. As shown
in Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
training with the adversarial examples generated by different labels
results in the different gradient variance. Since the adversarial
generation on CLs induces large gradient variance which causes the
difficult adversarial optimization, we can adjust the adversarial
generation to control the variance introduced into the optimization. To
be specific, we adopt a dynamic adversarial budget to gradually enhance
the degree of adversarial generation. Typically, adversarial budgets
consist of the radius of epsilon ball $\epsilon$, number of attack steps
$k$, and step size $\alpha$. Here, we mainly focus on the dynamics of
$\epsilon$ in Eq. ([[9]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.E9 "9 ‣ 4.3 Methodology ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}).
The radius of the epsilon ball is increased from 0 to $\epsilon$ within
$E_{s}$ epochs, and is kept constant until the end of adversarial
optimization. The number of attack steps is constant ($k_{e} = k$), and
the step size is increased proportionally to $\epsilon$
($\alpha_{e} = {\frac{\epsilon_{e}}{\epsilon}\operatorname{}\alpha}$).
In this way, the gradually increased adversarial budget can ease the
difficulty of adversarial optimization.
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS3.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Pseudo-Label Attack. {#pseudo-label-attack. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS3.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
Due to the existence of an adversarial budget scheduler, at the early
stage of optimization, simple patterns may be prevalent in adversarial
data found within an extremely small epsilon ball, which is helpful for
the self-evolvement of a discriminative model. The model tends to assign
high confidence to the potential ordinary label, while low confidence to
others. This informative model prediction is a strong supplementary
information that is promising for improving the quality of the
adversarial generation. Therefore, we propose to optimize the objective
consisting of the convex combination outputs of partial labels (labels
other than CLs) and predicted ordinary label. This transforms the whole
process into an optimization that starts with learning with CLs using
certain correction techniques, and gradually moves to learning with
predicted labels regarding them as ordinary labels. The loss function
can be defined as follows:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}{(x,\overline{y};\theta)}} = {- {{({K - 1})}\operatorname{}{\log{({{\gamma\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{j \neq \overline{y}}{p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| x \right.)}}}} + {{({1 - \gamma})}\operatorname{}p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. \hat{y} \middle| x \right.)}}})}}}}},{\hat{y} = {{\arg{\max\limits_{j \neq \overline{y}}p_{c}}}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| x \right.)}}}},$$      [(10)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------

where $\gamma \in {\lbrack 0,1\rbrack}$ is a hyperparameter that
controls the transition of learning. Recent studies found that
adversarial training can degrade the generalization, and a trade-off
between natural accuracy and adversarial robustness
exists \[[32](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}\].
To avoid the effect of such degradation and stabilize the optimization
(e.g., a drastic shift of model predictions may occur due to the
increasing adversarial budget), we use a simple exponential moving
average to collect the dynamics of model predictions, and choose the
label with the maximum probability as a predicted ordinary label.
Specifically, given the model predictions
$p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(x)}$ of the current epoch, the cashed model
predictions $p_{c}\operatorname{}{(x)}$ is updated as
${p_{c}\operatorname{}{(x)}} = {{\beta\operatorname{}p_{c}\operatorname{}{(x)}} + {{({1 - \beta})}\operatorname{}p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(x)}}}$,
where $p_{c}\operatorname{}{(x)}$ is initialized with
$\frac{{({\text{𝟏} - e_{\overline{y}}})}^{T}}{K - 1}$,
${p_{c}\operatorname{}{(\left. \overline{y} \middle| x \right.)}} = 0$,
and $\beta = 0.9$.
:::

## [5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Experiments {#experiments .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S5.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we empirically verify the effectiveness of our method
on
MNIST \[[22](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref}\],
Kuzushiji \[[8](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref}\],
CIFAR10 \[[20](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib20){.ltx_ref}\]
and
SVHN \[[28](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}\]
datasets, and conduct ablation studies to understand the two attacks.
More detailed information and the conducted experiments are presented in
Appendix [[D]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4 "Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Setups. {#setups. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
1\) *General Setups*: for MNIST and Kuzushiji, we train a small
convolutional neural network, as used in
 \[[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}\],
for 100 epochs[^1^[[^1^[1]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_note}Note that AT is nearly
10-40
times \[[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref};
[40](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib40){.ltx_ref}\]
slower than the natural training due to the adversarial generation. Here
we train 100 epochs which is kept the same as the most literature in
AT \[[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref};
[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref};
[5](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref};
[29](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}\].]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote1
.ltx_note .ltx_role_footnote} with batch size of 256. For CIFAR-10 and
SVHN, ResNet-18 is trained for 120 epochs with batch size of 128. The
training sets of CIFAR-10 and SVHN are augmented with random cropping
and horizontal flipping. 2) *Adversarial Training Setups*: we focus on
the $L_{\infty}$ threat model. For MNIST and Kuzushiji, stochastic
gradient descent (SGD) is used with learning rate of 0.01 and momentum
of 0.9. PGD is used as the approximation method of solving inner
maximization, with $\epsilon = 0.3$, $\alpha = 0.01$ and $k = 40$. For
CIFAR10 and SVHN, SGD is used with weight decay of 5e-4 and momentum of
0.9. The initial learning rate is 0.01, and decayed by 10 at Epoch 30
and 60 of adversarial optimization. For the $L_{\infty}$ threat model,
$\epsilon = {8/255}$, $\alpha = {2/255}$ and $k = 10$. 3) *Complementary
Learning Setups*: following the settings in
 \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref};
[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\],
for MNIST and Kuzushiji, Adam optimizer is used with learning rate of
1e-3 and weight decay of 1e-4. For CIFAR10 and SVHN, SGD is used with
weight decay of 5e-4 and momentum of 0.9. The learning rate is linearly
increased to 0.01 within the first 5 epochs. More details are provided
in Appendix [[D.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4.SS1 "D.1 Extra Setups ‣ Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Baseline. {#baseline. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS0.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
1). Two-stage method: it first trains a model for 50 epochs with the
methods of complementary learning (i.e., LOG, which shows better
performance and stability compared to
others \[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]).
Then we re-label the complementary training set using the checkpoint
with the best validation performance, and adversarially train another
model using the re-labeled training set afterwards. 2). Conducting
adversarial training directly with complementary losses, including
FORWARD \[[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}\],
FREE,
NN \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\],
SCL_NL,
SCL_EXP \[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\],
EXP and
LOG \[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\].
We evaluate the adversarial robustness using PGD20, CW30 and AutoAttack
(AA) \[[9](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref}\],
and report the results of checkpoint with the best PGD20 test accuracy.
All experiments are conducted with multiple random seeds (i.e., 1-3).
:::

### [5.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Performance Evaluation {#performance-evaluation .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

just given for measuring the performance gap between AT with CLs and
ordinary labels. For easy datasets (e.g., MNIST), the complementary
losses are possible to obtain satisfying results, while most of them
fail on complicated datasets. We would like to mention that the
performance of the two-stage and our methods slightly outperform that of
the oracle on SVHN. We assume it may be attributed to the recent
observation that noisy datasets (with a small portion of noisy labels)
are not always detrimental to AT, and can even further boost the
adversarial

::: {#S5.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Ablation Study {#ablation-study .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
In this part, we conduct the ablation study to demonstrate the
importance of the two components, i.e., Warm-up Attack and Pseudo-Label
Attack in our method with the critical hyperparameter.
:::

::: {#S5.SS2.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Two Critical Components. {#two-critical-components. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS2.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
We investigate the roles of two components in our method by removing
them separately. We report the results within three trials on Kuzushiji
and CIFAR10 datasets as shown in the left-middle and right-middle panels
of Figure [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S5.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ Baseline. ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
In summary, warm-up attack could stabilize the optimization especially
when the model capacity are not sufficient large, and pseudo-label
attack is crucial for the achieving high adversarial robustness.
Specifically, the warm-up attack achieves quite good performance on
Kuzushiji, while limited improvements on CIFAR10. This is expected due
to the difficulty of CIFAR10 without further informative attacks.
Pseudo-label attack solves this problem by incorporating the informative
model predictions, and hence achieves quite good performance on CIFAR10.
However, it has an unstable result on Kusushiji, which is attributed to
the limited model capacity. It is of great importance to achieve
adversarial robustness given limited model capacity. Therefore, warm-up
attack is still a crucial component for stabilizing the optimization.
:::
:::

::: {#S5.SS2.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Hyperparameter. {#hyperparameter. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
We investigate the performance sensitivity to the key hyperparameter
$E_{s}$, which controls the growing speed of informative attacks. We
report the results within three trials on Kuzushiji as shown in the
right panels of Figure [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S5.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ Baseline. ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
The result shows a similar performance across a wide range of $E_{s}$,
which demonstrates that our method is not sensitive to the tuning of key
hyperparameter.

::: {#S6 .section .ltx_section}
## [6 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Conclusion {#conclusion .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S6.p1 .ltx_para}
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to study
adversarial training (AT) with complementary labels (CLs), and to
analyze its challenges from both theoretical and empirical perspectives.
To solve them, we proposed a new learning strategy using gradually
informative attacks, narrowing the performance gap between AT with CLs
and ordinary labels. Our work shed light on the applications of AT to a
more practical scenario (e.g., with imperfect supervision). In this
paper, we focused on the standard AT algorithm, which formulates the
problem as min-max optimization. We leave more advanced algorithms
(e.g., TRADES) and other complementary settings (e.g., multiple CLs) to
the future work. Moreover, CLs can be viewed as an extreme case of noisy
labels with 100% noise rate, or partial labels where the labels other
than the CL are the candidate set of labels. Hence, we hope our work
could provide new insights for AT with various imperfect supervision in
the ML community.
:::

### [A.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Proof of Proposition 1 {#a.1-proof-of-proposition-1 .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A1.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Given
${{\eta\operatorname{}{(x)}} = {p\operatorname{}{({\left. Y \middle| X \right. = x})}}},{{{\overline{\eta}\operatorname{}{(x)}} = {p\operatorname{}{({\left. \overline{Y} \middle| X \right. = x})}}},{{{\overline{\eta}\operatorname{}{(x)}} = {Q^{T}\operatorname{}\eta\operatorname{}{(x)}}},{{\ell\operatorname{}{({g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}} = {\lbrack{\ell\operatorname{}{(1,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}},{\ell\operatorname{}{(2,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}},\ldots,{\ell\operatorname{}{(K,{g\operatorname{}{(x)}})}}\rbrack}}}}$,
and $e_{i}$ is the one-hot column vector with one on the $i_{th}$ entry,
the unbiased risk estimator (URE) is derived as follows:

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $$\begin{array}{cl}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      [(11)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     {R\operatorname{}(g;\ell)} & {= {E_{{(x,y)} \sim \mathcal{D}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\ell\operatorname{}\left( y,{g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} \right\rbrack}} \\                                                                                                                                  
      & {= {E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}E_{y \sim {\eta\operatorname{}{(x)}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\ell\operatorname{}\left( y,{g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} \right\rbrack}} \\                                                                                                  
      & {= {E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\eta\operatorname{}(x)^{T}\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( {g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} \right\rbrack}} \\                                                                                                               
      & {= {E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\overline{\eta}\operatorname{}(x)^{T}\operatorname{}\left( Q^{- 1} \right)\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( {g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} \right\rbrack}} \\                                                               
      & {= {E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}E_{\overline{y} \sim {\overline{\eta}\operatorname{}{(x)}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {e_{\overline{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}\left( Q^{- 1} \right)\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( {g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} \right\rbrack}} \\      
      & {= {E_{{(x,\overline{y})} \sim \overline{\mathcal{D}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {e_{\overline{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}\left( Q^{- 1} \right)\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( {g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} \right\rbrack}} \\                                                                
      & {= {E_{{(x,\overline{y})} \sim \overline{\mathcal{D}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( \overline{y},{g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} \right\rbrack}} \\                                                                                                                
      & {{= {\overline{R}\operatorname{}\left( g;\overline{\ell} \right)}}.}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  
     \end{array}$$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------

We consider the single complementary label (SCL) setting with the
uniform assumption, where complementary labels (CLs) are sampled
uniformly from the candidate set $\mathcal{Y} \smallsetminus {\{ y\}}$,
and Q is a transition matrix taking 0 on diagonals and $\frac{1}{K - 1}$
on non-diagonals. The corrected loss $\overline{\ell}$ is then rewritten
as

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( \overline{y},{g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} = {e_{\overline{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}\left( Q^{- 1} \right)\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( {g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)} = {{- {\left( {K - 1} \right)\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( \overline{y},{g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)}} + {\sum\limits_{j = 1}^{K}{\ell\operatorname{}\left( j,{g\operatorname{}(x)} \right)}}}}.$$      [(12)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
:::

::: {#A1.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [A.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Proof of Proposition 2 {#a.2-proof-of-proposition-2 .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A1.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
For the general backward correction, based on Eq. [[11]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A1.E11 "11 ‣ A.1 Proof of Proposition 1 ‣ Appendix A Proofs ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
conducting adversarial training (AT) on the complementary risk is
equivalent to that on the ordinary risk:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $$\begin{array}{cl}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       [(13)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}E_{y \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{Y|X} = x})}}}}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\ell\operatorname{}\left( y,{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack} & {= {\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}\left\lbrack {\eta\operatorname{}(x)^{T}\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( {g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}}} \\      
      & {= {\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}\left\lbrack {\overline{\eta}\operatorname{}(x)^{T}\operatorname{}\left( Q^{- 1} \right)\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( {g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}}} \\                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              
      & {= {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}E_{\overline{y} \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{\overline{Y}|X} = x})}}}}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {e_{\overline{y}}^{T}\operatorname{}\left( Q^{- 1} \right)\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( {g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}} \\                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            
      & {{= {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}E_{\overline{y} \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{\overline{Y}|X} = x})}}}}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( \overline{y},{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}.}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            
     \end{array}$$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------

In AT with ordinary labels, given
${p\operatorname{}{({Y = \left. y \middle| X \right. = x})}} \approx 1$,
the empirical formulation is a proper estimator of the expected one:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $$\begin{array}{cl}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           [(14)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}E_{y \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{Y|X} = x})}}}}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\ell\operatorname{}\left( y,{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack} & {= {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{j = 1}^{K}\left\lbrack {p\operatorname{}\left( {Y = \left. j \middle| X \right. = x} \right)\operatorname{}\ell\operatorname{}\left( j,{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}} \\      
      & {\approx {\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}\left\lbrack {\ell\operatorname{}\left( y,{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}}} \\                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   
      & {{\approx {{\min\limits_{\theta}\frac{1}{n}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}{\max\limits_{{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x_{i}\rbrack}}}\left\lbrack {\ell\operatorname{}\left( y_{i},{g\operatorname{}\left( {\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}}}.}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             
     \end{array}$$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------

While in AT with CLs,
${p\operatorname{}{({\overline{Y} = \left. j \middle| X \right. = x})}} = {\sum_{k \neq j}{p\operatorname{}{({\overline{Y} = \left. j \middle| Y \right. = k})}\operatorname{}p\operatorname{}{({Y = \left. k \middle| X \right. = x})}}} \approx {p\operatorname{}{({\overline{Y} = \left. j \middle| Y \right. = y})}} \approx Q_{y\operatorname{}j}$,
which takes $\frac{1}{K - 1}$ if $j \neq y$ and 0 otherwise. Therefore,
the empirical formulation is not a proper estimator of the expected one
on the SCL setting with the uniform assumption:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $$\begin{array}{cl}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             [(15)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}E_{\overline{y} \sim {p\operatorname{}{({{\overline{Y}|X} = x})}}}}}}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( \overline{y},{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack} & {= {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{j = 1}^{K}\left\lbrack {p\operatorname{}\left( {\overline{Y} = \left. j \middle| X \right. = x} \right)\operatorname{}\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( j,{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}} \\      
      & {\approx {{\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{j \neq y}\left\lbrack {Q_{y\operatorname{}j}\operatorname{}\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( j,{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}} \\                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               
      & {\neq {\min\limits_{\theta}{E_{x \sim {p\operatorname{}{(X)}}}\operatorname{}{\max\limits_{\overset{\sim}{x} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x\rbrack}}}\left\lbrack {\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( \overline{y},{g\operatorname{}\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}}} \\                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  
      & {{\neq {{\min\limits_{\theta}\frac{1}{n}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}{\max\limits_{{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x_{i}\rbrack}}}\left\lbrack {\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( {\overline{y}}_{i},{g\operatorname{}\left( {\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}}}.}                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          
     \end{array}$$                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------

Based on Eqs. [[13]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A1.E13 "13 ‣ A.2 Proof of Proposition 2 ‣ Appendix A Proofs ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
[[14]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A1.E14 "14 ‣ A.2 Proof of Proposition 2 ‣ Appendix A Proofs ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
and [[15]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A1.E15 "15 ‣ A.2 Proof of Proposition 2 ‣ Appendix A Proofs ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
the inequality holds between their empirical formulations:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{\min\limits_{\theta}\frac{1}{n}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}{\max\limits_{{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x_{i}\rbrack}}}\left\lbrack {\ell\operatorname{}\left( y_{i},{g\operatorname{}\left( {\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}} \neq {{\min\limits_{\theta}\frac{1}{n}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}{\max\limits_{{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \in {\mathcal{B}_{\epsilon}\operatorname{}{\lbrack x_{i}\rbrack}}}\left\lbrack {\overline{\ell}\operatorname{}\left( {\overline{y}}_{i},{g\operatorname{}\left( {\overset{\sim}{x}}_{i} \right)} \right)} \right\rbrack}}}}.$$      [(16)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------

## [Appendix B ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Loss Functions of Complementary Learning {#appendix-b-loss-functions-of-complementary-learning .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A2.p1 .ltx_para}
Table [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A2.T2 "Table 2 ‣ Appendix B Loss Functions of Complementary Learning ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
lists the popular loss functions in the literature of complementary
learning, where ${\overline{Y}}_{s}$ is the set of multiple
complementary labels (MCLs). FORWARD and FREE are the complementary loss
functions derived by the forward
correction \[[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}\]
and the general backward
correction \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\]
techniques, respectively.
NN \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\]
is a non-negative correction method for fixing the overfitting issue of
FREE in practice, by (lower) bounding the losses of all classes to 0.
SCL_NL and
SCL_EXP \[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\]
are the methods for reducing empirical gradient variance by introducing
a little bias. The above-mentioned are designed for the SCL setting,
while EXP and
LOG \[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]
are the bounded losses that could be used on both the SCL and MCLs
settings.

### [C.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Gradient Norm {#c.1-gradient-norm .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A3.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
We adversarially train a model with several complementary losses
separately on Kuzushiji. Figure [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A3.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ C.1 Gradient Norm ‣ Appendix C Empirical Analysis ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
shows the (average and the corresponding single) $l_{2}$-Norm of
stochastic gradients with respect to the model parameters on three
random seeds. The results show the same observation (as in Section
[[4.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.SS2 "4.2 Empirical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
that the gradient vanishing problem tends to occur at the early stage of
adversarial optimization when using the complementary losses. Although
FREE seems to have large gradient norm, it suffers from the huge
empirical gradient variance
problem \[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\]
due to the fixed single complementary label given in practice, and hence
inferior performance in both complementary learning and AT with CLs.
:::

## [Appendix D ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Experiments {#appendix-d-experiments .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A4.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [D.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Extra Setups {#d.1-extra-setups .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS1.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Setups for Performance Evaluation. {#setups-for-performance-evaluation. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#A4.SS1.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Following the adversarial training setups (in Section [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S5 "5 Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}),
for MNIST and Kuzushiji, our method starts with natural complementary
learning for $E_{i} = 10$ epochs (i.e., an initial period to discard the
less informative model predictions at the beginning of optimization).
Then, the warm-up attack (Warm-up) and pseudo-label attack (PLA) are
introduced with $E_{s} = 50$ (i.e.,
${\mathbb{T}}\operatorname{}{({{\epsilon_{\max} = {0.3,{e - E_{i}}}},{E_{s} = 50}})}$
and
${\mathbb{T}}\operatorname{}{({{\gamma_{\max} = {1,{e - E_{i}}}},{E_{s} = 50}})}$,
respectively). For CIFAR10 and SVHN, $E_{i} = 40$ and $E_{s} = 40$. An
example of their dynamics adopted on MNIST/Kuzushiji is illustrated in
the left panel of Figure [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S5.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ Baseline. ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
Moreover, a small adversarial budget could be viewed as an implicit way
of data augmentation, while a large adversarial budget tends to
sacrifice the generalization for adversarial
robustness \[[32](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}\],
hence we heuristically stop the update of cashed model predictions
$p_{c}$ as long as the radius of epsilon ball exceeds $\epsilon/2$. For
the baselines of AT with CLs, the two-stage method consists of a
complementary learning phase and an AT phase, following the setups of
complementary learning setups and AT setups (in Section [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S5 "5 Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}),
respectively. For the direct combinations of AT with CLs on
MNIST/Kuzushiji, we set the learning rates of
FORWARD \[[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref}\],
FREE,
NN \[[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\],
SCL_NL,
SCL_EXP \[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\],
EXP and
LOG \[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]
to 0.1, 0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 0.05, 0.01 and 0.01, respectively. For CIFAR10
and SVHN, their learning rates are set to 0.01. All experiments are
conducted on NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090.
:::
:::

::: {#A4.SS1.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Setups for Ablation Study. {#setups-for-ablation-study. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#A4.SS1.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
For Kuzushiji, all methods follow the previous settings (i.e.,
$E_{i} = 10$, $E_{s} = 50$, $\gamma_{\max} = 1$,
$\epsilon_{\max} = 0.3$, $\alpha = 0.01$ and $K = 40$). For W/O Warm-up,
$\gamma = {{\mathbb{T}}\operatorname{}{(\gamma_{\max},{e - E_{i}},E_{s})}}$,
and $\epsilon_{e},\alpha_{e}$, and $k_{e}$ are fixed to
$\epsilon_{\max}$, $\alpha$ and $k$, respectively. Note that a suddenly
shift of $\gamma$ from 1 to 0 would simply cause the failure of
training. For W/O PLA,
$\epsilon_{e} = {{\mathbb{T}}\operatorname{}{(\epsilon_{\max},{e - E_{i}},E_{s})}}$,
$\alpha_{e} = {\frac{\epsilon_{e}}{\epsilon_{\max}} \times \alpha}$,
$k_{e} = k$ and $\gamma = 1$. For CIFAR10, $E_{i} = 40$, $E_{s} = 40$,
$\gamma_{\max} = 1$, $\epsilon_{\max} = {8/255}$, $\alpha = {2/255}$ and
$k = 10$.
:::

### [D.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Empirical Justification for Proposition 2 {#d.2-empirical-justification-for-proposition-2 .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
Briefly, the proposition 2 (Eq. [[8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.E8 "8 ‣ Proposition 2. ‣ 4.1 Theoretical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
and its proof (Appendix [[A.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A1.SS2 "A.2 Proof of Proposition 2 ‣ Appendix A Proofs ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
state that the intractable adversarial optimization is attributed to the
limited CLs given in practice. Here, we conduct an experiment to justify
it by giving MCLs (e.g., MCLs-3 represents that each data is assigned
with three CLs) to each data. We conduct AT directly using the
complementary loss on Kuzushiji following the previous setups. As the
left panel of Figure [[8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4.F8 "Figure 8 ‣ Setups for Ablation Study. ‣ D.1 Extra Setups ‣ Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
shown, the adversarial optimization tends to be stable and comparable to
the oracle as the number of CLs increasing, which further justifies our
proposition.
:::

### [D.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Comparison with TRADES {#d.3-comparison-with-trades .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS3.p1 .ltx_para}
TRADES \[[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}\]
is an advanced AT algorithm without the needs of ordinary labels during
the adversarial generation. Therefore, we adopt it to the AT with CLs,
and conduct an experiment on Kuzushiji. Specifically, we generate
adversarial examples (inner maximization) using their original
implementation[^4^[[^4^[4]{.ltx_tag
.ltx_tag_note}[https://github.com/yaodongyu/TRADES](https://github.com/yaodongyu/TRADES){.ltx_ref
.ltx_url .ltx_font_typewriter
target="_blank"}]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote4
.ltx_note .ltx_role_footnote}, while optimizing the training loss (outer
minimization) using several variants: 1) *TRADES*: after the initial
period $E_{i} = 10$, we optimize the model using the original TRADES
loss with ${1/\lambda} = 1$, and regard predicted labels (i.e.,
${\arg{\max_{j \neq \overline{y}}p_{c}}}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| x \right.)}$)
as ordinary labels; 2) *TRADES-LOG*: we directly use the complementary
loss (i.e., LOG) to optimize the model; 3) *TRADES-PLA*: we still regard
predicted labels as ordinary labels, but using Eq. [[10]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.E10 "10 ‣ Pseudo-Label Attack. ‣ 4.3 Methodology ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
as the objective of outer minimization. As the middle panel of Figure
[[8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4.F8 "Figure 8 ‣ Setups for Ablation Study. ‣ D.1 Extra Setups ‣ Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
shown, the results are not comparable to ours. A specifically designed
loss function may be needed, and we would leave it to the future work.

### [D.4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Gradually Informative Attacks with Other Complementary Losses {#d.4-gradually-informative-attacks-with-other-complementary-losses .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS4.p1 .ltx_para}
We also try to incorporate our proposed method into other complementary
losses (i.e.,
EXP \[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\],
SCL_NL and
SCL_EXP \[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\]),

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{{\overline{\ell}}_{\text{EXP}}\operatorname{}{(x,\overline{y};\theta)}} = {{({K - 1})}\operatorname{}{\exp{({{- {\gamma\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{j \neq \overline{y}}{p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| x \right.)}}}}} - {{({1 - \gamma})}\operatorname{}p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. \hat{y} \middle| x \right.)}}})}}}},{\hat{y} = {{\arg{\max\limits_{j \neq \overline{y}}p_{c}}}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| x \right.)}}}}.$$      [(17)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{{\overline{\ell}}_{\text{SCL\_NL}}\operatorname{}{(x,\overline{y};\theta)}} = {- {\log{({{\gamma\operatorname{}{({1 - {p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. \overline{y} \middle| x \right.)}}})}} + {{({1 - \gamma})}\operatorname{}p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. \hat{y} \middle| x \right.)}}})}}}},{\hat{y} = {{\arg{\max\limits_{j \neq \overline{y}}p_{c}}}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| x \right.)}}}}.$$      [(18)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{{{\overline{\ell}}_{\text{SCL\_EXP}}\operatorname{}{(x,\overline{y};\theta)}} = {\exp{({{\gamma\operatorname{}{({p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. \overline{y} \middle| x \right.)}})}} - {{({1 - \gamma})}\operatorname{}p_{\theta}\operatorname{}{(\left. \hat{y} \middle| x \right.)}}})}}},{\hat{y} = {{\arg{\max\limits_{j \neq \overline{y}}p_{c}}}\operatorname{}{(\left. j \middle| x \right.)}}}}.$$      [(19)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------

Following the previous setups, we conduct experiments on Kuzushiji.
Taking EXP as an example, as shown in the right panel of Figure
[[8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4.F8 "Figure 8 ‣ Setups for Ablation Study. ‣ D.1 Extra Setups ‣ Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref},
our method (i.e., Warm-up+PLA (EXP)) could still greatly ease the
adversarial optimization and improve the adversarial robustness, though
it is not comparable with the one with LOG. The performance gap between
LOG and EXP may be attributed to the curvatures and optimizers. We
summarize the comprehensive ablation study results in Table
[[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4.T3 "Table 3 ‣ D.3 Comparison with TRADES ‣ Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}[^5^[[^5^[5]{.ltx_tag
.ltx_tag_note}The Warm-up is readily to combine with any complementary

.ltx_note .ltx_role_footnote} The results (e.g., Figure [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S5.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ Baseline. ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
(b-c) and Table [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4.T3 "Table 3 ‣ D.3 Comparison with TRADES ‣ Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref})
demonstrate the benefits of our proposed unified framework, which
naturally combines and adaptively controls the two indispensable
components throughout the adversarial optimization with CLs. The two
indispensable components have different motivations and underlying
principles with other related
literature \[[16](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref};
[23](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}\],
and are closely related to the unique challenges identified in AT with
CLs. Without either of them and a reasonable scheduler, the adversarial
optimization with CLs will be extremely unstable and result in a model
with poor robustness, or even failure.

### [D.7 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Ablation Study on Strategies of Warm-up Attack {#d.7-ablation-study-on-strategies-of-warm-up-attack .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#A4.SS7.p1 .ltx_para}
In the main paper, we implement the Warm-up attack (i.e.,
Warm-up($\epsilon\operatorname{}\alpha$)+PLA) by controlling the radius
of the epsilon ball $\epsilon$ and step size $\alpha$, while fixing the
number of attack steps $k$. Here, we conduct experiments on Kuzushiji,
with two more strategies of Warm-up attack: 1) Warm-up($k$)+PLA: we only
change the number of attack steps based on the same scheduler described
in Section [[4.3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.SS3 "4.3 Methodology ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref};
2) Warm-up($\epsilon\operatorname{}k$)+PLA: similar to the original
implementation, we proportionally increase $k$
($k_{e} = {\lceil{\frac{\epsilon_{e}}{\epsilon}\operatorname{}k}\rceil}$)
instead of the step size based on the dynamics of epsilon ball. As the
middle panel of Figure [[9]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#A4.F9 "Figure 9 ‣ D.5 Multiple Complementary Labels ‣ Appendix D Experiments ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}
shown, the performace of Warm-up($\epsilon\operatorname{}k$)+PLA is
comparable to that of Warm-up($\epsilon\operatorname{}\alpha$)+PLA.
However, Warm-up($k$)+PLA tends to unstabilize the adversarial
optimization in some runs, which may be caused by the huge gradient
variance in the big epsilon ball as our empirical analysis in Section
[[4.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2211.00269#S4.SS2 "4.2 Empirical Analysis ‣ 4 Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels ‣ Adversarial Training with Complementary Labels: On the Benefit of Gradually Informative Attacks"){.ltx_ref}.
In practice, Warm-up($\epsilon\operatorname{}k$)+PLA may be a good
choice considering both the performance and computational efficiency.

## [Appendix E ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_appendix}Broader Impact {#appendix-e-broader-impact .ltx_title .ltx_title_appendix}

::: {#A5.p1 .ltx_para}
Adversarial training (AT) is one of the most effective defensive methods
against human-imperceptible perturbations. Although AT (with perfect
supervision) has been thoroughly studied recently, the study of AT with
imperfect supervision is an unexplored yet significant direction. In
this paper, we study AT with complementary labels (CLs). From both
theoretical and empirical perspectives, we identity the challenges of AT
with CLs. To solve the problems, we propose a new learning strategy
using gradually informative attacks, and reduce the performance gap of
AT with ordinary labels and CLs. Since we introduce extra operations on
the basis of AT with CLs for addressing the algorithmic issues, the
computation cost may not be friendly to our environment. In this paper,
we mainly focus on the SCL with the uniform assumption. Other
complementary settings (e.g., biased CLs or MCLs) are worthy of
attention as well. Although we take a step forward in AT with imperfect
supervision, there are many other practical tasks needed to be explored
further (e.g., noisy labels or partial labels).
:::
