::: ltx_abstract
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

Mixup is a commonly adopted data augmentation technique for image
classification. Recent advances in mixup methods primarily focus on
mixing based on saliency. However, many saliency detectors require
intense computation and are especially burdensome for parameter-heavy
transformer models. To this end, we propose TokenMixup, an efficient
attention-guided token-level data augmentation method that aims to
maximize the saliency of a mixed set of tokens. TokenMixup provides
$\times 15$ faster saliency-aware data augmentation compared to
gradient-based methods. Moreover, we introduce a variant of TokenMixup
which mixes tokens within a single instance, thereby enabling
multi-scale feature augmentation. Experiments show that our methods
significantly improve the baseline models' performance on CIFAR and
ImageNet-1K, while being more efficient than previous methods. We also
reach state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-100 among from-scratch
transformer models. Code is available at
[[https://github.com/mlvlab/TokenMixup]{#id2.1.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_typewriter}.](https://github.com/mlvlab/TokenMixup){.ltx_ref
.ltx_href target="_blank"}
:::

## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para}
Various data augmentation methods have been proposed for computer vision
tasks. One of the most successful augmentation methods is
mixup [[zhang2017mixup]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}
, which is commonly applied to image classification. It attempts to
augment the input by taking a convex combination of two random
instances, and by reassigning the ground truth label correspondingly.
Building on mixup, many subsequent works [[yun2019cutmix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}
; [[kim2020puzzle]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}
; [[kim2021co]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}
; [[uddin2020saliencymix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}
; [[verma2019manifold]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref}
; [[venkataramanan2021alignmix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}
; [[hong2021stylemix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref}
; [[dabouei2021supermix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref}
have been introduced. They focus on mixing instances in a more
meaningful way ([e.g.]{#S1.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic},
saliency-aware mixup, manifold level mixup, submodular diversity
maximization [etc.]{#S1.p1.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}), while a
great majority attempts to perform mixup based on saliency.\
These saliency-aware mixup methods often entail gradient computation. To
detect salient regions, the input is forward-propagated once and
back-propagated to extract the gradient map. However, such a mechanism
tends to be computationally heavy. This is especially pertinent to
transformer-based models [[vaswani2017attention]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref}
, as they generally retain high parameter volume. Keeping this in mind,
we aim to present a saliency-aware mixup method favorable for
transformers. Specifically, we regard self-attention as the inherent
saliency detector, which serves as an efficient means for augmentation.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para}
We accordingly propose [TokenMixup]{#S1.p2.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}, an efficient attention-guided token-level mixup method.
Its objective is to mix intermediate token sets so that the saliency
level of a batch is maximized. To detect salient tokens, we take
advantage of the attention map to achieve a $\times 15$ speed-up
compared to the gradient-based saliency estimator, without compromising
model performance. The batch saliency is maximized by optimally matching
instance pairs, accomplished by an algorithm that exactly solves the
optimization problem. We also introduce ScoreNet, a simple module that
measures the difficulty of an instance. Based on ScoreNet, TokenMixup is
applied selectively with respect to sample difficulty, which can be
viewed as a type of curriculum learning method. To the best of our
knowledge, this is the first attempt in the literature to discuss mixup
from a curriculum learning perspective. Furthermore, we present a novel
mixup approach that combines intermediate tokens across transformer
layers within a single instance. We name this Vertical TokenMixup (VTM)
since it [vertically]{#S1.p2.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} mixes
tokens to provide rich multi-scale information to the attention layer.
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para}
Experiments on image classification datasets imply that our methods are
both effective and efficient. Also, an advantage of TokenMixup is that
it can be used in parallel with other mixup methods. In combination with
those methods, we improve our baselines' performance by significant
margins on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-1K. Especially for
CIFAR-100, we achieve a new state-of-the-art performance among
transformer-based models that do not use pre-trained models.
:::

::: {#S1.p4 .ltx_para}
Then, our contributions are fourfold:

-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i1}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    We propose TokenMixup, an efficient attention-guided token-level
    mixup for transformers. By incorporating the self-attention map, we
    achieve $\times 15$ faster saliency-aware data augmentation compared
    to gradient-based methods.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i2}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    We incorporate curriculum learning into mixup, to adaptively perform
    TokenMixup in an intermediate layer based on the confidence score of
    ScoreNet.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i3}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i3.p1 .ltx_para}
    We also studied a variant, Vertical TokenMixup, which performs mixup
    with a single sample and enables multi-scale feature augmentation.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S1.I1.i4}

    ::: {#S1.I1.i4.p1 .ltx_para}
    We achieve state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-100 among
    transformer models trained from scratch. We also gain significant
    improvements over baseline models for image classification.
    :::
:::

## [2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Related Works {#related-works .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Mixup. {#mixup. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Input Mixup [[zhang2017mixup]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}
is a data augmentation method widely adopted for image classification.
The classification model is trained with a convex combination of input
images and labels. A special case of Input Mixup is
CutMix [[yun2019cutmix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}
, which can be seen as a pixel-wise mixup method with binary masks.
Recent advances in these mixup methods mainly focus on appropriately
utilizing saliency information, and on mixing in image feature levels.\
The core motivation of saliency-based mixup is that salient regions
should be preserved when mixed, to retain a sufficient amount of
information and learn more natural feature representations.
SaliencyMix [[uddin2020saliencymix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}
adopts various saliency detectors to directly extract salient regions.
Puzzle Mix [[kim2020puzzle]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}
attempts to mix images using saliency information while retaining local
statistics. Co-Mixup [[kim2021co]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}
maximizes gradient-based saliency while encouraging supermodular
diversity of the mixed images. SuperMix [[dabouei2021supermix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref}
takes advantage of supervised signals to mix input images based on
saliency.\
On the other hand, Manifold Mixup [[verma2019manifold]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref}
has provided theoretical grounds on the advantages of mixing images in
the higher level of features. Manifold mixing helps the model learn
flatter representation and smoother decision boundaries. Moreover, it is
noted that it enhances robustness to adversarial attacks. Recently,
AlignMix [[venkataramanan2021alignmix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}
was proposed to further align geometric properties of objects by mixing
instances in the feature space. Furthermore,
StyleMix [[hong2021stylemix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref}
proposed to manipulate content and style information disparately to
generate more abundant and robust samples.
:::
:::

## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Methods {#methods .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.p1 .ltx_para}
Here, we introduce TokenMixup, a simple but effective mixup method for
transformer models. The goal of our method is to augment intermediate
tokens while maximizing the saliency level. This can be accomplished in
three major steps: 1) Sample difficulty assessment, 2) Attention-guided
saliency detection & optimal assignment, 3) Token-level mixup. The
following subsections provide detailed descriptions of the steps.
:::

::: {#S3.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Sample Difficulty Assessment {#sample-difficulty-assessment .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
We first assess the difficulty of the input to adaptively decide whether
to apply augmentation. To evaluate sample difficulty, a measuring
function $\mathcal{F}$ needs to be defined. We utilize a parameterized
module, ScoreNet, which is a simple MLP that predicts the target value
$Y^{(i)}$ based on the intermediate tokens $X^{(i)}$, where $i$ is the
index of the sample in a batch [(See the supplement for
figure)]{#S3.SS1.p1.4.1 .ltx_text style="color:#000000;"}. Given the
ScoreNet output, the difficulty score is evaluated with the prediction
loss computed as

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\mathcal{F}\operatorname{}{(X^{(i)},Y^{(i)})}} = {\text{CrossEntropy}\operatorname{}{({\text{ScoreNet}\operatorname{}{(X^{(i)})}},Y^{(i)})}}}.$$      [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

If the score is greater than a threshold $\tau$, the sample is deemed
sufficiently hard and no mixup is performed. On the other hand, the set
of [easy]{#S3.SS1.p1.7.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} samples with
${\mathcal{F}\operatorname{}{(X^{(i)},Y^{(i)})}} < \tau$ denoted as
$\overset{\sim}{X}$ will be augmented by Token-level mixup after pairing
with other samples in a mini-batch, as illustrated in
Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}.
Note that ScoreNet is trained simultaneously with the main model and
training ScoreNet to assess sample difficulty at the intermediate layer
can be viewed as an auxiliary task.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Attention-guided Saliency Detection & Optimal Assignment {#attention-guided-saliency-detection-optimal-assignment .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
Several works [[heo2021rethinking]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref}
; [[rao2021dynamicvit]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref}
; [[roh2021sparse]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}
; [[wang2021pnp]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref}
have discussed the imbalanced information of tokens. Due to this
imbalance, random token mixing would potentially cause significant
information loss and meaningless token replacements (See the supplement
for relevant analysis). Thus, we aim to mix tokens based on saliency.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Attention-guided saliency detection. {#attention-guided-saliency-detection. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Instead of the computationally heavy gradient-based saliency
detectors [[kim2020puzzle]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}
; [[kim2021co]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}
, we take advantage of the attention map, an inherent saliency
approximator within transformer modules. Specifically, we can infer the
saliency of the tokens in the $i^{t\operatorname{}h}$ layer via
Attention Rollout [[abnar2020quantifying]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}
, which computes the attention imposed on the tokens from layer $i$ to
$i + \ell$ ($\ell \geq 0$) as

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${A = {{\Phi^{(i)} \cdot \Phi^{({i + 1})}}\operatorname{}\cdots\operatorname{}\Phi^{({i + \ell})}}},$$      [(2)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

such that
$\Phi^{(i)} = {\frac{1}{H}\operatorname{}{\sum_{h = 1}^{H}\Phi_{h}^{(i)}}}$
where $H$ is the number of heads in the multi-head attention layer, and
$\Phi_{h}^{(i)}$ is the $h^{t\operatorname{}h}$ attention head of layer
$i$. In order to retrieve $A$, the samples need to be propagated for the
subsequent $\ell$ layers with stop-gradient. Since each additional
forward propagation is an overhead, we approximate Attention Rollout by
setting $\ell = 0$. That is, we only use the attention map of the
following layer as the saliency estimator. We find this a sufficient
approximation of the full Attention Rollout. See
section [[5.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S5.SS1 "5.1 Soundness of Saliency Estimation ‣ 5 Analysis ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}
for analysis on the soundness of the approximated saliency map. Then,
the saliency score $S_{t}$ can be computed as

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${S_{t} = {\frac{1}{n}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}A_{i,t}}}},$$      [(3)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $t = {1,2,\ldots,n}$ denotes the token index.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Optimal assignment. {#optimal-assignment. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
Based on the estimated saliency of tokens, we aim to maximize the
overall saliency level by optimally assigning a different mixup target
for each instance. So, we first define
$P_{i,j} \in {I\operatorname{}R^{n}}$ as the saliency difference between
a random instance pair $(i,j)$, which is computed as

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{P_{i,j}\operatorname{}\overset{\bigtriangleup}{=}\operatorname{}S_{j}} - S_{i}},$$      [(4)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where ${{i,j} = 1},{2,\ldots,b}$ and $S_{i} \in {I\operatorname{}R^{n}}$
is the token saliency map from the $i^{t\operatorname{}h}$ instance.
Then, we represent our objective as an optimization problem written as

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\max\limits_{\sigma \in \mathcal{M}}\max\limits_{r_{i} \in {I\operatorname{}B^{n}}}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{b}{{({P_{i,{\sigma\operatorname{}{(i)}}} - \rho})}^{\top}\operatorname{}r_{i}}}},$$      [(5)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $\mathcal{M}$ is the set of all possible batch permutations of the
$b$ instances, and $\sigma \in \mathcal{M}$ refers to an arbitrary
permutation. $\rho$ is the threshold hyperparameter that controls the
minimum saliency gain required for a token to be mixed, and $r_{i}$ is a
binary decision vector for the $i^{t\operatorname{}h}$ instance.
[i.e.]{#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p1.17.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, token $t$
of $i$ is replaced with token $t$ of $\sigma\operatorname{}{(i)}$ when
$r_{i,t} = 1$, and preserved when
$r_{i,t} = {0\operatorname{}{({t = {1,2,\ldots,n}})}}$.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p2 .ltx_para}
The above optimization problem can be exactly solved by utilizing the
Hungarian Matching algorithm [[munkres1957algorithms]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}
. The matching algorithm requires the score matrix,
$C \in {I\operatorname{}R^{b \times b}}$, which is computed as

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${C_{i,j} = {\sum\limits_{t = 1}^{n}{\text{max}\operatorname{}{({P_{i,j}^{(t)} - \rho},\:0)}}}},$$      [(6)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where each item in $C_{i,j}$ refers to the maximum saliency gain
resulting from mixing ${\overset{\sim}{X}}^{(i)}$ and $X^{(j)}$. The
actual maximum gain will be reached when $\rho = 0$, but we set a
positive threshold to control the minimum saliency gain required for
each token to be replaced. Then, by applying the Hungarian algorithm, we
can find the optimal batch permutation $\sigma^{*}$ such that

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\sigma^{*} = {\underset{\sigma}{\arg ⁡\max}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n}C_{i,{\sigma\operatorname{}{(i)}}}}}}.$$      [(7)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

Then, by mixing ${\overset{\sim}{X}}^{(i)}$ and
$X^{({\sigma^{*}\operatorname{}{(i)}})}$, our objective is optimized.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p3 .ltx_para}
By incorporating sample difficulty assessment (section [[3.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.SS1 "3.1 Sample Difficulty Assessment ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref})
into ([[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.E5 "5 ‣ Optimal assignment. ‣ 3.2 Attention-guided Saliency Detection & Optimal Assignment ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}),
our final objective is written as

  -- ---------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ---------------------------------------------------------
     $\max\limits_{\sigma \in \mathcal{M}}$   $\max\limits_{r_{i} \in {I\operatorname{}B^{n}}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{b}{\mathbb{1}_{\{{{\mathcal{F}\operatorname{}{(X^{(i)},Y^{(i)})}} < \tau}\}}\operatorname{}{({P_{i,{\sigma\operatorname{}{(i)}}} - \rho})}^{\top}\operatorname{}r_{i}}}$      [(8)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equationgroup .ltx_align_right}
                                              ${{\text{s.t.}\operatorname{}\:\:1^{\top}\operatorname{}r_{i}} \leq {n \times \mathbb{1}_{\{{{\mathcal{F}\operatorname{}{(X^{(i)},Y^{(i)})}} < \tau}\}}}},$                                                                                                        
  -- ---------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ---------------------------------------------------------

where $\mathcal{F}$ is the difficulty measurer, and $\tau$ is the
difficulty threshold. $\mathbb{1}$ denotes the indicator function, while
$\mathbf{1}$ is a one vector. If the sample is hard,
[i.e.]{#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p3.10.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic},
${\mathcal{F}\operatorname{}{(X^{(i)},Y^{(i)})}} \geq \tau$, the
constraint becomes ${\mathbf{1}^{\top}\operatorname{}r_{i}} \leq 0$,
enforcing all elements of $r_{i}$ to be 0. In that case, none of the
tokens will be mixed. On the other hand, if the sample is easy,
[i.e.]{#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p3.10.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic},
${\mathcal{F}\operatorname{}{(X^{(i)},Y^{(i)})}} < \tau$, the constraint
is ${\mathbf{1}^{\top}\operatorname{}r_{i}} \leq n$ which is satisfied
by any random $r_{i}$ vector. Then, the objective becomes identical
to ([[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.E5 "5 ‣ Optimal assignment. ‣ 3.2 Attention-guided Saliency Detection & Optimal Assignment ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}).
:::

::: {#S3.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Token-level Mixup {#token-level-mixup .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS3.p1 .ltx_para}
Now, the remaining question is [how]{#S3.SS3.p1.10.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} the tokens of ${\overset{\sim}{X}}^{(i)}$ and
$X^{({\sigma^{*}\operatorname{}{(i)}})}$ are going to be mixed. As
defined in objective ([[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.E5 "5 ‣ Optimal assignment. ‣ 3.2 Attention-guided Saliency Detection & Optimal Assignment ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}),
we use a hard replacement of tokens with respect to decision vector
$r_{i}$. Then, mixing is performed by replacing token index $t$ from the
original instance $i$, with token $t$ from the paired instance
$j = {\sigma^{*}\operatorname{}{(i)}}$, so that $P_{i,j}^{(t)} > \rho$
is satisfied. In other words, only the token indices that increase
saliency level greater than $\rho$ are mixed. This is accomplished by
defining an appropriate binary mask
$M_{t} \in {I\operatorname{}B^{n}\operatorname{}{({t = {1,2,\ldots,n}})}}$
containing

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $$M_{t} = \begin{cases}                                           [(9)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
     0 & {,S_{t}^{(j)} - {\overset{\sim}{S}}_{t}^{(i)} > \rho} \\      
     1 & {,\text{otherwise}}                                           
     \end{cases}$$                                                     
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where ${\overset{\sim}{S}}_{t}^{(i)}$ is the saliency score of
${\overset{\sim}{X}}_{t}^{(i)}$. Then, the mask is employed as

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\overset{\sim}{X}}_{\text{mix}}^{(i)} = {{M \odot {\overset{\sim}{X}}^{(i)}} + {{({1 - M})} \odot X^{(j)}}}},$$      [(10)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------

where operator $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication and
${\overset{\sim}{X}}^{(i)}$, $X^{(j)}$ refer to the paired instances. We
also reassign labels based on the replaced tokens' saliency score. The
new mixed label is computed as

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\overset{\sim}{Y}}_{\text{mix}}^{(i)} = {{\frac{\sum_{t = 1}^{n}{M_{t} \cdot {\overset{\sim}{S}}_{t}^{(i)}}}{{\sum_{t = 1}^{n}{M_{t} \cdot {\overset{\sim}{S}}_{t}^{(i)}}} + {{({1 - M_{t}})} \cdot S_{t}^{(j)}}} \cdot {\overset{\sim}{Y}}^{(i)}} + {\frac{\sum_{t = 1}^{n}{{({1 - M_{t}})} \cdot S_{t}^{(j)}}}{{\sum_{t = 1}^{n}{M_{t} \cdot {\overset{\sim}{S}}_{t}^{(i)}}} + {{({1 - M_{t}})} \cdot S_{t}^{(j)}}} \cdot Y^{(j)}}}}.$$      [(11)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- -----------------------------------------------------
:::

::: {#S3.SS3.p2 .ltx_para}
Consequently, relatively unimportant tokens will be replaced with
salient tokens from another instance, and its label will be adjusted
with respect to the change in overall saliency level. See
Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}
for visualization, and Algorithm [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#alg1 "Algorithm 1 ‣ Optimal assignment. ‣ 3.2 Attention-guided Saliency Detection & Optimal Assignment ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}
for overall computation process. More detailed pseudocode is provided in
the supplement.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS4 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Vertical TokenMixup {#vertical-tokenmixup .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

<figure id="S3.F2" class="ltx_figure">
<img src="./token_files/vtm_v17.png" id="S3.F2.g1"
class="ltx_graphics ltx_centering ltx_img_landscape" width="510"
height="264" alt="Refer to caption" />
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_figure">Figure 2: </span><span
id="S3.F2.2.1" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold"
style="font-size:80%;">Vertical TokenMixup<span id="S3.F2.2.1.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_medium">. By applying VTM, the <span
class="math inline"><em>κ</em></span> most salient tokens from each
previous layer are brought up to form the key and value tokens. By
utilizing a cross-attention mechanism, the dimension is preserved while
being able to aggregate multi-scale information for token augmentation.
The figure demonstrates the case when VTM is applied to layer 3.
</span></span></figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S3.SS4.p1 .ltx_para}
Mixing multiple [instances]{#S3.SS4.p1.5.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
is not the only way to mixup. We also present a simple variant of
TokenMixup, which provides token-level augmentation within a single
sample by utilizing the tokens from previous layers. Similar to
([[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.E5 "5 ‣ Optimal assignment. ‣ 3.2 Attention-guided Saliency Detection & Optimal Assignment ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}),
we can define a new objective for Vertical TokenMixup as

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${{\max\limits_{l \in \mathcal{L}}\max\limits_{r_{i} \in {I\operatorname{}B^{n}}}}\operatorname{}{\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{b}{{({P_{i,{l\operatorname{}{(i)}}} - \rho})}^{\top} \cdot r_{i}}}},$$      [(12)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------

where $\mathcal{L}$ is the set of previous layer indices, and
$l\operatorname{}{(i)}$ returns an arbitrary layer index for the
$i^{t\operatorname{}h}$ instance. This objective can be optimized by a
similar scheme as in Algorithm [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#alg1 "Algorithm 1 ‣ Optimal assignment. ‣ 3.2 Attention-guided Saliency Detection & Optimal Assignment ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}.
However, considering that the tokens from each layer with an identical
index tend to contain similar information, saliency difference matrix
$P_{i,{l\operatorname{}{(i)}}} = {S_{l\operatorname{}{(i)}} - S_{i}}$
(as in ([[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.E4 "4 ‣ Optimal assignment. ‣ 3.2 Attention-guided Saliency Detection & Optimal Assignment ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}))
will most likely be constant across indices. This may lead to
meaningless mixup without much increase in saliency level. Therefore, we
take a different approach to mix tokens [vertically]{#S3.SS4.p1.4.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}.
:::

::: {#S3.SS4.p2 .ltx_para}
The simplest method would be to concatenate all tokens from previous
layers and apply self-attention. But due to the quadratic complexity of
self-attention, naive concatenation may induce excessive computation. To
reduce overhead, we selectively pool the $\kappa$ most salient tokens
from each previous layer and concatenate them to the original token set.
Also, to preserve input dimension, we adopt the cross-attention
mechanism. If $X_{1}$ is the original token set and $X_{2}$ is the
concatenated token set, vertical cross-attention is expressed as

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------
     $${Z = {\text{Softmax}\operatorname{}{({X_{1}\operatorname{}W_{q}\operatorname{}{({X_{2}\operatorname{}W_{k}})}^{\top}})}\operatorname{}X_{2}\operatorname{}W_{v}}},$$      [(13)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- -----------------------------------------------------

where $W_{q},W_{k},W_{v}$ denote the projection layers for query, key,
and value, respectively. See Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3.4 Vertical TokenMixup ‣ 3 Methods ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}
for visualization, and the supplement for pseudocode.
:::
:::

### [3.5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Discussions {#discussions .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS5.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### TokenMixup as Curriculum Learning. {#tokenmixup-as-curriculum-learning. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS5.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
A general framework for curriculum learning consists of two main
components: Difficulty Measurer and Training
Scheduler [[wang2021survey]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}
. TokenMixup satisfies these conditions, as ScoreNet measures the
difficulty of each input, and augmenting the tokens based on input
difficulty naturally schedules training. In the early training phase
where the model parameter is not optimized, most instances will be
evaluated [difficult]{#S3.SS5.SSS0.Px1.p1.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} and no TokenMixup is performed. As the model
converges, on the other hand, many samples will become
[easy]{#S3.SS5.SSS0.Px1.p1.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, triggering
augmentation to make the task more challenging overall. Also, TokenMixup
is applied discriminatively based on individual sample difficulty which
enables more intricate curriculum scheduling. See
section [[5.3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S5.SS3 "5.3 What TokenMixup Learns ‣ 5 Analysis ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}
for empirical demonstration. These properties will be especially useful
when other mixup methods [[zhang2017mixup]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}
; [[yun2019cutmix]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}
; [[kim2020puzzle]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}
; [[kim2021co]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}
are used in parallel. For instance, if input mixup is already applied,
further augmentation may be unnecessary. In such a case, ScoreNet will
regard the sample sufficiently difficult, and no further mixup will be
performed.
:::
:::

experiments, we used the vanilla

style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref}
as baseline. Then, our augmentation methods are evaluated on three
representative image classification datasets: CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and
ImageNet-1K.

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Experimental setup. {#experimental-setup. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
We evaluate TokenMixup and its variant, Vertical TokenMixup (VTM), on
CIFAR and ImageNet with different settings. To avoid confusion, we
denote the original TokenMixup as Horizontal TokenMixup (HTM) in the
following sections. For CIFAR experiments, we adopt the 1500-epoch
version of CCT. We modified the learning rate scheduler and positional
embedding type to achieve better performance than original papers
(denoted $*$ in Table [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S4.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 4.2 Experimental Results ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref}).
Other experiment settings follow [[hassani2021escaping]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref}
, and all experiments on CIFAR datasets were conducted on a single RTX
A6000 GPU. For ImageNet-1K experiments, we used the ViT-B/16 model
pre-trained and fine-tuned on ImageNet-21k and ImageNet-1k, where we
took advantage of the officially released pre-trained weights.
Experiments for Horizontal TokenMixup were conducted on a single NVIDIA
A100 GPU, and 4 RTX 3090 GPUs were used in parallel for Vertical
TokenMixup. Other experiment settings are reported in the supplement.

### [5.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Soundness of Saliency Estimation {#soundness-of-saliency-estimation .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
In TokenMixup, we estimate the saliency using the attention map from the
subsequent layer. The soundness of this approximation would be one of
the main questions regarding saliency detection. So, we provide relevant
analyses on the appropriateness of our saliency approximation method to
answer the research question [Q1]{#S5.SS1.p1.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}.
:::

::: {#S5.SS1.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Qualitative examples. {#qualitative-examples. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS1.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Here, we provide a qualitative analysis on the performance of each
saliency detector. In Figure [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S5.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ 5 Analysis ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref},
we can observe that there is no significant difference between the full
attention rollout and the 1-step approximation,
[i.e.]{#S5.SS1.SSS0.Px1.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, the
subsequent attention map. Slight discrepancy in the sharpness of the
distribution can be observed, but it does not greatly affect our method
that utilizes saliency threshold $\rho$. Moreover, attention-based
methods seem to be more accurate than the gradient-based detector in
certain cases. See the supplement for additional qualitative examples,
and also the quantified comparison on the sharpness measures of the two
methods.
:::
:::

::: {#S5.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Component Analysis {#component-analysis .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we analyze the effect of each component in Horizontal
TokenMixup and Vertical TokenMixup so as to answer [Q2]{#S5.SS2.p1.3.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. In Table [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S5.T4 "Table 4 ‣ Efficiency comparison. ‣ 5.1 Soundness of Saliency Estimation ‣ 5 Analysis ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref},
we provide ablation studies on components of HTM: Sample difficulty
assessment (ScoreNet), Optimal assignment, Token-level mixup, and
Saliency-based label reassignment. By ablating ScoreNet, all instances
are mixed regardless of sample difficulty; note, ScoreNet still receives
supervision signals. By getting rid of optimal assignment, mixup pairs
are not assigned via Hungarian matching; pairs are randomly selected.
Token-level mixup and label reassignment is relevant to the method by
which tokens are selected. Then, by ablating mixup, we randomly select
tokens to replace, while label reassignment ablation derives new labels
based on the number of tokens that have been replaced. We also conducted
sensitivity tests on key hyperparameters $\tau$, $\rho$, and $\kappa$,
but tables and discussions were moved to the supplement due to spatial
constraints.
:::
:::

#### A Curriculum Learning perspective. {#a-curriculum-learning-perspective. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS3.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
In Figure [[4]{.ltx_text

number of mixed samples is relatively low, as there is less need for
augmentation in the early phase of learning. But as the learnable
parameters warm up, the number increases abruptly. In regime (B), the

steadily improves. In the last learning phase (regime (C)), mixup

::: {#S5.SS3.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Comparison with Manifold Mixup. {#comparison-with-manifold-mixup. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S5.SS3.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
TokenMixup is applied to the intermediate tokens, similarly to manifold
mixup [[verma2019manifold]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref}
which is an input mixup [[zhang2017mixup]{.ltx_text
style="font-size:90%;"}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}
method applied to intermediate features. By using manifold mixup,
transformer tokens are mixed via linear interpolation, instead of the
hard replacement of tokens as in TokenMixup. In Table [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2210.07562#S5.T5 "Table 5 ‣ 5.3 What TokenMixup Learns ‣ 5 Analysis ‣ TokenMixup: Efficient Attention-guided Token-level Data Augmentation for Transformers"){.ltx_ref},
our methods are compared with manifold mixup, along with various
combinations of original mixup methods ([i.e.]{#S5.SS3.SSS0.Px2.p1.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, input mixup and cutmix). The best setting
in the CCT paper adopted input mixup and cutmix. By applying HTM and VTM
on top of that setting, we achieved state-of-the-art performance. On the
other hand, by applying manifold mixup, we observed severe deterioration
in accuracy. We conjecture that soft mixing of transformer tokens leads
to over-smoothing of features, which may be inadequate for
self-attention layers. See the supplement for visual aid.
:::

::: {#S6 .section .ltx_section}
## [6 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Conclusion {#conclusion .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S6.p1 .ltx_para}
We proposed TokenMixup for transformers, which adopts the attention map
in place of computationally heavy gradient-based saliency detectors. By
adopting the attention as the saliency map, we achieve $\times 15$
speed-up compared to the gradient-based method with even better
performance. We also introduce a novel perspective of curriculum
learning to mixup, which enables adaptive augmentation based on sample
difficulty and training schedule. To add diversity, Vertical TokenMixup
is introduced, which mixes tokens from different layers for multi-scale
feature augmentation within a single sample. With TokenMixup, we achieve
state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-100, and improve baselines by
significant margins on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-1K.
:::
:::
