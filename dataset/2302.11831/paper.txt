# Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement {#embedding-fourier-for-ultra-high-definition-low-light-image-enhancement .ltx_title .ltx_title_document}

###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

Ultra-High-Definition (UHD) photo has gradually become the standard
configuration in advanced imaging devices. The new standard unveils many
issues in existing approaches for low-light image enhancement (LLIE),
especially in dealing with the intricate issue of joint luminance
enhancement and noise removal while remaining efficient. Unlike existing
methods that address the problem in the spatial domain, we propose a new
solution, [UHDFour]{#id10.id1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}, that embeds
Fourier transform into a cascaded network. Our approach is motivated by
a few unique characteristics in the Fourier domain: 1) most luminance
information concentrates on amplitudes while noise is closely related to
phases, and 2) a high-resolution image and its low-resolution version
share similar amplitude patterns. Through embedding Fourier into our
network, the amplitude and phase of a low-light image are separately
processed to avoid amplifying noise when enhancing luminance. Besides,
UHDFour is scalable to UHD images by implementing amplitude and phase
enhancement under the low-resolution regime and then adjusting the
high-resolution scale with few computations. We also contribute the
first real UHD LLIE dataset, [UHD-LL]{#id10.id1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}, that contains 2,150 low-noise/normal-clear 4K image
pairs with diverse darkness and noise levels captured in different
scenarios. With this dataset, we systematically analyze the performance
of existing LLIE methods for processing UHD images and demonstrate the
advantage of our solution. We believe our new framework, coupled with
the dataset, would push the frontier of LLIE towards UHD. The code and
dataset are available at
[https://li-chongyi.github.io/UHDFour/](https://li-chongyi.github.io/UHDFour/){.ltx_ref
.ltx_href target="_blank"}.
:::

::: {#S1 .section .ltx_section}
## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
With the advent of advanced imaging sensors and displays,
Ultra-High-Definition (UHD) imaging has witnessed rapid development in
recent years. While UHD imaging offers broad applications and makes a
significant difference in picture quality, the extra pixels also
challenge the efficiency of existing image processing algorithms.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
In this study, we focus on one of the most challenging tasks in image
restoration, namely low-light image enhancement (LLIE), where one needs
to jointly enhance the luminance and remove inherent noises caused by
sensors and dim environments. Further to these challenges, we lift the
difficulty by demanding efficient processing in the UHD regime.
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Despite the remarkable progress in low-light image enhancement (LLIE)
(Li et al.,
[2021a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref}),
existing methods (Zhao et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib53){.ltx_ref};
Wu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref};
Xu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref}),
as shown in Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref},
show apparent drawbacks when they are used to process real-world UHD
low-light images. This is because (1) most methods (Guo et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref};
Liu et al.,
[2021b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref};
Ma et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref})
only focus on luminance enhancement and fail in removing noise; (2) some
approaches (Wu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref};
Xu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref})
simultaneously enhance luminance and remove noise in the spatial domain,
resulting in the suboptimal enhancement; (3) existing methods (Wei
et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref};
Zhao et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib53){.ltx_ref};
Wu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref};
Xu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref})
are mainly trained on low-resolution (LR) data, leading to the
incompatibility with high-resolution (HR) inputs; and (4) some studies
(Xu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref};
Zamir et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref})
adopt heavy structures, thus being inefficient for processing UHD
images. More discussion on related work is provided in the Appendix.
:::

::: {#S1.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
To overcome the challenges aforementioned, we present a new idea for
performing LLIE in the Fourier Domain. Our approach differs
significantly from existing solutions that process images in the spatial
domain. In particular, our method, named as [UHDFour]{#S1.p4.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}, is motivated by our observation of two
interesting phenomena in the Fourier domain of low-light noisy images:
i) luminance and noise can be decomposed to a certain extent in the
Fourier domain. Specifically, luminance would manifest as amplitude
while noise is closely related to phase, and ii) the amplitude patterns
of images of different resolutions are similar. These observations
inspire the design of our network, which handles luminance and noise
separately in the Fourier domain. This design is advantageous as it
avoids amplifying noise when enhancing luminance, a common issue
encountered in existing spatial domain-based methods. In addition, the
fact that amplitude patterns of images of different resolutions are
similar motivates us to save computation by first processing in the
low-resolution regime and performing essential adjustments only in the
high-resolution scale.
:::

::: {#S1.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
We also contribute the first benchmark for UHD LLIE. The dataset, named
[UHD-LL]{#S1.p5.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}, contains 2,150
low-noise/normal-clear 4K UHD image pairs with diverse darkness and
noise levels captured in different scenarios. Unlike existing datasets
(Wei et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref};
Lv et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref};
Bychkovsky et al.,
[2011](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref})
that either synthesize or retouch low-light images to obtain the paired
input and target sets, we capture real image pairs. During data
acquisition, special care is implemented to minimize geometric and
photometric misalignment due to camera shake and dynamic environment.
With the new UHD-LL dataset, we design a series of quantitative and
quantitative benchmarks to analyze the performance of existing LLIE
methods and demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.
:::

::: {#S1.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Our contributions are summarized as follows: [(1)]{#S1.p6.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} We propose a new solution for UHD LLIE that is inspired
by unique characteristics observed in the Fourier domain. In comparison
to existing LLIE methods, the proposed framework shows exceptional
effectiveness and efficiency in addressing the joint task of luminance
enhancement and noise removal in the UHD regime. [(2)]{#S1.p6.1.2
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We contribute the first UHD LLIE dataset,
which contains 2,150 pairs of 4K UHD low-noise/normal-clear data,
covering diverse noise and darkness levels and scenes. [(3)]{#S1.p6.1.3
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We conduct a systematical analysis of existing
LLIE methods on UHD data.
:::
:::

## [2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Our Approach {#our-approach .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
In this section, we first discuss our observations in analyzing

### [2.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Observations in Fourier Domain {#observations-in-fourier-domain .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

<figure id="S2.F2" class="ltx_figure">

<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_figure">Figure 2:
</span>Motivations. We observed that <span id="S2.F2.3.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">(a)</span> luminance and noise can be
‘decomposed’ to a certain extent in the Fourier domain and <span
id="S2.F2.4.2" class="ltx_text ltx_font_bold">(b)</span> HR image and
its LR versions share similar amplitude patterns. The amplitude and
phase are produced by Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) and the compositional
images are obtained by Inverse FFT (IFFT). For visualization, we show
the amplitude and phase in imagery format with common transformations.
Lines of the same color indicate a set of FFT/IFFT transforms. The red
triangles mark the similar pattern (obviously different from the gray
one). Zoom in for the details and noise. We show more examples and

</figure>

::: {#S2.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Here we provide more details to supplement the observations we
highlighted in Sec. [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S1 "1 Introduction ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref}.
We analyze real UHD low-light images in the Fourier domain and provide a
concise illustration in Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2.1 Observations in Fourier Domain ‣ 2 Our Approach ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref}.
Specifically, (a) Swapping the amplitude of a low-light and noisy
(low-noise) image with that of its corresponding normal-light and clear
(normal-clear) image produces a normal-light and noisy (normal-noise)
image and a low-light and clear (low-clear) image. We show more examples
in the Appendix. The result suggests that the luminance and noise can be
decomposed to a certain extent in the Fourier domain. In particular,
most luminance information is expressed as amplitudes, and noises are
revealed in phases. This inspires us to process luminance and noise
separately in the Fourier domain. (b) The amplitude patterns of an HR
normal-clear image and its LR versions are similar and are different
from the corresponding HR low-noise counterpart. Such a characteristic
offers us the possibility to first enhance the amplitude of an LR scale
with more computations and then only make minor adjustments in the HR
scale. In this way, most computations can be conducted in the LR space,
reducing the computational complexity.
:::
:::

### [2.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}The UHDFour Network {#the-uhdfour-network .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

<figure id="S2.F3" class="ltx_figure">
<img
src="./%5B2302.11831%5D%20Embedding%20Fourier%20for%20Ultra-High-Definition%20Low-Light%20Image%20Enhancement_files/framework.jpg"
id="S2.F3.g1" class="ltx_graphics ltx_centering ltx_img_landscape"
width="538" height="143" alt="Refer to caption" />
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_figure">Figure 3:
</span>Overview of UHDFour. Our approach consists of an LRNet and an
HRDNet. The LRNet is an encoder-decoder network that produces 8<span
class="math inline">×</span> downsampled result <span
class="math inline">$\hat{y_{8}}$</span> and the refined amplitude <span
class="math inline"><em>A</em><sub><em>r</em></sub></span> and phase
<span class="math inline"><em>P</em><sub><em>r</em></sub></span>
features. We omit the skip connections for brevity. The HRNet contains
an Adjustment Block and the upsampling operation, producing the final
result <span class="math inline"><em>ŷ</em></span>. Most computation is
conducted in the LRNet.</figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S2.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Overview.]{#S2.SS2.p1.5.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} UHDFour aims to map
an UHD low-noise input image $x \in {\mathbb{R}}^{H \times W \times C}$
to its corresponding normal-clear version
$y \in {\mathbb{R}}^{H \times W \times C}$, where $H$, $W$, and $C$
represent height, width, and channel, respectively. Figure
[[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S2.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ 2.2 The UHDFour Network ‣ 2 Our Approach ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref}
shows the overview of UHDFour. It consists of an LRNet and an HRNet.
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Motivated by the observation in Sec. [[2.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S2.SS1 "2.1 Observations in Fourier Domain ‣ 2 Our Approach ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref},
LRNet takes the most computation of the whole network. Its input is
first embedded into the feature domain by a Conv layer. To reduce
computational complexity, we downsample the features to 1/8 of the
original resolution by bilinear interpolation. Then, the LR features go
through an encoder-decoder network, which contains four FouSpa Blocks
with two 2$\times$downsample and two 2$\times$upsample operations,
obtaining outputs features. The outputs features are respectively fed to
FFT to obtain the refined amplitude $A_{r}$ and phase $P_{r}$ features
and a Conv layer to estimate the LR normal-clear image
$\hat{y_{8}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{{{{H/8} \times W}/8} \times C}$.
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The outputs of LRNet coupled with the input are fed to the HRNet.
Specifically, the input $x$ is first reshaped to
$x_{p\operatorname{}u} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{H \times W \times C \times 64}$
via PixelUnshuffle (8$\times$ $\downarrow$) to preserve original
information, and then fed to an Adjustment Block. With the refined
amplitude $A_{r}$ and phase $P_{r}$ features, the Adjustment Block
produces adjusted features that are reshaped to the original height and
width of input $x$ via Pixelshuffle (8$\times$ $\uparrow$). Finally, we
resize the estimated LR normal-clear image $\hat{y_{8}}$ to the original
size of input $x$ via bilinear interpolation and combine it with the
upsampled features to estimate the final HR normal-clear image
$\hat{y}$. We detail the key components as follows.
:::

<figure id="S2.F4" class="ltx_figure">
<img
src="./%5B2302.11831%5D%20Embedding%20Fourier%20for%20Ultra-High-Definition%20Low-Light%20Image%20Enhancement_files/blocks.jpg"
id="S2.F4.g1" class="ltx_graphics ltx_centering ltx_img_landscape"
width="479" height="172" alt="Refer to caption" />
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_figure">Figure 4:
</span>Structures of the FouSpa Block (a) and Adjustment Block
(b).</figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S2.SS2.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[FouSpa Block.]{#S2.SS2.p4.4.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} In
Sec. [[2.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S2.SS1 "2.1 Observations in Fourier Domain ‣ 2 Our Approach ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref},
we observe that luminance and noise can be decomposed in the Fourier
domain. Hence, we design the FouSpa Block to parallelly implement
amplitude and phase enhancement in the Fourier domain and feature
enhancement in the spatial domain. As shown in Figure [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S2.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 2.2 The UHDFour Network ‣ 2 Our Approach ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref}(a),
the input features are forked into the Fourier and Spatial branches. In
the Fourier branch, FFT is first used to obtain the amplitude component
($A$) and phase component ($P$). The two components are separately fed
to two Conv layers with 1$\times$`<!-- -->`{=html}1 kernel. Note that
when processing amplitude and phase, we only use
1$\times$`<!-- -->`{=html}1 kernel to avoid damaging the structure
information. Then, we transform them back to the spatial domain via IFFT
and concatenate them with the spatial features enhanced by a Half
Instance Normalization (HIN) unit (Chen et al.,
[2021a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}).
We adopt the HIN unit based on its efficiency. The concatenated features
are further fed to a Conv layer and then combined with the input
features in a residual manner. Although our main motivations are in the
Fourier domain, the use of the spatial branch is necessary. This is
because the spatial branch and Fourier branch are complementary. The
spatial branch adopts convolution operations that can model the
structure dependency well in spatial domain. The Fourier branch can
attend global information and benefit the disentanglement of energy and
degradation.
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Adjustment Block.]{#S2.SS2.p5.4.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} The
Adjustment Block is the main structure of the HRNet, and it is
lightweight. As shown in Figure [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S2.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 2.2 The UHDFour Network ‣ 2 Our Approach ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref}(b),
the Adjustment Block shares a similar structure with the FouSpa Block.
Differently, in the Fourier branch, with the refined amplitude $A_{r}$
features obtained from the LRNet, we use Spatial Feature Transform
(SFT) (Wang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref})
to modulate the amplitude features of the input $x_{p\operatorname{}u}$
via simple affine transformation. Such a transformation or adjustment is
possible because the luminance, as global information, manifests as
amplitude components, and the amplitude patterns of an HR scale and its
LR scales are similar (as discussed in Sec. [[2.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S2.SS1 "2.1 Observations in Fourier Domain ‣ 2 Our Approach ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref}).
Note that we cannot modulate the phase because of its periodicity.
Besides, we do not find an explicit relationship between the HR scale's
phase and its LR scales. However, we empirically find that concatenating
the refined phase $P_{r}$ features achieved from the LRNet with the
phase features of the input $x_{p\operatorname{}u}$ improves the final
performance. We thus apply such concatenation in our solution.
:::

::: {#S2.SS2.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Losses.]{#S2.SS2.p6.8.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We use $l_{1}$ to
supervise $\hat{y_{8}}$ and $\hat{y}$. We also add perceptual loss to
supervise $\hat{y_{8}}$ while the use of perceptual loss on $\hat{y}$ is
impracticable because of its high resolution. Instead, we add SSIM loss
$\mathcal{L}_{s\operatorname{}s\operatorname{}i\operatorname{}m}$ on
$\hat{y}$. The final loss $\mathcal{L}$ is the combination of these
losses:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathcal{L} = {{\|{\hat{y} - y}\|}_{1} + {{0.0004 \times \mathcal{L}_{s\operatorname{}s\operatorname{}i\operatorname{}m}}\operatorname{}{(\hat{y},y)}} + {0.1 \times {\|{\hat{y_{8}} - y_{8}}\|}_{1}} + {0.0002 \times {\|{{\text{VGG}\operatorname{}{(\hat{y_{8}})}} - {\text{VGG}\operatorname{}{(y_{8})}}}\|}_{2}}}},$$      [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $y$ is the ground truth, $y_{8}$ is the 8$\times$ downsampled
version of $y$, [VGG]{#S2.SS2.p6.13.1 .ltx_text .ltx_markedasmath} is
the pre-trained VGG19 network, in which we use four scales to supervise
training Zhou et al.
([2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref}).
:::
:::

::: {#S3 .section .ltx_section}
## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}UHD-LL Dataset {#uhd-ll-dataset .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
We collect a real low-noise/normal-clear paired image dataset that
contains 2,150 pairs of 4K UHD data saved in 8bit sRGB format. Several
samples are shown in Figure [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S3.F5 "Figure 5 ‣ 3 UHD-LL Dataset ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S3.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
Images are collected from a camera mounted on a tripod to ensure
stability. Two cameras, [i.e.]{#S3.p2.3.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}.,
a Sony $\alpha\operatorname{}7$ III camera and a Sony Alpha a6300
camera, are used to offer diversity. The ground truth (or normal-clear)
image is captured with a small ISO $\in {\lbrack 100,800\rbrack}$ in a
bright scene (indoor or outdoor). The corresponding low-noise image is
acquired by increasing the ISO $\in {\lbrack 1000,20000\rbrack}$ and
reducing the exposure time. Due to the constraints of exposure gears in
the cameras, shooting in the large ISO range may produce bright images,
which opposes the purpose of capturing low-light and noisy images. Thus,
in some cases, we put a neutral-density (ND) filter with different
ratios on the camera lens to capture low-noise images. In this way, we
can increase the ISO to generate heavier noises and simultaneously
obtain extremely dark images, enriching the diversity of darkness and
noise levels.
:::

::: {#S3.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
The main challenge of collecting paired data is to reduce misalignment
caused by camera shakes and dynamic objects. We take several measures to
ameliorate the issue. Apart from using a tripod, we also use remote
control software (Imaging Edge) to adjust the exposure time and ISO
value to avoid any physical contact with the camera. To further reduce
subtle misalignments, we adopt an image alignment algorithm (Evangelidis
& Psarakis,
[2008](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref})
to estimate the affine matrix and align the low-light image and its
ground truth. We improve the alignment method by applying AdaIN (Huang &
Belongie,
[2017](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref})
before the affine matrix estimation to reduce the intensity gap.
Finally, we hire annotators to check all paired images carefully and
discard those that still exhibit misalignments.
:::

::: {#S3.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
We split the UHD-LL dataset into two parts: 2,000 pairs for training and
115 pairs for testing. The training and test partitions are exclusive in
their scene and data. We also ensure consistency in pixel intensity
distribution between the training and test splits. More analysis of this
data, [e.g.]{#S3.p4.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}., the pixel
intensity and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) distributions, can be found in
the Appendix.
:::

::: {#S3.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
A comparison between our UHD-LL dataset and existing paired low-light
image datasets is presented in Table [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S3.F5 "Figure 5 ‣ 3 UHD-LL Dataset ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref}.
The LOL dataset (two versions: LOL-v1: 500 images; LOL-v2: 789 images)
is most related to our UHD-LL dataset as both focus on real low-light
images with noise. The LOL-v2 contains all images of the LOL-v1. In
contrast to the LOL dataset, our dataset features a more extensive
collection, where diverse darkness and noise levels from rich types of
scenes are considered. Moreover, the images of our dataset have higher
resolutions than those from the LOL dataset. As shown in Figure
[[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ Embedding Fourier for Ultra-High-Definition Low-Light Image Enhancement"){.ltx_ref},
the models pre-trained on the LOL dataset cannot handle the cases in our
UHD-LL dataset due to its insufficient training data, which are
low-resolution and contains mostly mild noises. Different from SID
dataset that focuses on RAW data, our dataset only studies the data with
RGB format. The images in the SID dataset are captured in extremely dark
scenes. Its diversity of darkness levels and scenes is limited. When
these RAW data with extremely low intensity are transformed into sRGB
images, some information would be truncated due to the bit depth
constraints of 8bit sRGB image. In this case, it is challenging to train
a network for effectively mapping noise and low-light images to clear
and normal-light images using these sRGB images as training data.
:::
:::

::: {#S4 .section .ltx_section}
## [4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Experiments {#experiments .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S4.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Implementation.]{#S4.p1.7.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We implement our
method with PyTorch and train it on six NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPUs. We use
an ADAM optimizer for network optimization. The learning rate is set to
0.0001. A batch size of 6 is applied. We fix the channels of each Conv
layer to 16, except for the Conv layers associated with outputs. We use
the Conv layer with stride $= 2$ and 4$\times$`<!-- -->`{=html}4 kernels
to implement the 2$\times$ downsample operation in the encoder and
interpolation to implement the 2$\times$ upsample operation in the
decoder in the LRNet. Unless otherwise stated, the Conv layer uses
stride $= 1$ and 3$\times$`<!-- -->`{=html}3 kernels. We use the
training data in the UHD-LL dataset to train our model. Images are
randomly cropped into patches of size 512 $\times$ 512 for training.
:::

::: {#S4.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Compared Methods.]{#S4.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We include 14
state-of-the-art methods (21 models in total) for our benchmarking study
and performance comparison. These methods includes 12 light enhancement
methods: NPE (TIP'13) (Wang et al.,
[2013](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref})
SRIE (CVPR'16) (Fu et al.,
[2016](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref}),
DRBN (CVPR'20) (Yang et al.,
[2020a](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib47){.ltx_ref}),
Zero-DCE (CVPR'20) (Guo et al.,
[2020](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref}),
Zero-DCE++ (TPAMI'21) (Li et al.,
[2021b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}),
RUAS (CVPR'21) (Liu et al.,
[2021b](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref}),
Zhao [et al.]{#S4.p2.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} (ICCV'21) (Zhao
et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib53){.ltx_ref}),
EnlightenGAN (TIP'21) (Jiang et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}),
Afifi [et al.]{#S4.p2.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} (CVPR'21) (Afifi
et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}),
SCI (CVPR'22) (Ma et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}),
SNR-Aware (CVPR'22) (Xu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref}),
URetinex-Net (CVPR'22) (Wu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref})
and 2 Transformers: Uformer (CVPR'22) (Wang et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib40){.ltx_ref})
and Restormer (CVPR'22) (Zamir et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref}).
We use their released models and also retrain them using the same
training data as our method. Note that some methods provide different
models trained using different datasets. Due to the heavy models used in
Restormer (Zamir et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref})
and SNR-Aware (Xu et al.,
[2022](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref}),
we cannot infer the full-resolution results of both methods on UHD
images, despite using a GPU with 48G memory. Following previous UHD
study (Zheng et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib54){.ltx_ref}),
we resort to two strategies for this situation: (1) We downsample the
input to the largest size that the model can handle and then resize the
result to the original resolution, denoted by the subscript 'resize'.
(2) We split the input into four patches without overlapping and then
stitch the result, denoted by the subscript 'stitch'.
:::

::: {#S4.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Evaluation Metrics.]{#S4.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We employ
full-reference image quality assessment metrics PSNR, SSIM (Wang et al.,
[2004](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}),
and LPIPS (Alex version) (Zhang et al.,
[2018](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib52){.ltx_ref})
to quantify the performance of different methods. We also adopt the
non-reference image quality evaluator (NIQE) (Mittal et al.,
[2013](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref})
and the multi-scale image quality Transformer (MUSIQ) (trained on
KonIQ-10k dataset) (Ke et al.,
[2021](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2302.11831#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref})
for assessing the restoration quality. We notice that the quantitative
results reported by different papers diverge. For a fair comparison, we
adopt the commonly-used IQA PyTorch Toolbox[^1^[[^1^[1]{.ltx_tag
.ltx_tag_note}https://github.com/chaofengc/IQA-PyTorch]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote1
.ltx_note .ltx_role_footnote} to compute the quantitative results of all
compared methods. We also test the trainable parameters and running time
for processing UHD 4K data.
