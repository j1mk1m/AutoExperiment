::: ltx_abstract
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

Visual Prompting (VP) is an emerging and powerful technique that allows
sample-efficient adaptation to downstream tasks by engineering a
well-trained frozen source model. In this work, we explore the benefits
of VP in constructing compelling neural network classifiers with
differential privacy (DP). We explore and integrate VP into canonical DP
training methods and demonstrate its simplicity and efficiency. In
particular, we discover that VP in tandem with PATE, a state-of-the-art
DP training method that leverages the knowledge transfer from an
ensemble of teachers, achieves the state-of-the-art privacy-utility
trade-off with minimum expenditure of privacy budget. Moreover, we
conduct additional experiments on cross-domain image classification with
a sufficient domain gap to further unveil the advantage of VP in DP.
Lastly, we also conduct extensive ablation studies to validate the
effectiveness and contribution of VP under DP consideration.
:::

## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para}
Originating from the domain of deep learning for natural language
processing, prompt engineering has gained significant popularity as an
emergent technique for efficient adoption and adaptation of pre-trained
language models for solving different downstream tasks
\[[21](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref}\].
In recent years, the notion of prompting has been extended to other
domains and data modalities, especially in computer vision and images
\[[16](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref},
[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}\].
Specifically, the term [visual prompting]{#S1.p1.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} (VP) has been coined by
\[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}\],
and the authors show competitive accuracy of VP on some downstream image
classification tasks over linear probing (i.e., attaching a trainable
linear head to a pre-trained model) when used with a large vision model
such as CLIP
\[[32](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}\]
(only the image encoder). It is worth noting that VP in
\[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}\]
can be viewed as a special case of [model reprogramming]{#S1.p1.1.2
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} (MR)
\[[8](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref}\]
on a pre-trained model. MR inserts an input transformation layer and an
output mapping layer into a pre-trained frozen model for fine-tuning
downstream tasks. MR is equivalent to VP in
\[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}\]
when the input transformation is a trainable input perturbation and the
output mapping is a specified source-target label correspondence or a
set of text prompts for label inference (e.g., "a photo of \[predicted
class\]"). Throughout this paper, for ease of elucidation, we will use
VP and MR interchangeably.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para}
VP has been extensively studied for various use cases, ranging from
image classification
\[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}\],
enhancing adversarial robustness
\[[6](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref}\],
image-inpainting
\[[4](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}\],
cross-domain adaptation
\[[35](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref},
[28](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}\],
to name a few. In this paper, we explore yet another benefit of VP with
pre-trained models -- deep learning with differential privacy (DP). In
deep learning, scaling the training parameters of a neural network often
leads to improved task performance (e.g., a classification model with
higher accuracy)
\[[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref}\].
However, with a DP budget, training a larger neural network usually
means more consumption of data privacy
\[[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}\].
Motivated by this dilemma of the tradeoff between neural network
capacity and DP, we aim to study the following fundamental question:

[Can VP with a pre-trained model (trained on non-private data) improve
the privacy-accuracy tradeoff in off-the-shelf DP-training
mechanisms?]{#S1.p2.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}

In this paper, we give an affirmative answer to this question, validated
through a comprehensive analysis and empirical comparisons. Note that we
purposely focus on existing DP-training mechanisms, in order to study
the benefit of improved performance contributed by VP. Our proposed
approach applies VP (at data inputs) to off-the-shelf DP-training
mechanisms, together with a pre-trained model trained on non-private
data. Particularly, when VP is used in PATE (Private Aggregation of
Teacher Ensembles)
\[[30](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}\],
a DP training mechanism, we show that the classification accuracy under
a privacy constraint can achieve the current state-of-the-art
performance (over 97%) on the common benchmark of CIFAR-10
classification task. Furthermore, we also demonstrate that the
performance increases with minimum expenditure of privacy budget.
Consequently, our results uncovered new benefits of VP in DP and offer
new use cases and insights into prompt engineering.
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Contribution.]{#S1.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We highlight our
main contributions as follows. We are the first to explore the benefits
of VP with pre-trained models in the design of DP classifiers. By
leveraging VP, we present Prom-PATE as a training strategy for DP
classifiers. While sophisticated backbones are usually difficult to be
used in DP training, Prom-PATE has great flexibility in utilizing the
high accuracy of the backbone without compromising privacy. Overall,
Prom-PATE enjoys the following characteristics. Prom-PATE relies on VP
to resolve the demand for huge data from PATE, improving practicality
and accuracy. In the design of Prom-PATE, the public pre-trained models
are utilized [twice]{#S1.p3.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic},
significantly growing the accuracy. Through extensive experiments, we
demonstrate that Prom-PATE outperforms current DP classifiers on
CIFAR-10, showing an accuracy 97.07% under a privacy budget of
$\epsilon = 1.019$. We also show significant accuracy gain of Prom-PATE
in other datasets over existing methods.
:::
:::

## [2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Related Work and Background {#related-work-and-background .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Visual Prompting (VP) and Model Reprogramming (MR). {#visual-prompting-vp-and-model-reprogramming-mr. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Both VP and MR focus on the problem setup of reusing a pre-trained model
to perform a new task (either in-domain or cross-domain) without
changing the model weights during fine-tuning (i.g., the pre-trained
model is "frozen"). MR was first studied through the lens of adversarial
machine learning (ML). Elsayed et al.
\[[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\]
showed that an attacker can "steal" an ML model's computation resource
to perform another task without the model owner's consent. Later on, MR
was shown to deliver competitive image classification results in
data-limited and cross-domain settings
\[[35](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref},
[28](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}\],
wherein the authors demonstrated the possibility of reusing a
pre-trained model from a source domain (e.g., general image classifiers
or language models) to solve challenging image classification problems
in a target domain (e.g., bio-medical measurements). We refer the
readers to the survey paper of MR in
\[[8](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref}\]
for more details. VP through a trainable (padded) universal input
perturbation is revisited in
\[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}\],
and the authors showed competitive results on some subset of 12 image
classification tasks over linear probing and full fine-tuning on
pre-trained image classifiers and the CLIP model
\[[32](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref}\].
Chen et al.
\[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\]
improved VP by introducing iterative label mapping during training.
Beyond image classification, VP was extended to image inpainting tasks
\[[4](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}\].
In this paper, we note that we limit the scope of VP to input-level
prompt engineering as studied in
\[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref},
[6](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref}\],
and we leave the broader notion of VP via injecting trainable token
embeddings (e.g., the visual prompt tuning as in
\[[16](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}\])
to different layers of a pre-trained model as future work.
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Differentially Private Classifiers. {#differentially-private-classifiers. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
One of the most widely used techniques to achieve DP deep learning is
DPSGD \[[1](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}\],
where DP noise is added to the clipped gradient updates during the
training process. The definition and properties of DP are provided in
the Supplementary Material. DPSGD suffers from information loss due to
the fact that the gradient clipping and the noise scale are proportional
to the norm of clipped gradient. Recent
research \[[10](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref},
[27](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref}\]
finds that we may overestimate the privacy loss for DPSGD because the
attacker does not have access to the gradient in each training
iteration. One of the current trends in training a DP classifier is to
privately fine-tune large pre-trained models such as BERT variants and
GPT-2
\[[40](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib40){.ltx_ref},
[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref},
[20](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib20){.ltx_ref}\].
This private fine-tuning strategy can also be applied to the realm of
images \[[14](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref},
[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref},
[19](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref},
[33](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref},
[11](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref},
[5](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}\].
For example, Tramèr and
Boneh \[[33](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref}\]
improved the model utility by conducting private fine-tuning with SimCLR
features \[[9](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref}\].
De et
al. \[[11](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref}\]
also pre-trained the model with the public data. After that, they apply
many techniques including large batch size and weight standardization to
improve accuracy. Bu et
al. \[[5](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}\]'s
DP classifier relies on the notion of ghost clipping to calculate the
clipped gradient required by DPSGD.
:::

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2.p2 .ltx_para}
PATE \[[30](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref},
[31](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}\]
is another approach that trains a DP classifier. In PATE, the sensitive
dataset is first partitioned into slices, with each [teacher
model]{#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} trained on a
different slice of the data (through SGD). Then, the non-sensitive
samples labeled by the DP noisy votes from teacher models are used to
train a [student model]{#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px2.p2.1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}, which turns out to be a DP classifier. Compared to
DPSGD, fewer research efforts are put into the improvement of PATE. For
example,
Private-kNN \[[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}\]
relies on the private release of k-nearest neighbor (kNN) queries to
avoid splitting the training set in PATE.
:::
:::

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px3 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Visual Prompting with DP. {#visual-prompting-with-dp. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S2.SS0.SSS0.Px3.p1 .ltx_para}
A recent work that combines VP and DP is
Reprogrammable-FL \[[2](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}\].
Reprogrammable-FL is designed for DP federated learning (FL). More
specifically, Reprogrammable-FL considers multiple clients, each with a
common pre-trained model in each server-client interaction. The aim is
to learn privatized visual prompts and label mappings for each client
using
DPSGD \[[1](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}\],
enabling DPFL with more efficient use of the privacy budget.
Reprogrammable-FL outperforms methods that rely on private fine-tuning
from pre-trained models, currently considered the standard for achieving
high accuracy in DPFL. However, in each training round of
Reprogrammable-FL, the update of visual prompts and label mapping for
each client is still subject to clipped noisy gradient updates to ensure
privacy. As a result, the overall performance may still degrade compared
to the non-private setting of visual
prompting \[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref}\],
as will be demonstrated in this paper.
:::
:::
:::

## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Main Approach {#main-approach .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we aim to investigate how VP can improve the
privacy-utility trade-off of deep learning models.
:::

::: {#S3.SS0.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Notations. {#notations. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS0.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
As VP was originally proposed for model re-utilization, we denote a
source model $f_{S}\operatorname{}{(\theta_{S};x)}$ which is trained
from a large, source (pubic) dataset $D_{S}:={\{{(x_{S},y_{S})}\}}$ with
$x_{S}$, where $x_{S}$ denotes the feature and $y_{S}$ denotes the
label, both from the source domain. We denote our target (private)
dataset $D_{T}:={\{{(x_{T},y_{T})}\}}$ with $x_{T}$ in which we
re-utilize model $f_{S}\operatorname{}{(\theta_{S};x)}$ to accomplish
the task in $D_{T}$ via VP without modifying the weights $\theta_{S}$.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Design Challenges for DP Classifiers {#design-challenges-for-dp-classifiers .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Though PATE outperforms DPSGD because of the reduced noise scale and no
information loss from the gradient clipping, we identify three
challenges for designing DP classifiers based on PATE.

-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S3.I1.i1}

    ::: {#S3.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    [(C1)]{#S3.I1.i1.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} The performance of
    PATE is sensitive to data partitioning. In particular, the teacher
    models may perform badly when the sensitive data is limited in size.
    As also shown in
    \[[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}\],
    each teacher model has an accuracy under 50% due to only 200 images
    for each partition, given 250 teacher models for CIFAR-10. One might
    leverage transfer learning (TL), as suggested in
    \[[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref}\],
    to train teacher models in PATE. Specifically, this involves using a
    public pre-trained model and fine-tuning it on the private dataset.
    However, Table [[1]{.ltx_text
    .ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 3.1 Design Challenges for DP Classifiers ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
    shows that this TL-based method leads to inefficient performance in
    PATE[^1^[[^1^[1]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_note}The poor accuracy of the
    TL-based method can be attributed to the over-partitioning of the
    sensitive data. In such a case, data are insufficient for the
    training of each teacher
    model.]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote1 .ltx_note
    .ltx_role_footnote}.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S3.I1.i2}

    ::: {#S3.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    [(C2)]{#S3.I1.i2.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} A current trend in
    training a high-accuracy classifier in a DP manner is to take
    advantage of either public labeled data or a public pre-trained
    model. For example, De et
    al. \[[11](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref}\]
    pre-train the model with ImageNet (seen as a public dataset) and
    then fine-tune the model with CIFAR-10 (seen as a private dataset)
    through DPSGD. De et al. achieve the predicting accuracy 94.7% under
    $\epsilon = 1$. While many pieces of evidence show that properly
    exploiting public datasets and models may significantly improve
    accuracy, a natural question that arises is whether exploiting
    public datasets and models more times in the design of DP
    classifiers benefits accuracy.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S3.I1.i3}

    ::: {#S3.I1.i3.p1 .ltx_para}
    [(C3)]{#S3.I1.i3.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} Privately training
    a model pre-trained on the public dataset is a promising solution
    for DP-classifiers. However, take ImageNet and CIFAR-10 as examples.
    They may share a very similar distribution and thus make the above
    training strategy doubtful in DP guarantee
    \[[34](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib34){.ltx_ref}\].
    :::
:::

::: {#S3.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Prom-PATE {#prom-pate .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
Here, we present a new approach, Prom-PATE, which leverages VP and PATE
for private learning. The workflow of Prom-PATE is shown in
Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Design Challenges for DP Classifiers ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}.
Prom-PATE is a simple yet effective approach to training a classifier in
a DP manner. Basically, Prom-PATE follows all of the steps in PATE
\[[31](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref},
[30](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}\]
except that each teacher model in PATE is reprogrammed from a
pre-trained source model to a [re-teacher model]{#S3.SS2.p1.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}. The structure of re-teacher model is also
shown in Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Design Challenges for DP Classifiers ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}.
Such simplicity of Prom-PATE also enjoys the direct inheritance of DP
guarantee from PATE.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Prom-PATE Procedures. {#prom-pate-procedures. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
Prom-PATE consists of three steps: (a) training re-teacher models, (b)
executing private aggregation, and (c) training a student model. Step
(a) considers a public pre-trained model as a [source
model]{#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px1.p1.4.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} and trains
visual prompting and label mapping on sensitive data. In particular, we
are aimed to train only the prompting parameter $\omega$ while the
pre-trained source model is always fixed. The prompting parameter
$\omega$ (including trainable parameters $\omega_{1}$ and $\omega_{2}$
in Eq. ([[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.E1 "1 ‣ Training re-teacher Models. ‣ 3.2 Prom-PATE ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref})
and Eq. ([[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.E2 "2 ‣ Training re-teacher Models. ‣ 3.2 Prom-PATE ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}),
respectively) and collectively called re-teacher model (see
Figure [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Design Challenges for DP Classifiers ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}).
We note that the re-teacher model is trained on the sensitive dataset
through SGD, and hence does not fulfill DP. The next step contributes to
the DP guarantee of Prom-PATE. Step (b) uses PATE to aggregate the
predictions of the re-teacher models; i.e., when a sample is fed into
re-teacher models, all of them have votes and use the DP noisy top-1
outcome as the label. In step (c), a student model is trained using
semi-supervised learning with a pre-trained classifier. In particular,
certain unlabeled public samples with labels from the DP noisy votes are
used to train the student model, which serves as the resulting DP
classifier. One can easily prove that Prom-PATE satisfies DP; the proof
can be found in the Supplementary Materials.
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Training re-teacher Models. {#training-re-teacher-models. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
During the training of each re-teacher model, we keep the source model
fixed while conducting SGD to update only the label mappings and visual
prompts. The visual prompt ${\hat{x}}_{S}$ can be expressed as

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${{\hat{x}}_{S} = {{M \odot \omega_{1}} + {{{({I - M})} \odot \text{ZeroPad}}\operatorname{}{(x_{T})}}}},$      [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

where $\odot$ stands for Hadamard product, $\omega_{1}$ denotes the
trainable noise parameter, and $M$ denotes the binary mask of the same
dimension with the source data $x_{S}$ (i.e. $M \in {\{ 0,1\}}^{d_{S}}$,
where $d_{S}$ denotes the dimension of the source domain image). On the
other hand, upon obtaining the pre-trained model output
${\hat{y}}_{S}:={f_{S}\operatorname{}{(\theta_{S};{\hat{x}}_{S})}}$, we
further render it through a label mapping function
$f_{\ell}\operatorname{}{(\omega_{2}; \cdot )}$ that maps the source
labels to target labels and obtain the final prediction ${\hat{y}}_{T}$
which has the following form

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${{\hat{y}}_{T} = {\text{softmax}\operatorname{}{({f_{\ell}\operatorname{}{(\omega_{2};{\hat{y}}_{S})}})}}}.$      [(2)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
:::

#### Algorithmic Details of Prom-PATE. {#algorithmic-details-of-prom-pate. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px3.p1 .ltx_para}
Figure [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Training re-teacher Models. ‣ 3.2 Prom-PATE ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
illustrates different label mapping techniques used in Prom-PATE. To
have a correspondence in label classes between the target and source
domains, in the first approach, we conduct random label
mapping \[[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref},
[35](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}\].
Particularly, before training, we establish a random mapping between the
labels of two domains and train the model according to the predetermined
label mapping (e.g., ImageNet label $i$ $\rightarrow$ CIFAR-10 label
$j$). In this case, $\omega_{2}$ specifies the source-target label
correspondence in VP. For the second approach, we consider using fully
connected (FC) layers as part of the label mapping for greater
expressiveness, as studied in
\[[2](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}\].
This allows Prom-PATE to learn how to adapt labels from the source
domain to the target domain. Overall, the re-teacher models in Prom-PATE
only need to train the parameters $\omega:={\{\omega_{1},\omega_{2}\}}$
on the private/sensitive dataset.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px3.p2 .ltx_para}
To enforce DP in Prom-PATE, we adopt the DP aggregation from PATE by
considering Confident-GNMax
\[[31](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref},
[30](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}\].
Specifically, given an unlabelled public data sample $x$, the
aggregation mechanism would collect the response from every re-teacher
model, establishing votes for each $j$-th class,
$n_{j}\operatorname{}{(x)}$. The aggregation then proceeds to determine
whether the noisy votes are consent among re-teachers above a threshold
$T$. Namely,

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${{{\max\limits_{j}{\{{n_{j}\operatorname{}{(x)}}\}}} + {\mathcal{N}\operatorname{}{(0,\sigma_{1}^{2})}}} \geq T}.$      [(3)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

If the inequality is met, then the aggregation would proceed to offer
noisy votes of re-teachers model as follows.

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${\arg{\max\limits_{j}{\{{{n_{j}\operatorname{}{(x)}} + {\mathcal{N}\operatorname{}{(0,\sigma_{2}^{2})}}}\}}}}.$      [(4)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

Otherwise, the aggregation would output nothing.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px3.p3 .ltx_para}
To limit the privacy budget and further enhance performance in
Prom-PATE, we use a subset of the public training data and label it
using the private aggregation mechanism while conducting training for
the rest of the training data in a semi-supervised fashion. Similar to
PATE, this approach allows us to improve the privacy-utility trade-off
by reducing the amount of data that needs to be labeled while still
achieving high accuracy.
:::

::: {#S3.SS2.SSS0.Px3.p4 .ltx_para}
Since re-teacher models can adapt to the private domain under small
sample complexity, we adopt the approach presented in
\[[36](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref}\]
for our semi-supervised learning of the student model. We explain the
details of this approach in Section [[4.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.SS2 "4.2 Ablation Study of Prom-PATE ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
where we compare it to other baseline settings. Using this approach, we
can achieve a better privacy-utility trade-off and improve the overall
performance of Prom-PATE.
:::
:::
:::

::: {#S3.SS3 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [3.3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Why Prom-PATE is Beneficial to DP? {#why-prom-pate-is-beneficial-to-dp .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S3.SS3.p1 .ltx_para}
This section provides an explanation as to why Prom-PATE, as a
combination of VP and PATE, can attain an improved privacy-utility
trade-off by overcoming the design challenges
[(C1)$\sim$(C3)]{#S3.SS3.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}.
:::

::: {#S3.SS3.p2 .ltx_para}
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S3.I2.i1}

    ::: {#S3.I2.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    [(C1)]{#S3.I2.i1.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} As mentioned in
    Section [[3.1]{.ltx_text
    .ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.SS1 "3.1 Design Challenges for DP Classifiers ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
    though PATE is superior to DPSGD from the perspectives of noise
    scale and information loss, it can apply only to huge datasets
    because, otherwise, the teacher models fail to have decent accuracy,
    leading to poor student classifier accuracy. However, VP has proven
    to successfully transfer knowledge from large source domains to
    small target
    domains \[[35](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}\].
    Thus, considering each partitioned slice of the sensitive dataset as
    a small target domain enables re-teacher models in Prom-PATE to
    avoid the problem of data insufficiency when increasing the number
    of re-teacher models, amplifying the benefits of ensemble learning
    in the ordinary PATE.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S3.I2.i2}

    ::: {#S3.I2.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    [(C2)]{#S3.I2.i2.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} Prom-PATE is
    featured by utilizing the public data [twice]{#S3.I2.i2.p1.1.2
    .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}; once in training re-teacher models and
    another one in training the student classifier. This can be
    attributed to our finding that PATE, in essence, can easily be
    modified to take advantage of pre-trained models (see the design of
    Prom-PATE in Section [[3.2]{.ltx_text
    .ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.SS2 "3.2 Prom-PATE ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}).
    Such efficient re-use of the public data can be highly beneficial to
    the resulting DP classifier accuracy, as shown in
    Table [[2]{.ltx_text
    .ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 3.3 Why Prom-PATE is Beneficial to DP? ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
    where Prom-PATE, Prom-PATE w/o pre-trained classifier, and PATE
    means utilizing public data two, one, and zero times, respectively.
    Obviously, the accuracy grows with the increased number of times for
    utilizing public data.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S3.I2.i3}

    ::: {#S3.I2.i3.p1 .ltx_para}
    [(C3)]{#S3.I2.i3.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} Because of the
    cross-domain capability of
    VP/MR \[[35](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref},
    [8](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref},
    [37](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref}\],
    even if the distribution of the dataset for the source model (used
    in training re-teacher models) is highly different from the
    distribution of the sensitive dataset, re-teacher models can still
    successfully attain high accuracies, which consequently improve the
    accuracy of the resulting DP classifier. Experiment evidence can be
    found in Section [[4.4]{.ltx_text
    .ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.SS4 "4.4 Cross-Domain Dataset Evaluation ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}.
    :::

### [4.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Datasets and Implementation Details {#datasets-and-implementation-details .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
The CIFAR-10 dataset contains 60,000 $32 \times 32$ RGB images with 10
classes. CIFAR-10 is widely used for benchmarking in image
classification and serves as one of the performance evaluation datasets
in the realm of privatized ML.

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px1 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Cross Domain Dataset. {#cross-domain-dataset. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px1.p1 .ltx_para}
To evaluate how Prom-PATE behaves in private domain adaptation with a
large domain gap, we consider Blood-MNIST in our experiments. The
Blood-MNIST
dataset \[[38](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref}\]
contains images of blood cells sampled from uninfected patients, with an
original shape of $3 \times 360 \times 363$. It contains 17,092 images
of 8 different blood cells (11,959 of training and 3421 of testing) and
has been processed to the size of
$3 \times 28 \times 28$ \[[38](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref}\].
We note that the sample distribution of Blood-MNIST is highly different
from the sample distribution of ImageNet because the images in
Blood-MNIST are taken under microscopic devices and planar in sight. Due
to the large domain gap between Blood-MNIST and ImageNet, we consider
Blood-MNIST in our experiments to resolve the concern of
[(C3)]{#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px1.p1.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. Please see
Section [[4.4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.SS4 "4.4 Cross-Domain Dataset Evaluation ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
for more details.
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Implementation Details. {#implementation-details. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
All of the experiment results below are derived by averaging the results
from three independent experiments. We use the official pre-trained
models provided by PyTorch and set the parameters to default values for
all pre-trained models. Regarding the training of each re-teacher model,
since the source model is pre-trained on ImageNet, the visual prompt has
a dimension of $224 \times 224$. When training the re-teacher model, we
optimize the model with Adam whilst using a learning rate of $0.05$ with
a decay rate of $70\%$, batch size of $16$, and training epoch of $10$.
In Section [[4.7]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.SS7 "4.7 Binary Mask in Visual Prompting ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
we also investigate the effect of the binary mask $M$ on visual prompt
performance. For label mapping, we randomly select ten classes from the
1,000 source classes as a one-to-one mapping. We also investigate using
FC layers as the label mapping function in Section [[4.8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.SS8 "4.8 Label Mapping Techniques ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.SSS0.Px2.p2 .ltx_para}
For the training of the student model, similar to the setting in
PATE \[[31](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}\],
in the case of CIFAR-10, the student has access to 9,000 samples that
are partially labeled through the noisy aggregation mechanism (step (b)
in Prom-PATE) in Section [[3.2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.SS2 "3.2 Prom-PATE ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}.
The performance is evaluated on the remaining 1,000 samples in the
testing set. Meanwhile, in the case of
Blood-MNIST \[[38](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref}\],
the student has access to 2,421 samples that are as well partially
labeled with privacy. The performance is evaluated on the remaining
1,000 samples in the testing set.
:::

target="_blank"}]{.ltx_note_content}]{.ltx_note_outer}]{#footnote3
.ltx_note .ltx_role_footnote}, Prom-PATE achieves a meaningful
improvement in accuracy. We also note that, unlike the other
approaches \[[14](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref},
[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref},
[19](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref},
[33](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref},
[11](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref},
[5](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}\],
Prom-PATE enjoys great flexibility in replacing source models (in
re-teacher models) by the latest classifiers, so as to effortlessly
improve the accuracy.

### [4.9 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Rescale Ratio in Visual Prompting {#rescale-ratio-in-visual-prompting .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS9.p1 .ltx_para}
Usually, in VP/MR, the image from the target domain needs to be rescaled
and surrounded by trainable noises, as shown in Eq. ([[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S3.E1 "1 ‣ Training re-teacher Models. ‣ 3.2 Prom-PATE ‣ 3 Main Approach ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}).
The resulting ${\hat{x}}_{S}$ can then be fed into the source model. A
higher rescale ratio generally leads to better performance. The
rationale is that a higher rescale ratio provides more information from
the target domain, which enables the re-teacher model to generate better
visual prompts that can more effectively guide the source model in
learning the relevant features of the target domain. However, a too-high
rescale ratio could potentially result in overfitting to the target
domain, leading to poor generalization performance. Hence, one strikes a
balance between providing sufficient information from the target domain
and avoiding overfitting. In our experiments, a rescale ratio of $0.6$
achieves the best performance.
:::

<figure id="S4.T11" class="ltx_table">
<div id="S4.T11.15"
class="ltx_inline-block ltx_align_center ltx_transformed_outer"
style="width:216.8pt;height:102.2pt;vertical-align:-0.0pt;">
<span class="ltx_transformed_inner"
style="transform:translate(-6.2pt,2.9pt) scale(0.945940584040349,0.945940584040349) ;">
</span>
</div>
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_table">Table 11: </span>Effect
on the rescale ratio of target Data. The number of queries is 1000 in

accuracy (%), and threshold, respectively.</figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S4.SS9.p2 .ltx_para}
As observed from Table [[11]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.T11 "Table 11 ‣ 4.9 Rescale Ratio in Visual Prompting ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
rescaling $x_{T}$ to $192 \times 192$ for visual prompting achieved the
highest utility. As explained in Section [[4.7]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.SS7 "4.7 Binary Mask in Visual Prompting ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
the rescale size provides a ratio between the trainable parameter
$\omega_{1}$ and target data $x_{T}$. Too many noise parameters and a
small target image might degrade performance due to the quality of the
target image and insufficient data. Conversely, a larger target image
and fewer parameters of $\omega_{1}$ might cause sub-optimal input
transformation from target to source, leading to a poor prompt.
:::

::: {#S5 .section .ltx_section}
## [5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Conclusion {#conclusion .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S5.p1 .ltx_para}
In this paper, we conducted a comprehensive study and discovered a new
benefit of VP in DP. In particular, we propose Prom-PATE, a new
VP-empowered training method for constructing DP classifiers. Prom-PATE
leverages VP to assist in the adaptation of pre-trained models in a more
efficient way without losing privacy. Empirical evaluations show that
Prom-PATE provides state-of-the-art performance compared to several
baselines and existing works. We also find that Prom-PATE achieves an
even better accuracy gain when the target task has a sufficient domain
gap against the pre-trained model (i.e., the ImageNet to Blood-MNIST
setting), demonstrating the generality of Prom-PATE. Our findings
suggest that VP is a promising approach to facilitating further research
in building DP classifiers that improve or even extinguish the
privacy-utility trade-off.
:::

### [6.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Privacy Analysis of Prom-PATE {#privacy-analysis-of-prom-pate .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S6.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
Here, we first present the definitions for
$(\epsilon,\delta)$-differential privacy ($(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP) and
its variant, Rènyi Differential Privacy (RDP). After that, we prove that
Prom-PATE satisfies $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP.
:::

::: {#Thmdefinition1 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_definition}
###### [[Definition 1]{#Thmdefinition1.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[ ]{#Thmdefinition1.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}(Differential Privacy, DP)[.]{#Thmdefinition1.3.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#definition-1-differential-privacy-dp. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmdefinition1.p1 .ltx_para}
[A randomized algorithm $\mathcal{M}$ is $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-DP if
for all
$\mathcal{S} \subseteq {\text{𝑅𝑎𝑛𝑔𝑒}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{M})}}$ and
for any neighboring datasets $\mathcal{D}$ and
$\mathcal{D}^{\prime}$,]{#Thmdefinition1.p1.5.5 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}

  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     ${{\text{𝑃𝑟}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{{\mathcal{M}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{D})}} \in \mathcal{S}}\rbrack}} \leq {{e^{\varepsilon}\operatorname{}\text{𝑃𝑟}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{{\mathcal{M}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{D}^{\prime})}} \in \mathcal{S}}\rbrack}} + \delta}}.$      [(5)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
:::
:::

::: {#S6.SS1.p2 .ltx_para}
In Definition [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.EGx5 "Definition 1 (Differential Privacy, DP). ‣ 6.1 Privacy Analysis of Prom-PATE ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
$\mathcal{D}$ and $\mathcal{D}^{\prime}$ are neighboring if
$\mathcal{D}$ can be obtained by adding or removing one sample from
$\mathcal{D}^{\prime}$
:::

::: {#Thmdefinition2 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_definition}
###### [[Definition 2]{#Thmdefinition2.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[ ]{#Thmdefinition2.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}(Rènyi Differential Privacy, RDP)[.]{#Thmdefinition2.3.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#definition-2-rènyi-differential-privacy-rdp. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmdefinition2.p1 .ltx_para}
[A randomized algorithm $\mathcal{M}$ is
$(\alpha,{\epsilon\operatorname{}{(\alpha)}})$-RDP with $\alpha > 1$ if
for any neighboring datasets $\mathcal{D}$ and $\mathcal{D}^{\prime}$,
[]{#Thmdefinition2.p1.5.5.1 .ltx_text
style="font-size:70%;"}]{#Thmdefinition2.p1.5.5 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $D_{\alpha}\left( \mathcal{M}(\mathcal{D}) \middle| \middle| \mathcal{M}\left( \mathcal{D}^{\prime} \right) \right) = \frac{1}{\alpha - 1}\log{\mathbb{E}}_{x \sim {\mathcal{M}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{D}^{\prime})}}}\left\lbrack \left( \frac{\text{𝑃𝑟}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {(\mathcal{D}) = x} \right\rbrack}{\text{𝑃𝑟}\operatorname{}\left\lbrack {\left( \mathcal{D}^{\prime} \right) = x} \right\rbrack} \right)^{\alpha - 1} \right\rbrack \leq \epsilon(\alpha),$      [(6)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------

[where $D_{\alpha}$ is the Rényi divergence of order
$\alpha$.]{#Thmdefinition2.p1.7.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::
:::

::: {#Thmtheorem1 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_theorem}
###### [[Theorem 1]{#Thmtheorem1.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[ ]{#Thmtheorem1.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}(Sequential Composition on RDP \[[25](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref}\])[.]{#Thmtheorem1.3.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#theorem-1-sequential-composition-on-rdp-25. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmtheorem1.p1 .ltx_para}
[If the mechanism $\mathcal{M}_{1}$ satisfies
$(\alpha,\epsilon_{1})$-RDP and the mechanism $\mathcal{M}_{2}$
satisfies $(\alpha,\epsilon_{2})$-RDP, then
$\mathcal{M}_{2} \circ \mathcal{M}_{1}$ satisfies
$(\alpha,{\epsilon_{1} + \epsilon_{2}})$-RDP.]{#Thmtheorem1.p1.6.6
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::
:::

::: {#Thmtheorem2 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_theorem}
###### [[Theorem 2]{#Thmtheorem2.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[ ]{#Thmtheorem2.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}(Translation of RDP \[[26](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref}\])[.]{#Thmtheorem2.3.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#theorem-2-translation-of-rdp-26. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmtheorem2.p1 .ltx_para}
[If a mechanism $\mathcal{M}$ satisfies $(\alpha,\epsilon)$-RDP, then
$\mathcal{M}$ satisfies
$({\epsilon + \frac{\log{({1/\delta})}}{\alpha - 1}},\delta)$-DP for any
$\delta \in {(0,1)}$.]{#Thmtheorem2.p1.5.5 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::
:::

::: {#S6.SS1.p3 .ltx_para}
With the above definition and results, in Theorem [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#Thmtheorem3 "Theorem 3. ‣ 6.1 Privacy Analysis of Prom-PATE ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
we prove that Prom-PATE satisfies $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP.
:::

::: {#Thmtheorem3 .ltx_theorem .ltx_theorem_theorem}
###### [[Theorem 3]{#Thmtheorem3.1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_theorem}[.]{#Thmtheorem3.2.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} {#theorem-3. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_title_theorem}

::: {#Thmtheorem3.p1 .ltx_para}
[Prom-PATE satisfies $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP.]{#Thmtheorem3.p1.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::
:::

::: {#S6.SS1.1 .ltx_proof}
###### Proof. {#proof. .ltx_title .ltx_runin .ltx_font_italic .ltx_title_proof}

::: {#S6.SS1.1.p1 .ltx_para}
Basically, Prom-PATE follows the framework of PATE. One can find that
the re-teacher models will not be released according to the design of
PATE. Therefore, the only step that Prom-PATE "touches" the sensitive
dataset is when the student model queries the re-teacher ensemble for
labeling the unlabelled samples. Hence, by leveraging proposition $8$ in
\[[31](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref}\],
we know that the Confident-GNMax fulfills
$(\alpha,\frac{\alpha}{2\operatorname{}\sigma^{2}})$-RDP guarantee.
Here, consider the case where Prom-PATE has $T$ unlabeled samples that
need to be labeled by the Confident-GNMax result before the training of
the student model. According to Theorem [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#Thmtheorem1 "Theorem 1 (Sequential Composition on RDP [25]). ‣ 6.1 Privacy Analysis of Prom-PATE ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
Prom-PATE satisfies
$(\alpha,{T \cdot \frac{\alpha}{2\operatorname{}\sigma^{2}}})$-RDP.
Hence, equivalently, Prom-PATE satisfies $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP for any
$\delta \in {(0,1)}$, where
$\epsilon = {{T \cdot \frac{\alpha}{2\operatorname{}\sigma^{2}}} + \frac{\log{({1/\delta})}}{\alpha - 1}}$
according to Theorem [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#Thmtheorem2 "Theorem 2 (Translation of RDP [26]). ‣ 6.1 Privacy Analysis of Prom-PATE ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}.
∎
:::
:::

::: {#S6.SS2.SSS0.Px2 .section .ltx_paragraph}
#### Additional Experiment Results for High-Resolution Images. {#additional-experiment-results-for-high-resolution-images. .ltx_title .ltx_title_paragraph}

::: {#S6.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p1 .ltx_para}
Here, we present additional experiment results for high-resolution
images. CelebA is a popular dataset that contains colorful celebrity
images of different sizes. All of CelebA images were rescaled into
$64 \times 64$ colorful images in our experiments. Based on CelebA, we
consider CelebA-Gender and CelebA-Hair. In particular, CelebA-Gender is
for binary classification with gender as the label. CelebA-Hair is for
the three-class classification with hair color (black/blonde/brown) as
the label. On the other hand, FFHQ contains 70000 $128 \times 128$
colorful facial images with gender as labels.
:::

::: {#S6.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p2 .ltx_para}
With the comparison between Table [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.T4 "Table 4 ‣ 4.2 Ablation Study of Prom-PATE ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
and Tables [[18]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T18 "Table 18 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}$\sim$ [[20]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T20 "Table 20 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
we can see that when images of higher resolutions are considered,
Prom-PATE reaches a lower accuracy. A potential explanation is that
because the images from the target task will be rescaled, certain
features in the images from the target task will disappear and
consequently have a negative impact on the resulting accuracy.
:::

::: {#S6.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p3 .ltx_para}
With the comparison among Tables [[18]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T18 "Table 18 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
[[19]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T19 "Table 19 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
and  [[20]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T20 "Table 20 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
we can see that Prom-PATE outperforms Transfer-PATE in nearly all cases.
Nonetheless, we can also find in Table [[18]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T18 "Table 18 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
that Prom-PATE only slightly outperforms Transfer-PATE. Such a minor
victory of Prom-PATE comes from the fact that the number of labels
returned by the noisy aggregation in Prom-PATE does not have a
significant increase compared to that in Transfer-PATE (e.g., see the
row [Answered Queries]{#S6.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p3.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} in Table [[18]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T18 "Table 18 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}).
The similarity between the numbers of labels returned by the noisy
aggregation in Prom-PATE and Transfer-PATE can be attributed to the
similar training result of the teacher models. A potential explanation
for such a phenomenon is that the CelebA-Gender is a binary
classification task, which is easy for both Prom-PATE and Transfer-PATE.
By comparing Table [[18]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T18 "Table 18 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
and Table [[19]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T19 "Table 19 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
we can see that the accuracy difference between Prom-PATE and
Transfer-PATE is becoming clear because CelebA-Hair is a three-class
classification task. When considering Table [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S4.T5 "Table 5 ‣ 4.4 Cross-Domain Dataset Evaluation ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
and Table [[19]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T19 "Table 19 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
despite the different context in CIFAR-10 and CelebA-Hair, we can use
them as evidence for claiming that Prom-PATE works especially better
than Transfer-PATE in the multi-class classification task.
:::

::: {#S6.SS2.SSS0.Px2.p4 .ltx_para}
Tables [[18]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T18 "Table 18 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref},
[[19]{.ltx_text

and  [[20]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2303.12247#S6.T20 "Table 20 ‣ Additional Experiment Results for Cross-Domain Tasks. ‣ 6.2 Additional Experiments ‣ 6 Supplementary Material ‣ Exploring the Benefits of Visual Prompting in Differential Privacy"){.ltx_ref}
also report the accuracies when two different semi-supervised learning

.ltx_font_italic},

.ltx_role_footnote} and FreeMatch[^5^[[^5^[5]{.ltx_tag
