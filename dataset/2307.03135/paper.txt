::: ltx_abstract
###### Abstract {#abstract .ltx_title .ltx_title_abstract}

Large vision-language models have achieved outstanding performance, but
their size and computational requirements make their deployment on
resource-constrained devices and time-sensitive tasks impractical. Model
distillation, the process of creating smaller, faster models that
maintain the performance of larger models, is a promising direction
towards the solution. This paper investigates the distillation of visual
representations in large teacher vision-language models into lightweight
student models using a small- or mid-scale dataset. Notably, this study
focuses on open-vocabulary out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, a
challenging problem that has been overlooked in previous model
distillation literature. We propose two principles from vision and
language modality perspectives to enhance student's OOD generalization:
[(1)]{#id1.id1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} by better imitating teacher's
visual representation space, and carefully promoting better coherence in
vision-language alignment with the teacher; [(2)]{#id1.id1.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold} by enriching the teacher's language representations with
informative and finegrained semantic attributes to effectively
distinguish between different labels. We propose several metrics and
conduct extensive experiments to investigate their techniques. The
results demonstrate significant improvements in zero-shot and few-shot
student performance on open-vocabulary out-of-distribution
classification, highlighting the effectiveness of our proposed
approaches. Our code will be released at [this
link](https://github.com/xuanlinli17/large_vlm_distillation_ood){.ltx_ref
.ltx_href target="_blank"}.
:::

## [1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Introduction {#introduction .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S1.p1 .ltx_para}
In recent years, there has been a significant growth and development of
large vision language models (large VLMs) such as
CLIP \[[44](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref}\],
GLIP \[[33](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref}\],
OFA \[[55](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref}\],
SimVLM \[[59](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib59){.ltx_ref}\],
BEiT \[[56](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref}\],
Florence \[[66](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib66){.ltx_ref}\],
and
Flamingo \[[2](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}\],
which have been pretrained on massive amounts of internet-scale data.
These models have demonstrated enormous potential in a wide range of
downstream applications, including classification/detection,
image-to-text generation, and vision-language reasoning, especially for
the out-of-distribution (OOD) samples and open-vocabulary settings.
Despite their potential, the large model sizes, high computational
resource requirements, and inefficient inference speed of these models
restrict their deployment on mobile and IoT edge
devices \[[26](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib26){.ltx_ref},
[50](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref}\],
as well as in scenarios like robotic
control \[[5](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib5){.ltx_ref}\]
that require rapid network inference. Thus, it would be ideal if we can
obtain small and compact models that still possess strong
generalizability towards diverse open-set concepts encountered in the
real world.
:::

::: {#S1.p2 .ltx_para}
We note that the internet-scale data has endowed foundation models with
well-aligned vision and language representation spaces across diverse
domains and datasets. Ideally, if such representation spaces can be
perfectly distilled into smaller models, the resulting models can
naturally possess similar open-set OOD generalization ability as their
larger counterparts. However, it is usually not the case, as
demonstrated in our later experiments. Therefore, we ask the following
question: [How to effectively distill representation spaces of large
vision-language teacher models to benefit OOD generalization of smaller
student models?]{#S1.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::

::: {#S1.p3 .ltx_para}
In this study, we investigate the principles and techniques for
distilling visual representations from large vision-language models
using small- to mid-scale datasets, with a specific focus on
out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization. Although large-scale datasets
with image-text pairs exist, as a pioneer work in this direction, we
argue that small- to mid-scale datasets has many practical scenarios for
vision application researchers (e.g., for robotics), because this is
more flexible, allowing for faster research and development cycles with
fewer resource dependencies. Additionally, as we shall see, extensive
experiments yield a deeper understanding of the representation spaces of
vision-language models, which can be beneficial for future tasks like
distilling image-based foundation models for
detection \[[27](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib27){.ltx_ref},
[55](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref}\],
segmentation \[[29](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib29){.ltx_ref},
[13](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib13){.ltx_ref}\],
and other data modalities like 3D
geometries \[[64](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib64){.ltx_ref},
[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref},
[34](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib34){.ltx_ref}\].
To keep study focused and fundamental, we also choose the image
classification task to explore many possible strategies.
:::

::: {#S1.p4 .ltx_para}
Based on extensive experimental results, we propose to preserve the
internal structure of the representation spaces. Specifically, we
propose techniques to maintain the relationship between the visual and
language representation spaces of the teacher models and a novel
strategy to enhance the text representation used during distillation.
Across our study, we make the following contributions:\
[1)]{#S1.p4.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We motivate the ability for
students to generalize towards out-of-distribution (OOD) concepts by
designing several metrics that measure the visual representation space
consistency and the vision-language alignment consistency between the
student and the teacher vision-language model.\
[2)]{#S1.p4.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We find that by better
imitating teacher's visual representation space, and carefully promoting
better coherence in vision-language alignment with the teacher, we
substantially strengthen student's OOD generalization ability.\
[3)]{#S1.p4.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We further improve student's
OOD generalization ability by enriching teacher's language
representations with more informative, finegrained, and meaningful
semantic attributes to effectively distinguish between different
labels.\
[4)]{#S1.p4.1.4 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We conduct a thorough
experimental analysis to understand the efficacy and impact of our
techniques on students' OOD generalization ability.
:::
:::

## [2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Related Work {#related-work .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Vision-Language Models (VLMs)]{#S2.p1.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}.
Recent years have witnessed tremendous progress on vision language
models \[[44](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib44){.ltx_ref},
[33](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib33){.ltx_ref},
[55](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref},
[59](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib59){.ltx_ref},
[56](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib56){.ltx_ref},
[66](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib66){.ltx_ref},
[2](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib2){.ltx_ref}\].
Various lines of approaches have been proposed to improve the downstream
task performance of VLMs, such as prompt
learning \[[72](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib72){.ltx_ref},
[65](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib65){.ltx_ref},
[67](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib67){.ltx_ref},
[73](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib73){.ltx_ref}\],
prompt
engineering \[[43](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib43){.ltx_ref},
[24](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib24){.ltx_ref},
[35](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib35){.ltx_ref}\],
and finetuning via vision-language
fusion \[[48](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib48){.ltx_ref},
[57](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib57){.ltx_ref},
[30](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib30){.ltx_ref}\].
Different from these works that utilize existing VLMs on downstream
tasks, our work studies distilling large VLM's feature space structures
towards smaller student networks. Specifically, we investigate
principles that effectively leverage the visual representation spaces
and vision-language alignments in large VLMs to enhance the OOD
generalization ability of students.
:::

::: {#S2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Network Distillation on VLMs]{#S2.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. The
teacher-student distillation
framework \[[25](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref}\]
aims to distill teacher model knowledge into student models, thereby
improving their downstream task performance. A classic setting considers
teachers and students to be vision-only. In particular, distilling image
representations from teacher visual backbones, such as
CNNs \[[22](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib22){.ltx_ref}\]
and Vision
Transformers \[[15](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref}\],
have been
well-studied \[[53](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib53){.ltx_ref},
[39](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib39){.ltx_ref},
[36](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib36){.ltx_ref},
[8](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib8){.ltx_ref},
[52](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib52){.ltx_ref},
[42](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib42){.ltx_ref},
[17](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib17){.ltx_ref},
[62](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib62){.ltx_ref}\].
With the success of large-scale VLMs, recent works start to distill
their powerful multimodal visual and language representations towards
various downstream tasks, such as object
detection \[[20](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib20){.ltx_ref},
[37](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib37){.ltx_ref}\],
visual-language
reasoning \[[58](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib58){.ltx_ref},
[11](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib11){.ltx_ref},
[16](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib16){.ltx_ref}\],
scene
understanding \[[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib41){.ltx_ref}\],
or adopt distillation during vision language model pretraining to
further enhance their performance
 \[[31](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib31){.ltx_ref},
[32](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib32){.ltx_ref},
[14](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib14){.ltx_ref},
[70](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib70){.ltx_ref},
[51](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib51){.ltx_ref}\].
:::

::: {#S2.p3 .ltx_para}
As the real world is full of concepts unseen during model training, it
is essential for student models to attain strong OOD generalization
capabilities. However, prior works have rarely [carefully]{#S2.p3.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} studied how to design distillation
approaches to enhance such ability. In this work, we propose principles
and approaches to improve student's OOD generalization ability, and we
carefully analyze their efficacies through various metrics and extensive
experiments.
:::
:::

::: {#S3 .section .ltx_section}
## [3 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Overview {#overview .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S3.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Problem Setup]{#S3.p1.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. We distill a large
vision-language teacher model $T$ (e.g., CLIP ViT-L/14) to a small
student image model $S$ (e.g., ResNet18) by focusing on
out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization for open-vocabulary object
classification. We choose small- or mid-scale datasets to achieve the
distillation so that the distillation process is flexible for fast
research cycle and has less resource dependency.
:::

::: {#S3.p2 .ltx_para}
The teacher consists of an image encoder
$T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{( \cdot )}$ and a text encoder
$T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{( \cdot )}$. During distillation, we keep
the flexibility of the existing teacher text encoder for the open-set
setting, and we let the student model $S$ be vision-only, i.e.,
$S = S_{\text{img}}$. Through this process, we hope that $S$ not only
achieves high prediction accuracy on seen labels $\mathcal{Y}_{id}$, but
also attains strong generalization ability on out-of-distribution labels
$\mathcal{Y}_{ood}$. In addition, we train students from scratch to
avoid label contamination, allowing us to more carefully assess and
understand their OOD generalization ability.
:::

::: {#S3.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Experiment Setup]{#S3.p3.15.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. We are given a
training (distillation) dataset $\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}$, an
in-distribution evaluation dataset $\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$, and an
out-of-distribution evaluation dataset $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$. Each
dataset consists of image-label pairs $\{{(\mathbf{x},y)}\}$. For
${(\mathbf{x},y)} \in {\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}} \cup \mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}}$,
we have $y \in \mathcal{Y}_{id}$, and for
${(\mathbf{x},y)} \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$, we have
$y \in \mathcal{Y}_{ood}$, where $\mathcal{Y}_{id},\mathcal{Y}_{ood}$
denote in-distribution and out-of-distribution label sets, and
${\mathcal{Y}_{id} \cap \mathcal{Y}_{ood}} = \varnothing$. We evaluate
the student on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$, along with zero-shot and
few-shot generalization on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$. For few-shot
learning, we finetune our student models (we will show later in our
ablation study that finetuning achieves much higher performance on
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$ than training-free retrieval). We also adopt
balanced training batches where at most half of samples come from
few-shot data and the rest from $\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}$. This
strategy leads to similar performance on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$
before and after finetuning. More implementation details are presented
in Appendix [[B]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S2a "B Training Details and Hyperparameters for Main Experiments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S3.p4 .ltx_para}
We adopt a diverse collection of recognition tasks using small to
medium-scale datasets, including
CaltechBirds \[[61](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib61){.ltx_ref}\],
StanfordCars \[[28](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib28){.ltx_ref}\],
Flower102 \[[40](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib40){.ltx_ref}\],
Food101 \[[4](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib4){.ltx_ref}\],
SUN397 \[[63](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib63){.ltx_ref}\],
and
tiered-ImageNet \[[45](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib45){.ltx_ref},
[12](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib12){.ltx_ref}\].
We split the dataset labels such that
${|\mathcal{Y}_{id}|} = {|\mathcal{Y}_{ood}|}$, except tiered-ImageNet,
which comes with an existing split. Detailed dataset statistics are
listed in Appendix [[A]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S1a "A Dataset Statistics ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S3.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Organization of Exposition]{#S3.p5.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. Like
other papers on understanding neural network
behaviors \[[21](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib21){.ltx_ref},
[3](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib3){.ltx_ref},
[23](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib23){.ltx_ref},
[54](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib54){.ltx_ref}\],
we will propose techniques and invent metrics to help understand the
effectiveness of each technique. Following the organization of a
representative work of this
kind \[[68](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib68){.ltx_ref}\],
we present different techniques in a waterfall style and organize the
presentation of each technique by grouping the approach, metric, and
results together.
:::

::: {#S3.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Approach Overview]{#S3.p6.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. Our approach
aims to preserve the structure of the visual representation space and
its relationship with the text representation space inherited from the
teacher model to enhance the student's OOD generalization ability.
Specificially, [(1)]{#S3.p6.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} In
Sec. [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4 "4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
we show that by better imitating teacher's visual representation space,
and carefully promoting better coherence in vision-language alignment
with the teacher, students achieve substantially better OOD
generalization. [(2)]{#S3.p6.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} In
Sec. [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S5 "5 Language Representation Enrichment ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
we show that by enriching teacher's language representations, there are
more meaningful attributes to effectively distinguish between different
labels, thereby further enhancing student's OOD generalization ability.
A summary of our main experimental findings is illustrated in
Fig. [[1]{.ltx_text

and Fig. [[2]{.ltx_text

## [4 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments {#teacher-student-visual-space-and-vision-language-alignments .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S4.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Better Imitating Teacher's Visual Representation Space {#better-imitating-teachers-visual-representation-space .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS1.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Naive baseline without visual space alignment between teacher and
student]{#S4.SS1.p1.12.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. A straightforward
approach to training a student model is to directly align its visual
representation with teacher's language representation through
[only]{#S4.SS1.p1.12.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} the following
contrastive loss:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ---------------------------------------------------------
     $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x},y)}$        $= {\sum\limits_{y^{\prime}} - {\mathbf{1}_{y^{\prime} = y}\operatorname{}{\log P_{S}}\operatorname{}{(\left. y^{\prime} \middle| \mathbf{x} \right.)}}}$                                                                                                                                              [(1)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equationgroup .ltx_align_right}
     $P_{S}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \mathbf{x} \right.)}$   $= \frac{\exp{({{\cos{({S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}},{T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l\operatorname{}{(y)}})}})}}/\tau})}}{\sum_{y^{\prime} \in \mathcal{Y}}{\exp{({{\cos{({S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}},{T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l\operatorname{}{(y^{\prime})}})}})}}/\tau})}}}$      
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ---------------------------------------------------------

Here $\tau$ is the temperature parameter.
$S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}$ denotes the feature of $\mathbf{x}$
extracted by a student image model $S$.
${l\operatorname{}{(y)}} = {\text{prompt} + {\text{description}\operatorname{}{(y)}}}$
is a language generation function that maps the label $y$ to its natural
language representation, which consists of a prompt and a description of
$y$. For this baseline, we use the suggested prompt "A photo of a" from
the CLIP paper, and let description($y$) to be simply the class label
name (e.g., "lotus"). From here on, we assume that all teacher and
student representations are normalized, i.e.,
${S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}}\leftarrow\frac{S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}}{\left\| {S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} \right\|_{2}}$,
${T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l\operatorname{}{(y)}})}}\leftarrow\frac{T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l\operatorname{}{(y)}})}}{\left\| {T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l\operatorname{}{(y)}})}} \right\|_{2}}$.
In this case,
${\cos{({S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}},{T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l\operatorname{}{(y)}})}})}} = {1 - \frac{\left\| {{S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} - {T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l\operatorname{}{(y)}})}}} \right\|_{2}^{2}}{2}}$.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.p2 .ltx_para}
Training student models solely using $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$ does not
enforce similarity between teacher and student visual representation
space structures. Consequently, student visual space structures tend to
overfit to training labels, hindering their OOD generalizability. This
motivates us to introduce auxiliary losses to align teacher and student
visual representation spaces, which we will describe next.
:::

class="math inline">ℒ<sub>mse</sub></span> (RN50)”, where the student is

challenging for students to <span id="S4.T2.18.1"
class="ltx_text ltx_font_italic">precisely</span> match teacher’s visual
features through <span class="math inline">ℒ<sub>mse</sub></span>.
Moreover, in conjunction with Tab. <a
href="https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T1"
class="ltx_ref"
title="Table 1 ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"><span
class="ltx_text ltx_ref_tag">1</span></a>, we observe that a lower
visual feature MSE is not necessary for better student OOD
generalization.</figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S4.SS1.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Directly fitting teacher visual features]{#S4.SS1.p3.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}. We note that teacher's visual representation space is
well-aligned with language across diverse datasets, and such alignment
demonstrates strong generalization across many domains. By imitating
teacher's visual space structure, we hope to enhance the ability for
student's visual space to generalize and extrapolate towards unseen
concepts, thereby implicitly enhancing the generalizability of student's
vision-language alignment and improving its OOD generalization.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.p4 .ltx_para}
A direct approach to achieve this is to align the teacher and student
visual representations through the Mean Squared-Error (MSE) loss:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathcal{L}_{\text{mse}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} = {\|{{S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} - {T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}}}\|}_{2}^{2}$$      [(2)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

In Tab. [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
we show that adding $\mathcal{L}_{\text{mse}}$ on top of
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$ improves student OOD generalization. However,
upon further examination, we find that [students face significant
challenges in precisely reproducing teacher's visual
representations]{#S4.SS1.p4.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}, as
evidenced by the substantial errors shown in Tab. [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 4.1 Better Imitating Teacher’s Visual Representation Space ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
Such errors persist even when the student and teacher networks possess
the same representation power (e.g., both ResNet50 networks). This
phenomenon highlights that achieving precise matching between teacher
and student's high-dimensional visual feature spaces is inherently
challenging, which can be attributed to differences in weight
initialization, training data, and the presence of local minima in the
loss landscape. Moreover, we later find that when students struggle to
precisely match teacher's visual features, they also struggle to
preserve teacher's [local visual space structure]{#S4.SS1.p4.2.2
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} and [relative visual feature
relationship]{#S4.SS1.p4.2.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} between
different images, hindering their OOD generalization ability.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Better imitating teacher visual representation space]{#S4.SS1.p5.3.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. Since precisely matching teacher's visual
features is inherently challenging, we propose to augment the training
objective with the following contrastive loss, which "softly" matches
teacher's visual features:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} = \frac{\exp{({- {{\|{{S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} - {T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}}}\|}_{2}^{2}/\tau}})}}{\sum_{\mathbf{x}^{\prime}}{\exp{({- {{\|{{S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} - {T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}^{\prime})}}}\|}_{2}^{2}/\tau}})}}}$$      [(3)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

By combining $\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}$ with
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$, we observe in Tab. [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}
that the student exhibits significantly better zero-shot and few-shot
OOD generalization ability across different datasets. Interestingly,
such improvement is accompanied by a larger distance between student and
teacher visual features, as shown in Tab. [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 4.1 Better Imitating Teacher’s Visual Representation Space ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
This finding suggests that a lower MSE visual feature matching loss,
which only captures the absolute distance to teacher's visual features,
does [not]{#S4.SS1.p5.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} necessarily imply
better visual space consistency. This is because [MSE does not consider
the coherence of relative visual feature relationships or local visual
space structures between teacher and student]{#S4.SS1.p5.2.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.p6 .ltx_para}
In the following paragraphs, we will develop several metrics to better
assess the teacher-student visual space consistency. These metrics
provide us with valuable insights into how $\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}$
facilitates students to achieve closer visual space proximity to the
teacher while yielding a deeper understanding of the teacher's visual
representation space.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.p7 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Quantifying teacher-student visual space alignment]{#S4.SS1.p7.11.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. To quantify how student models preserve
teacher's visual representation space structures, we propose two
metrics. For the first metric, we are motivated by the fact that if the
relative relationships among teacher's visual representations are
preserved, then for an image $\mathbf{x}$,
$S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}$ tends to be the closest to
$T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}$ rather than
$T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}^{\prime})}$ of some other
image $\mathbf{x}^{\prime} \neq \mathbf{x}$. The latter scenario tends
to break the relative feature relationship between $\mathbf{x}$ and
$\mathbf{x}^{\prime}$ that is originally present in the teacher, thereby
causing the student to have distinct visual feature manifold structure
from the teacher, as illustrated in Fig. [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ 4.1 Better Imitating Teacher’s Visual Representation Space ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}(a).
Furthermore, if $S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}$ is closer to
$T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}^{\prime})}$, then the
language feature closest to $S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}$ often
differs from the language feature closest to
$T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}$, and the latter is
typically the ground truth language label. Thus, such discrepancy often
results in erroneous label predictions. We can then formally define the
first metric as follows:

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{X})}} = \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathcal{X}|}{\mathbf{1}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{i = {\text{argmin}_{j}\operatorname{}{\|{{T_{\text{img}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}_{j})}} - {S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}_{i})}}}\|}_{2}^{2}}}\rbrack}}}{|\mathcal{X}|}$$      [(4)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

The second metric measures the proximity between local neighborhoods of
student visual features and teacher visual features. That is, for each
image, how much the $k$ images whose features have the closest proximity
to it (excluding itself) overlap between the student and the teacher
(see Fig. [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ 4.1 Better Imitating Teacher’s Visual Representation Space ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}b
for illustration). We can define this metric as follows:

  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathcal{M}_{\text{neigh}}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{X},k)}} = \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathcal{X}|}{|{{\text{KNN}\operatorname{}{(S,\mathbf{x}_{i},k)}} \cap {\text{KNN}\operatorname{}{(T_{\text{img}},\mathbf{x}_{i},k)}}}|}}{k\operatorname{}{|\mathcal{X}|}}$$      [(5)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

Here
${\text{KNN}\operatorname{}{(S,\mathbf{x}_{i},k)}} = \left. \{\text{argbottomk}_{j} \middle| {{|{{S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}_{j})}} - {S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}_{i})}}}|}|}_{2}\} \right.$
outputs the $k$ nearest-neighbor image-ids of
$S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}_{i})}$, and
$\text{KNN}\operatorname{}{(T_{\text{img}},\mathbf{x}_{i},k)}$ is
defined similarly. This metric is complementary to
$\mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}}$ as it only requires the set of neighbor image
ids to be identical between the student and the teacher, and does not
enforce these neighbor images to have the same relative feature
relationships.
:::

::: {#S4.SS1.p8 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
$\mathcal{L}_{\textbf{im-cst}}$[ improves teacher-student visual space
alignments through better relative and local visual space
coherence]{#S4.SS1.p8.13.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. We assess
$\mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}}$ and $\mathcal{M}_{\text{neigh}}$ on different
students and present the results in Tab. [[3]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 4.1 Better Imitating Teacher’s Visual Representation Space ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We find that students trained solely with $\mathcal{L}_{\text{mse}}$ and
without $\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}$ exhibit poor values for both
$\mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}}$ and $\mathcal{M}_{\text{neigh}}$. Notably,
$\mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}})}$
is only 0.03, indicating a large discrepancy between student and teacher
visual spaces, even on the training set. On the other hand, after
incorporating $\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}$, we observe significant
improvements of $\mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}}$ and
$\mathcal{M}_{\text{neigh}}$ on both $\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}$ and
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$. This demonstrates that the student exhibits
much better consistency in relative and local visual structures with the
teacher, both for seen and unseen concepts, thereby enhancing the
generalization and extrapolation ability of the student's visual space.
A better-aligned visual space also implicitly enables better
generalization and extrapolation in vision-language alignments (which we
show later), contributing to improved OOD performance. Furthermore, our
findings suggest a broader principle: when students face challenges in
precisely matching the teacher's visual space, the proximity of local
visual feature structures and relative visual feature relationships have
a greater impact on OOD generalization than the absolute distance to the
teacher's features.
:::
:::

::: {#S4.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [4.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Better Coherence with Teacher's Vision-Language Alignment Structure {#better-coherence-with-teachers-vision-language-alignment-structure .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S4.SS2.p1 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Motivation.]{#S4.SS2.p1.9.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} In
Section [[4.1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.SS1 "4.1 Better Imitating Teacher’s Visual Representation Space ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
we focused on improving the student's OOD generalization ability by
better aligning student-teacher visual spaces. Since teacher's visual
space is well-aligned with language across diverse concepts and domains,
a better student coherence with teacher's visual space
[implicitly]{#S4.SS2.p1.9.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} leads to better
vision-language (V-L) alignments. Naturally, an alternative perspective
to improve student's OOD generalization becomes to enhance its
[explicit]{#S4.SS2.p1.9.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} V-L alignments and
improve their coherence with the teacher's, where we previously only
used a simple contrastive V-L matching loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$.
Another motivation to focus on [explicit]{#S4.SS2.p1.9.4 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} V-L alignments arises from our finding that they play
an essential role to ensure precise and accurate V-L alignments,
especially when training on seen concepts or performing few-shot
learning on novel concepts. [Relying solely on implicit V-L alignments
is inadequate in these scenarios]{#S4.SS2.p1.9.5 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic}. This is evident in Tab. [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
where [solely]{#S4.SS2.p1.9.6 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} utilizing the
visual space alignment loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}$ yields better
performance on 0-shot $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$ (where classes are
unseen) but worse performance on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$ and 5-shot
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$ (where classes are seen). On the other hand,
by combining both implicit and explicit V-L alignment losses
($\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$), students
excel in all of $\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$, 0-shot
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$, and 5-shot $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$
scenarios. Therefore, by improving explicit V-L alignments, we not only
hope to further enhance student's 0-shot OOD generalization ability, but
also improve their performance on familiar concepts and their ability to
few-shot adapt to novel concepts.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Explicitly enhancing teacher-student vision-language alignment
coherency.]{#S4.SS2.p2.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} We note that while
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$ performs explicit V-L alignments, it has the
limitation of indiscriminately pushing an image away from all non
ground-truth language features, therefore disregarding teacher's
relative alignment relationship between the same image and different
language features. Furthermore, we find that even though preserving
teacher's relative V-L alignment structure is desirable, it may not
always be perfect due to potential misalignments between teacher image
features and their corresponding language labels. These misalignments
can introduce inconsistent noise during distillation, ultimately harming
student performance.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p3 .ltx_para}
Motivated by these observations, we propose to augment our training
objective with $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$, which effectively and
carefully preserves the teacher's vision-language alignment structure
while accounting for potential misalignments:

  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ---------------------------------------------------------
     $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x},k)}$                      $= I{(\mathbf{x})} \cdot \mathcal{D}_{\text{KL}}{(P_{T,\text{topk}}{( \cdot |\mathbf{x})}||P_{S,\text{topk}}{( \cdot |\mathbf{x})})}$                                                                                                                                                                                                                   [(6)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equationgroup .ltx_align_right}
     $I\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}$                                                  $= {\mathbf{1}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{{\text{argmax}_{y}\operatorname{}P_{T}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \mathbf{x} \right.)}} = {\text{label}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}}}\rbrack}}$                                                                                                                                                           
     $P_{\cdot ,\text{topk}}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \mathbf{x} \right.)}$   ${= \frac{\mathbf{1}_{y \in Y_{\text{topk}}}\operatorname{}P_{\cdot}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \mathbf{x} \right.)}}{\sum_{y \in Y_{\text{topk}}}{P_{\cdot}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \mathbf{x} \right.)}}}};{Y_{\text{topk}} = {\text{argtopk}_{y}\operatorname{}P_{T}\operatorname{}{(\left. y \middle| \mathbf{x} \right.)}}}$      
  -- --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ---------------------------------------------------------

Here $P_{T}$ and $P_{S}$ denote teacher and student label probabilities;
$I\operatorname{}{( \cdot )}$ filters out images misaligned with
language labels; and $k$ controls the number of most-similar language
features for each image. In our implementations, we find a larger $k$
beneficial for OOD generalization, and we choose $k = 256$.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p4 .ltx_para}
We demonstrate the effectiveness of $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$ in
Tab. [[1]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We find that by combining $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$ with
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}$, we further
improve student's ability to generalize towards OOD concepts.
Interestingly, we also observe that while $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$ and
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$ both explicitly perform V-L alignments,
adding them together yields significantly better student performance on
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$ and 5-shot $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$ than
solely keeping $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$. This observation is
distinct from those in the traditional model distillation
literature \[[25](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib25){.ltx_ref}\],
where distilling teacher logits alone from vision-only models typically
produces good student performance.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Quantifying teacher-student vision-language alignment
coherency.]{#S4.SS2.p5.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold} To better
understand the vision-language alignment proximity between teacher and
student, we propose a metric $\mathcal{M}_{\text{vlalign}}$ to quantify
such proximity:

  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ---------------------------------------------------------
     $\mathcal{M}_{\text{vlalign}}\operatorname{}{(\mathcal{X},k)}$   $= \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathcal{X}|}{\text{\#reverse\_pairs}\operatorname{}{({\text{arrS}\operatorname{}{(i,k)}})}}}{|\mathcal{X}|}$                                                  [(7)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equationgroup .ltx_align_right}
     $\text{arrS}\operatorname{}{(i,k)}$                              $= {\lbrack{\|{{S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x}_{i})}} - {T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l\operatorname{}{(y_{j})}})}}}\|}_{2}\rbrack}_{j \in {\mathcal{I}\operatorname{}{(i,k)}}}$      
     $\mathcal{I}\operatorname{}{(i,k)}$                              $= \text{argtopk}{({\lbrack - ||T_{\text{im}}{(\mathbf{x}_{i})} - T_{\text{txt}}{(l{(y_{j})})}||_{2})}\rbrack}_{j = 1}^{|\mathcal{Y}|})$                                               
  -- ---------------------------------------------------------------- ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ---------------------------------------------------------

Here $\text{\#reverse\_pairs}\operatorname{}{( \cdot )}$ measures the
number of reverse pairs $\{{(i,j)}:{a_{i} > a_{j}}\}$ in an array.
Overall, $\mathcal{M}_{\text{vlalign}}$ measures the extent to which the
distance ordering of the $k$ nearest language features to the teacher
image feature differs from the distance ordering of the same $k$
language features with respect to the student image feature. In other
words, a lower value of $\mathcal{M}_{\text{vlalign}}$ indicates a
higher degree of proximity to the teacher's vision-language alignment,
as teacher's relative language orderings with respect to each image
becomes better preserved.
:::

::: {#S4.SS2.p6 .ltx_para}
We assess $\mathcal{M}_{\text{vlalign}}$ on different students and
present the results in Tab. [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4.T4 "Table 4 ‣ 4.2 Better Coherence with Teacher’s Vision-Language Alignment Structure ‣ 4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We find that $\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}$ not only fosters strong
student-teacher visual space alignments, but also
[implicitly]{#S4.SS2.p6.5.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic} facilitates
effective V-L alignments along this process. This observation
illustrates that a better relative and local visual space coherence with
the teacher (indicated by better $\mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}}$ and
$\mathcal{M}_{\text{neigh}}$) can lead to enhanced alignment coherence
in the V-L space. Additionally, the inclusion of
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$ further strengthens teacher-student V-L
alignments. Such improved alignments are then effectively transferred to
unseen concepts, demonstrating that the student has acquired better V-L
alignment structures that generalize well to OOD scenarios.
:::

## [5 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Language Representation Enrichment {#language-representation-enrichment .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

<figure id="S5.T5" class="ltx_table">
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_table"><span id="S5.T5.17.5.1"
class="ltx_text" style="font-size:129%;">Table 5</span>: </span><span
id="S5.T5.9.4" class="ltx_text" style="font-size:129%;">Comparison
between different language representation enrichment strategies. The
three numbers <span
class="math inline"><em>x</em><sub>1</sub>/<em>x</em><sub>2</sub>/<em>x</em><sub>3</sub></span>
in each entry denote the evaluation performance on <span
class="math inline">𝒳<sub>id</sub></span>, zero-shot performance on
<span class="math inline">𝒳<sub>ood</sub></span>, and 5-shot performance
on <span class="math inline">𝒳<sub>ood</sub></span>,
respectively.</span></figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S5.p1 .ltx_para}
In Sec. [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4 "4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
we focused on improving the imitation of teacher's visual space and
promoting better coherence with teacher's vision-language alignment.
Throughout this process, we kept the language representations fixed.
However, the quality of language representations also plays a pivotal
role in student learning and inference. Ideally, language
representations should be capture precise, finegrained, and meaningful
semantic attributes, such that the student can effectively distinguish
between different labels. We therefore ask the following question: can
we leverage better and richer teacher language representations to
further enhance student's OOD generalization ability? We propose the
following candidate strategies:
:::

::: {#S5.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Enriching semantic details of label descriptions by prompting
LLMs]{#S5.p2.5.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. Previously, when we generate
language representations
${l\operatorname{}{(y)}} = {\text{prompt} + {\text{description}\operatorname{}{(y)}}}$
for student training, we adopted a simple strategy. In particular, for
the description of a label $y$, we merely used its label name, e.g.,
"lotus". However, these simplistic descriptions overlook many
finegrained properties of semantic categories, such as the shape, color,
and texture of flowers, along with the description of their petals,
leaves, and stems. In addition, we hope to automatically and efficiently
generate enriched language descriptions for a wide range of labels,
ensuring scalability for an arbitrary number of labels. Motivated by the
recent progress on instruction-finetuned large language models
(LLMs) \[[38](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref},
[9](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib9){.ltx_ref},
[46](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib46){.ltx_ref},
[60](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib60){.ltx_ref}\],
which have demonstrated impressive sequence generation abilities given
user prompts, we find these models well-suited for our goal. Therefore,
we propose to use
ChatGPT \[[6](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib6){.ltx_ref},
[38](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib38){.ltx_ref}\]
to generate category descriptions. We prompt ChatGPT with the following
instruction: [Use a single sentence to describe the appearance and shape
of {cls}. Only describe the shape and appearance]{#S5.p2.5.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_typewriter}. This allows ChatGPT to generate informative,
finegrained, and meaningful descriptions for target classes (e.g.,
[large, round, flat leaves; tall, slender stems; delicate petals in
shades of pink, white, or yellow]{#S5.p2.5.3 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_typewriter}), while keeping sequence lengths within the 77
token limit of the CLIP text encoder. We then set
$\text{description}\operatorname{}{(y)}$ by concatenating "a photo of
{cls}" with ChatGPT-generated class descriptions. Note that we still
keep the same vision-language alignment losses
($\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$) as
before.
:::

::: {#S5.p3 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Augmenting text through auxiliary captions]{#S5.p3.5.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}. Currently, during student training, there is only one
language description per category, i.e.,
${|{\{{l\operatorname{}{(y)}}:{{(\mathbf{x},y)} \in \mathcal{X}_{train}}\}}|} = {|\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}}|}$.
On the other hand, the number of training images significantly exceeds
the number of labels, i.e.,
${|\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}|} \gg {|\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}}|}$. We
therefore wish to generate language descriptions for each individual
image, such that we can substantially enrich the number of language
features during student training, which potentially benefits student
performance. To achieve this, we propose using
OFA \[[55](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib55){.ltx_ref}\]
to generate captions for each image, resulting in a new dataset
$\{{(\mathbf{x},{\text{cap}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}},y)}:{{(\mathbf{x},y)} \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}}\}$
augmented with captions. During student training, besides using the same
vision-language alignment losses $\mathcal{L}_{\text{cls}}$ and
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$ as before, we also adopt the following
auxiliary loss:

  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
     $${\mathcal{L}_{\text{cap}}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} = \frac{\exp{({{\cos{({S\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}},{T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({\text{cap}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}})}})}}/\tau})}}{\sum_{{(\mathbf{x}^{\prime},y^{\prime})}:{y^{\prime} \neq y}}\exp{(\cos{(S{(\mathbf{x})},T_{\text{txt}}{(\text{cap}{(\mathbf{x}^{\prime})})}/\tau)}}}$$      [(8)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
  -- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

The loss pushes $\mathbf{x}$ and its corresponding caption
$\text{cap}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}$ together while pulling away
from captions belonging to different categories. Our preliminary
experiments show that distinguishing captions belonging to the same
category could degrade student performance as they are usually similar.
[Note that we only incorporate captions for the auxiliary loss during
student training. For student inference and label predictions, we
continue to use the same $l\operatorname{}{(y)}$ as before.]{#S5.p3.8.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
:::

::: {#S5.p4 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Learning prompts to modify language representations]{#S5.p4.3.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. In previous experiments, we adopted a simple
prompt "a photo of" to generate $l\operatorname{}{(y)}$ for student
learning. By modifying prompts, we can alter the teacher's language
representation space and thereby influence vision-language alignment.
Furthermore, in real-world scenarios, student networks often need to
continuously learn new concepts and semantics, leading to ongoing
updates in their backbones. We therefore explore the potential of
performing prompt learning during student training on
$\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}}$ and few-shot learning on
$\mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}$. Note that our setting is different from many
prior settings on prompt
tuning \[[72](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib72){.ltx_ref},
[71](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib71){.ltx_ref},
[49](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib49){.ltx_ref},
[18](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib18){.ltx_ref}\],
where there are no OOD concepts, and vision backbones are kept fixed. We
adopt
CoOp \[[72](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib72){.ltx_ref}\]
and optimize 8 tokens as our prompt.
:::

::: {#S5.SS1 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.1 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Analyzing the Efficacy of Different Strategies {#analyzing-the-efficacy-of-different-strategies .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS1.p1 .ltx_para}
We adopt the aforementioned language-enriching strategies for student
learning, and we present the results in Tab. [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S5.T5 "Table 5 ‣ 5 Language Representation Enrichment ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We find that combining LLM-enriched label descriptions with auxiliary
captions yields the best OOD generalization. However, upon analyzing
their individual effectiveness, we find that [LLM-enriched label
descriptions provide significantly better zero-shot OOD benefit than
auxiliary captions, and solely relying on auxiliary captions only
marginally improves zero-shot OOD generalizability]{#S5.SS1.p1.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}. Upon further analysis, we find that many
generated captions only broadly describe objects and are much less
informative than ChatGPT-generated descriptions for distinguishing
fine-grained categories. For instance, in the StanfordCars dataset, a
generated caption for an [Acura Integra Type R 2001]{#S5.SS1.p1.1.2
.ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter} image is [a white car is parked in a
field]{#S5.SS1.p1.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter}, and solely
relying on the white color provides little information to distinguish
different car categories. Consequently, captions have limited impact on
enhancing the generalizability of student's vision-language alignment
structures.
:::

::: {#S5.SS1.p2 .ltx_para}
We also observe that learning prompts during student training harms its
zero-shot OOD generalization performance, suggesting that this approach
leads to the overfitting of vision-language alignments on the training
categories. However, when provided with a small number of samples from
OOD categories, the prompts can quickly adapt to these novel concepts,
enabling students to achieve the best few-shot OOD performance overall.
:::
:::

::: {#S5.SS2 .section .ltx_subsection}
### [5.2 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_subsection}Comparing Language Representation Spaces Before and After Enrichment {#comparing-language-representation-spaces-before-and-after-enrichment .ltx_title .ltx_title_subsection}

::: {#S5.SS2.p1 .ltx_para}
To gain further insights into the efficacy of our language enrichment
strategies, in this section, we analyze the changes in language space
structures before and after adopting such strategies. On the
out-of-distribution evaluation dataset of Flower102, we obtain text
features
${\{{T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l_{\text{new}}\operatorname{}{(y)}})}}\}}_{y = 1}^{|\mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}|}$
and
${\{{T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({l_{\text{old}}\operatorname{}{(y)}})}}\}}_{y = 1}^{|\mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}|}$,
where $l_{\text{new}}$ denotes the language generation function with LLM
enrichment, and $l_{\text{old}}$ uses simple label names as label
descriptions. We also obtain the average caption features for each
class, namely
${\{{\text{mean}\operatorname{}{\lbrack{\{{T_{\text{txt}}\operatorname{}{({\text{cap}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}})}}:{{\text{label}\operatorname{}{(\mathbf{x})}} = y}\}}\rbrack}}\}}_{y = 1}^{|\mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}|}$.
We then mean-center these 3 sets of text features, perform
Singular-Value Decomposition, and plot the resulting eigenvalues in
Fig. [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S5.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 5.2 Comparing Language Representation Spaces Before and After Enrichment ‣ 5 Language Representation Enrichment ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We observe that language features of $l_{\text{new}}$ contain many more
large eigenvalues than $l_{\text{old}}$, demonstrating that the text
space generated by $l_{\text{new}}$ confers more independent and
meaningful attributes to effectively distinguish between different
classes. On the other hand, the language space of auxiliary captions
only contains a small number of meaningful attributes, indicating that
auxiliary captions are not very helpful for distinguishing between
different labels. Additionally, in conjunction with Tab. [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S5.T5 "Table 5 ‣ 5 Language Representation Enrichment ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
we find that the advantages of richer attributes are concealed when
evaluating students on the in-distribution classes, since students tend
to overfit the existing attributes of these labels during training.
However, for OOD scenarios, the presence of more finegrained attributes
allows OOD text features to be more precisely aligned with image
features, especially when no vision-language alignment training is done
on these OOD samples (see Fig. [[2]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S0.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}
for an illustration). As a result, students exhibit better OOD
generalization ability.
:::

::: {#S5.SS2.p2 .ltx_para}
Furthermore, we analyze the average cosine similarity between all pairs
of text features within each of the three sets we introduced earlier. We
present the results in Tab. [[6]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S5.T6 "Table 6 ‣ 5.2 Comparing Language Representation Spaces Before and After Enrichment ‣ 5 Language Representation Enrichment ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We observe that LLM-enriched semantic details allow text features to
naturally separate further apart from each other, making it easier to
distinguish between different classes. In contrast, auxiliary captions
show limited effectiveness in achieving such separation between classes.
:::

## [6 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Ablations {#ablations .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S6.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we present further ablation studies to complement our
findings in Sec. [[4]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S4 "4 Teacher-Student Visual Space and Vision-Language Alignments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}
and Sec. [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S5 "5 Language Representation Enrichment ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
:::

::: {#S6.p2 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Control semantic details by prompting LLMs]{#S6.p2.1.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}. In Sec. [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S5 "5 Language Representation Enrichment ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
we found that descriptive semantic details in $l\operatorname{}{(y)}$
are particularly helpful for student generalization on
out-of-distribution concepts. We thus ask the following question: how
specific should semantic details be, and which semantic details are
helpful? We construct the following prompts to [control how ChatGPT
generates semantic details]{#S6.p2.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
(example generations in Appendix): [More Succinct]{#S6.p2.1.3 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}: "Use a single sentence to broadly describe the
appearance and shape of {cls}. Don't give too much details. Only
describe the shape and appearance." [More Detailed]{#S6.p2.1.4 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}: "Use a single sentence and short, simple, descriptive
phrases to describe the detailed appearance and detailed shape of
{cls}." [More Distinct]{#S6.p2.1.5 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}: "Use a
single sentence to describe the unique, distinctive appearance and shape
of {cls}. Only describe the unique, distinctive shape and appearance."
:::

::: {#S6.p3 .ltx_para}
We compare these prompts in Tab. [[7]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S6.T7 "Table 7 ‣ 6 Ablations ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
Interestingly, we observe that generating more detailed semantic
descriptions on labels does not always perform better. We conjecture
that this is because (1) LLM-generated details are not grounded in
specific images, causing some attributes to be invisible and confusing
the students; (2) the teacher CLIP is trained on
LAION \[[47](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib47){.ltx_ref}\],
where most language descriptions do not contain many finegrained
appearance details, so CLIP's text embeddings are not very sensitive to
some of these details. Additionally, we find that explicitly prompting
ChatGPT to generate more concise text descriptions could be still
helpful. Upon further analysis, we find that the resulting generations
remain highly descriptive, albeit with slightly fewer details (e.g.,
when describing a [trumbone]{#S6.p3.1.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter},
the more concise description becomes [a brass instrument with a long
cylindrical tube curved into an elongated S shape with a flared bell at
the end]{#S6.p3.1.2 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter}, whereas under our
original prompt, additional details like [a sliding U-shaped section
called the slide]{#S6.p3.1.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_typewriter} are
included).
:::

[Different designs on visual space and vision-language
alignments]{#S6.p4.2.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. We ablate on our
designs of visual space and vision-language alignments and present the
results in Tab. [[9]{.ltx_text

We first explore various designs of $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vlprox}}$, which
aims to improve the [vision-language]{#S6.p4.2.2 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_italic} alignment coherency with the teacher. We find that
enhancing coherency with a greater number of most-similar language
features by increasing $k$ improves student OOD generalization.
Additionally, it is also beneficial to filter out teacher's image
features that are misaligned with their corresponding language labels.
Interestingly, we observe a different pattern when considering the
teacher-student [visual space]{#S6.p4.2.3 .ltx_text .ltx_font_italic}
alignments. In this case, we did not find filtering out misaligned image
features helpful, and imitating the entirety of teacher's visual space
yields better student OOD generalization.
:::

::: {#S6.p5 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Different student visual backbones]{#S6.p5.3.1 .ltx_text
.ltx_font_bold}. In our prior experiments, we adopted ResNet as our
student visual backbone. In this section, we further investigate whether
our findings are consistent across different student network
architectures. We adopt
ViT-B/32 \[[15](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib15){.ltx_ref}\]
as our student, while keeping CLIP ViT-L/14 as our VLM teacher. Results
are shown in Tab. [[8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S6.T8 "Table 8 ‣ 6 Ablations ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We observe that even though we apply strong data augmentations
(RandAugment) when training the student ViT-B/32, it still suffers from
severe overfitting (the training accuracy on
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}$ is $> {90\%}$, which is significantly
higher than the accuracy on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$ in
Tab. [[8]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S6.T8 "Table 8 ‣ 6 Ablations ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}).
The in-distribution and OOD generalization performance of the ViT-B/32
student are also worse than the ResNet student. Nevertheless, we still
observe that better teacher-student visual space alignment, improved
coherence in vision-language alignment, and language representation
enrichment all enhance student's OOD generalization ability, aligning
with our previous findings.
:::

::: {#S6.p6 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Few-shot learning strategies on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{𝐨𝐨𝐝}}$]{#S6.p6.1.1
.ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. Previously, we finetune the student visual
networks during few shot learning on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$.
Alternatively, we could adopt a finetune-free retrieval-based strategy
like
\[[69](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib69){.ltx_ref}\],
which has been shown effective on large VLMs. We compare these two
strategies in Tab. [[10]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S6.T10 "Table 10 ‣ 6 Ablations ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
Further implementation details are presented in Appendix. Interestingly,
we observe that the retrieval-based strategy significantly underperforms
finetuning student visual backbones. We hypothesize that this phenomenon
arises because our student networks are trained on small to medium-scale
datasets, and have been exposed to far fewer concepts than their teacher
VLMs. Consequently, student's image feature structure and
vision-language alignment structure are less generalizable on OOD
concepts, making them less suitable for retrieval-based approaches.
:::

::: {#S6.p7 .ltx_para .ltx_noindent}
[Zero-shot OOD generalization ability after few-shot
learning]{#S6.p7.7.1 .ltx_text .ltx_font_bold}. In the real world, it is
essential for student networks to continuously adapt to new concepts,
and we aim to find a student learning strategy that accomplishes such
goal. Since we finetune student visual backbones during few-shot
learning on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$, we would like to know whether
finetuned student backbones overfit seen concepts and exhibit weaker
zero-shot generalization ability when encountering novel unseen concepts
again. We conduct an experiment on Flower102, where we split
$\mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}$ into two equal sets, and then split
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$ into $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood1}}$ and
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood2}}$ accordingly. We then select the best student
model and evaluate it on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood2}}$ both before and
after few-shot finetuning it on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood1}}$. Results are
presented in Tab. [[11]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S6.T11 "Table 11 ‣ 6 Ablations ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We observe that the student's zero-shot OOD generalization ability
slightly improves after few-shot learning, demonstrating that students
can continuously adapt to novel concepts.
:::
:::

## [7 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Application {#application .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_table">Table 12: </span>Results
on grasp feasibility prediction in the PickClutter task. In each entry,
<span
class="math inline"><em>x</em><sub>1</sub>/<em>x</em><sub>2</sub></span>
denote zero-shot and few-shot evaluation results, respectively. (<span
class="math inline">ℒ<sub>vlprox</sub></span> is not available for this
experiment since multiple objects exists in an observation)</figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S7.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we demonstrate that we can adopt our previous findings
for improving student's OOD generalization ability towards novel tasks
and domains. We augment the PickClutter task from a robot object
manipulation skill benchmark
ManiSkill2 \[[19](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib19){.ltx_ref}\]
with language, where given the name of a YCB
object \[[7](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib7){.ltx_ref}\],
a robot needs to detect whether it exists among a pile of objects given
the current visual observation captured from a hand camera, and if
exists, picks up this object. The task is illustrated in
Fig. [[5]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S7.F5 "Figure 5 ‣ 7 Application ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
We randomly sample different configurations of objects, and given
observations in each configuration, the student network outputs whether
it is feasible to grasp each YCB object. This setup resembles
observation-based affordance prediction in works such as
SayCan \[[1](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib1){.ltx_ref}\].
We select 26 visually-distinctive YCB objects and split them equally
into $\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}}$ and $\mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}$. We then
construct the datasets such that $\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}$ and
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$ only contain objects in
$\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}}$, while $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$ contains
objects in both $\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}}$ and
$\mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}$. We use 3000 scenes for training and 50
scenes for few shot learning. We adopt
EfficientNet \[[50](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib50){.ltx_ref}\]
as the student network and CLIP ViT-L-14 as the teacher. As we are in a
multi-label classification setting, our preliminary experiments show
that having positive and negative prompts like
\[[49](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib49){.ltx_ref}\]
and learning these prompts during student training can significantly
improve student performance, so we adopt these techniques in our
experiments. We calculate two metrics: (1) overall accuracy across all
YCB objects in the label set (i.e., $\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}}$ for
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$, and
$\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}} \cup \mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}$ for
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$); (2) F1-measure calculated over the objects
present in an observation, averaged over all observations. Further
details are presented in Appendix.
:::

<img src="./2307.03135_files/x6.png" id="S7.F5.g1"
class="ltx_graphics ltx_centering ltx_img_square" width="219"
height="200" alt="Refer to caption" />
<figcaption><span class="ltx_tag ltx_tag_figure"><span id="S7.F5.2.1.1"
class="ltx_text" style="font-size:90%;">Figure 5</span>: </span><span
id="S7.F5.3.2" class="ltx_text" style="font-size:90%;">Illustration of
the PickClutter task. Given the name of a target object, the robot agent
needs to decide whether the corresponding object is graspable based on
the current visual observation.</span></figcaption>
</figure>

::: {#S7.p2 .ltx_para}
We present the results in Tab. [[12]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S7.T12 "Table 12 ‣ 7 Application ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
Consistent with our previous findings, we find that improving
teacher-student visual space alignments through
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{im-cst}}$ significantly enhances the student's
generalization ability on OOD objects. Additionally, leveraging language
models to enrich the semantic details of object descriptions benefits
few-shot OOD generalization. Interestingly, for zero-shot OOD
generalization, while this enrichment improves the overall prediction
accuracy for novel objects, it adversely affects the recall (and thus
the F1-measure) on these objects. Further analysis reveals that students
tend to ignore objects unseen during training, resulting in lower
recall. However, with just a few examples of novel objects, students
achieve significantly better recall on these objects.
:::

::: {#S8 .section .ltx_section}
## [8 ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Conclusion {#conclusion .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S8.p1 .ltx_para}
In this work, we studied distillation of large teacher vision-language
models into lightweight student models by focusing on open-vocabulary
out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization for object classification using
small to medium-scale datasets. We investigated strengthening students'
OOD generalization ability from two key perspectives: first, by better
imitating teacher's visual representation space and carefully promoting
better teacher-student vision-language alignment coherence; and second,
by enhancing the teacher's language representations with informative and
meaningful semantic attributes to effectively differentiate between
different labels. We analyzed the efficacy and impact of our techniques
by introducing metrics and conducting a comprehensive experimental
analysis. Along this process, we significantly improve student's
zero-shot and few-shot generalization performance on open-vocabulary OOD
classification tasks.
:::
:::

::: {#S2a .section .ltx_section}
## [B ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}Training Details and Hyperparameters for Main Experiments {#b-training-details-and-hyperparameters-for-main-experiments .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S2a.p1 .ltx_para}
During agent training on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}$, for CaltechBirds,
Stanford Cars, and Flower102, due to their relatively small dataset
size, we train agents for 450 epochs to ensure convergence; for Food101,
SUN397, and tiered-ImageNet, we train agents for 90 epochs. For
ResNet-based students, we adopt an initial learning rate of 0.05 with
batch size 128, which is decreased to $0.005$ after $1/3$ of training
epochs and to $0.0005$ after $2/3$ of training epochs. We adopt standard
data augmentation (Random cropping image to 224x224, and random
horizontal flip). For ViT-based students, we adopt a one-cycle learning
rate schedule with a learning-rate peak of $0.0002$. We also apply
RandAugment \[[10](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib10){.ltx_ref}\],
a strong data augmentation method, to cope with overfitting. However,
even with RandAugment, we still observe that the performance on
$\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$ starts to decrease at some point in training,
suggesting that it is challenging to train ViT students on small to
medium-scale datasets to obtain good out-of-distribution
generalizability.
:::

::: {#S2a.p2 .ltx_para}
During few-shot learning on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$, we adopt a
balanced training batch, where at most half of samples come from
few-shot samples on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$ and the rest of samples
come from $\mathcal{X}_{\text{train}}$. For CaltechBirds, Stanford Cars,
and Flower102, we train agents for 100 epochs. For Food101, SUN397, and
tiered-ImageNet, we train agents for 20 epochs. We adopt a one-cycle
learning rate schedule for all student networks. For ResNet-based
students, the learning-rate peak is $0.003$. For ViT-based students, the
learning-rate peak is $0.0001$. We adopt the same data augment strategy
as before.
:::

::: {#S2a.p3 .ltx_para}
All experiment results are obtained from the average performance of the
last 5 epochs of training.
:::

## [E ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}More details on Comparing Different Few Shot Learning Strategies {#e-more-details-on-comparing-different-few-shot-learning-strategies .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S5a.p1 .ltx_para}
In this section, we provide more implementation details of our ablation
experiment in Sec. [[6]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S6 "6 Ablations ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
where we compare finetuning student visual backbone vs. training-free
retrieval for few shot learning on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$.
Finetuning student visual backbone follows the same settings in
Sec. [[B]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S2a "B Training Details and Hyperparameters for Main Experiments ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}.
For training-free retrieval, we adopt an approach similar to
Tip-Adapter \[[69](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib69){.ltx_ref}\],
except that we use the student image model $S$ as the visual encoder and
teacher's language model $T_{\text{img}}$ as the textual encoder. We set
$\alpha = 1.0$ and $\beta = 5.5$.
:::
:::

::: {#S6a .section .ltx_section}
## [F ]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_section}More details on Sec. [[7]{.ltx_text .ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S7 "7 Application ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref} Application {#f-more-details-on-sec.-7-application .ltx_title .ltx_title_section}

::: {#S6a.p1 .ltx_para}
For our experiments in Sec. [[7]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S7 "7 Application ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref},
we are in a multi-label classification setting, and the student network
needs to output whether it is feasible to grasp each YCB object given
the current observation. During student training, we find learning a
common positive prompt and a common negative prompt for all labels to be
very helpful, and we adopt
DualCoOp \[[49](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#bib.bib49){.ltx_ref}\]
to learn these prompts. To encourage the global student visual feature
be region-aware, we adopt the region aggregation method from DualCoOp
for all of our experiments. We additionally calibrate the positive and
negative probabilities (for each label, positive probability + negative
probability = 1) by learning a common probability bias on the fly. An
object $y$ is then predicted as positive if its positive probability is
greater than $0.5 + \text{bias}$.
:::

::: {#S6a.p2 .ltx_para}
The two evaluation metrics we calculated in Sec. [[7]{.ltx_text
.ltx_ref_tag}](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2307.03135#S7 "7 Application ‣ Distilling Large Vision-Language Model with Out-of-Distribution Generalizability"){.ltx_ref}
can be formally defined as follows:

-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S6.I1.i1}

    ::: {#S6.I1.i1.p1 .ltx_para}
    Overall accuracy over all YCB objects in the label set $\mathcal{Y}$
    ($\mathcal{Y} = \mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}}$ on
    $\mathcal{X}_{\text{id}}$ and
    $\mathcal{Y} = {\mathcal{Y}_{\text{id}} \cup \mathcal{Y}_{\text{ood}}}$
    on $\mathcal{X}_{\text{ood}}$):

      -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------
         $$\mathcal{M}_{1} = \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathcal{Y}|}{\sum_{j = 1}^{|\mathcal{X}|}{\text{correct}\operatorname{}{(x_{j},y_{i})}}}}{{|\mathcal{X}|}\operatorname{}{|\mathcal{Y}|}}$$      [(9)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equation .ltx_align_right}
      -- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- -- ----------------------------------------------------

    Here $\text{correct}\operatorname{}{(x_{j},y_{i})}$ outputs 1 if the
    existence of object $y_{i}$ is predicted correctly in the
    observation $x_{j}$, and 0 otherwise.
    :::
-   [[•]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_item}]{#S6.I1.i2}

    ::: {#S6.I1.i2.p1 .ltx_para}
    F1-measure over objects that exist in an observation, averaged over
    all observations:

      -- -------------------------------------------------------- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------------
         $\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{precision}}\operatorname{}{(y)}$   $= \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathcal{X}|}{{\mathbf{1}_{y \in {\text{obj}\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}}}*\text{correct}}\operatorname{}{(x_{i},y)}}}{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathcal{X}|}{\min{({\mathbf{1}_{y \in {\text{obj}\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}}} + {\text{pred}\operatorname{}{(x_{i},y)}}},1)}}}$      [(10)]{.ltx_tag .ltx_tag_equationgroup .ltx_align_right}
         $\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{precision}}$                       $= \frac{\sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}}{\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{precision}}\operatorname{}{(y)}}}{|\mathcal{Y}|}$                                                                                                                                                                                      
         $\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{recall}}\operatorname{}{(y)}$      $= \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathcal{X}|}{{\mathbf{1}_{y \in {\text{obj}\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}}}*\text{correct}}\operatorname{}{(x_{i},y)}}}{\sum_{i = 1}^{|\mathcal{X}|}\mathbf{1}_{y \in {\text{obj}\operatorname{}{(x_{i})}}}}$                                                              
         $\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{recall}}$                          $= \frac{\sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}}{\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{recall}}\operatorname{}{(y)}}}{|\mathcal{Y}|}$                                                                                                                                                                                         
         $\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{F1}}$                              $= \frac{2}{\frac{1}{\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{precision}}} + \frac{1}{\mathcal{M}_{2,\text{recall}}}}$                                                                                                                                                                                            
      -- -------------------------------------------------------- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ -- ----------------------------------------------------------

    Here $\text{pred}\operatorname{}{(x_{i},y)}$ equals 1 if the student
    network predicts that the object $y$ exists in observation $x_{i}$,
    and 0 otherwise.
    :::
:::
